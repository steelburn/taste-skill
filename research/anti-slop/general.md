# AI-Generated Web Slop: Research Report

*Synthesis of a 10-stream parallel research program on "AI slop" in AI-generated websites and frontend interfaces (v0, Lovable, Bolt, Cursor, and LLM-generated code generally). Streams covered: visual styling, structural/layout, behavioral/functional, content/copy, accessibility, implementation/engineering, imagery/iconography, public discourse & detection heuristics, UX primary-source violations, and performance/resilience. Evidence policy: peer-reviewed studies, standards bodies (W3C/WCAG, NN/g, web.dev), regulators (FTC), and measured datasets are preferred over vendor blogs; every pattern carries a confidence rating; claims no stream could verify are quarantined in §5 rather than asserted. Report date: 2026-07-18.*

---

## 1. Executive Summary

"AI slop" in web design is not a vibe; it is a measurable, lint-able defect class with a documented mechanism. Across ten research streams, the same conclusion recurs: the community objection is rarely "AI was used" but "defaults were shipped unreviewed" (Simon Willison's canonical framing: slop is content "mindlessly generated and thrust upon someone who didn't ask for it," May 2024). The most important findings:

**1. Sameness predates AI; AI industrialized it.** Goree, Doosti, Crandall & Su (CHI 2021) analyzed 227,802 screenshots of ~10,000 sites (2003–2019) and found average pairwise layout distance fell ~44% from 2010–2019 (color distance 32%), with framework adoption strongly predicting visual similarity. CRO culture A/B-tested its way to the same template before any LLM shipped a page. This is the critical calibration: the problem is not "AI patterns are bad" but "patterns are emitted unchosen."

**2. The mechanism is mode-averaging, and it is peer-reviewed.** LLMs return the statistical mode of 2019–2024 scraped Tailwind/shadcn tutorials. Alignment sharpens that mode: RLHF measurably reduces output diversity (Kirk et al., ICLR 2024); 70+ models converge on near-identical answers to open-ended queries ("Artificial Hivemind," Jiang et al., NeurIPS 2025, Infinity-Chat dataset); AI-assisted work is individually better but collectively more similar (Doshi & Hauser, Science Advances 2024). Deployed AI sites are then scraped into the next training corpus, narrowing the distribution further (model-collapse research: Shumailov et al., Nature 2024). A concrete traceable example: Tailwind creator Adam Wathan's semi-apologetic Aug 2025 post acknowledging he made every Tailwind UI demo button `bg-indigo-500` — one library default → millions of tutorials → training-data median → every model's first guess.

**3. The brand-swap test is the core diagnostic.** Swap the logo and brand name; if nothing breaks, the element was sampled from the training-data median rather than designed from content. A feature grid of three icon cards whose copy works unchanged for any company is slop; the same grid carrying brand-specific claims is convention. Every pattern in this report has a legitimate, earned form — the failure is always the unexamined default filled with content that would work for any brand, which is to say for none.

**4. Social proof is flipping into an anti-signal.** The trust components are now the most legally dangerous ones. The FTC's Consumer Reviews and Testimonials Rule (16 CFR Part 465, effective Oct 21, 2024) explicitly bans fabricated testimonials "including those generated by AI," with civil penalties up to $51,744 per violation (2024 schedule; $53,088 after 2025 inflation adjustment). Fabrication is industrialized: fakelogo.com sells 200 fictional logos via API for placeholder walls; avatar services (pravatar.cc, randomuser.me, thispersondoesnotexist) supply fake reviewer faces. Even *honest-looking* uniformity backfires: purchase likelihood peaks at ratings of 4.2–4.7 and declines approaching 5.0 (Spiegel Research Center); 46% of shoppers distrust perfect ratings (PowerReviews). The grayscale "Trusted by" row now reads as "low-investment template" — an anti-proof.

**5. Fake or dead UI is the most harmful family.** Buttons that do nothing, forms that route nowhere, hardcoded "live" dashboards, toast notifications claiming success for actions that never happened. WebGen-Bench (NeurIPS 2025): 647 interaction test cases against LLM-agent-generated sites — the best tool/model combo passed only 27.8%, with functional scores far below visual-design scores (Claude-3.5-Sonnet: 17.1% functional vs 52.0% design). "Superficial aspects are easier to implement than deeper internal functionalities" — the generated site is optimized to look complete, not to be complete. The decisive modifier is intent + honesty: the same artifact is a legitimate labeled experiment (fake-door test, demo data) and a hard failure in shipped production.

**6. Accessibility violations are the objectively checkable hard failures.** AI-generated UI is inaccessible by default: unprompted output produces keyboard traps, unlabeled controls, missing semantics, and contrast failures at rates comparable to or worse than human code (Aljedaani et al., W4A 2024; Suh et al., 2025; Abu Doush & Kassem, UAIS 2025; Gurita & Vatavu, W4A 2025). These are not taste judgments — they are per-instance WCAG violations detectable with axe/WAVE/lint rules. The one architectural counter-example is v0 (shadcn/ui on Radix = accessibility contracts shipped by the library), and practitioners call it "the exception, not the rule."

**7. Performance failures concentrate on weight and rendering architecture, not layout stability.** The one measured dataset of AI-builder sites (DevNova, n=93, Jun 2026): Lighthouse SEO averages 96/100 but median LCP is 6.6 s with 70% "poor" — driven by client-side-rendering of static content, monolithic JS bundles, and unoptimized hero media. Counter-calibration: 93% had *good* CLS. AI sites fail on bytes and architecture, not on layout shift.

**8. Single tells are weak; clusters are diagnostic.** Any one pattern false-positives (Krebs's deterministic Show HN scanner measured 5–10% false positives; cards and rounded corners predate AI; em dashes predate AI by two centuries; AI-detector heuristics misclassify non-native English writers at high rates — Stanford found 61.3% of human TOEFL essays flagged). The reliable signal is the *cluster*: shadcn defaults + Inter + indigo/purple + gradient text + icon-card grid + buzzword copy + dead footer links is one fingerprint, not seven coincidences.

**9. Second-order slop is already here.** Banning defaults just displaces them: after Anthropic banned Inter, models overused Jost/Cormorant; Opus-class models have their own fallback "house style" (cream + Fraunces + terracotta). The durable fix is deliberate per-project choices (tokens first, reference images, one hand-made element per page), not a bigger blacklist.

## 2. Taxonomy

### 2.1 Six categories

The 65 patterns in the ledger are grouped into six categories. The first four describe *what layer of the artifact* fails; the last two are cut on a different axis — *what kind of evidence* condemns them.

**Visual (V) — surface styling.** Color, type, surfaces, motion styling, imagery, and iconography as seen. Failures here are mostly distinctiveness failures: instantly recognizable defaults that erase brand identity and pattern-match to "AI-built" in under a second. Scope note: includes generated/stock *imagery* and icon systems; excludes layout structure (S) and contrast-as-WCAG-violation (A).

**Structural (S) — page architecture and layout.** Section skeletons, hero compositions, grids, navigation architecture, and page rhythm. The canonical failure is the monoculture skeleton: navbar → centered hero → logo cloud → 3-col features → stats → testimonials → pricing → FAQ → CTA → footer, emitted regardless of product. Scope note: a component's *existence and arrangement* is S; its visual skin is V; whether it works is B.

**Behavioral (B) — functional behavior.** Whether controls, forms, widgets, and flows actually do what they afford. This is the "hollow demo" family: plausible-looking UI with no working layer behind it. Scope note: the strongest cross-cutting modifier in the whole taxonomy applies here — *labeled prototype vs shipped production*. The same dead button is legitimate in a demo and a hard failure in production; classify the artifact in its shipping context, not in isolation.

**Content (C) — copy and claims.** Marketing-speak, fabricated social proof, invented metrics, placeholder/meta-text leftovers, hallucinated claims, and rhythm/punctuation tells. Scope note: content failures split cleanly into *fabrication* (hard failures with legal exposure: FTC rule, trademark law, tribunal precedent) and *regression-to-the-mean style* (avoid-by-default tells that erode distinctiveness without being false).

**Accessibility (A) — WCAG-level barriers.** Keyboard traps, unlabeled controls, missing semantics, contrast failures, motion without opt-out. **Why a separate category:** accessibility is the one category with a *normative external standard*. Whether purple gradients are tacky is taste; whether `text-gray-400` on white meets WCAG 2.2 SC 1.4.3 (4.5:1) is checkable by anyone with a contrast tool, and each failure is a per-instance violation of a published success criterion. That has three consequences: (1) classification here is evidence, not sentiment; (2) detection is automatable (axe, WAVE, Lighthouse, jsx-a11y); (3) the failures carry compliance and legal weight that taste categories do not. A pattern that is *also* a visual or behavioral pattern (gradient text, dead buttons) is cross-referenced, but its WCAG-violating form is registered here.

**Implementation/Engineering (I) — code-level and runtime signatures.** Console noise, hydration errors, scaffold debris (`Vite + React` titles), bundle/rendering architecture, unoptimized media, dead dependencies, insecure-by-default full-stack output. Scope note: these are detected from source, network traces, or runtime behavior rather than from the rendered surface — several are invisible in screenshots, which is exactly why they survive visual review.

### 2.2 Three-level classification scheme

Every pattern is assigned exactly one primary classification. Where a pattern has a materially worse variant, the escalation condition is stated in the entry.

**Hard failure** — the pattern is objectively broken, inaccessible, misleading, fabricated, or consistently harmful. Reserved for: dead or fake controls and fabricated functionality; fabricated testimonials, statistics, logos, or product claims; WCAG violations (contrast, keyboard, names/roles, reflow, target size, motion without opt-out); broken responsiveness; placeholder assets shipped in production; missing UI states causing silent failure; console/runtime errors in production; CLS-causing or LCP-breaking markup; insecure-by-default output. *Overuse alone is never a hard failure.*

**Avoid by default** — heavily overused, usually generic, sometimes appropriate. The failure is distinctiveness/information loss, not breakage: purple gradients, Inter, unmodified shadcn themes, hero+3-cards skeletons, bento-by-reflex, logo clouds, buzzword copy, emoji bullets, rounded-everything surfaces. These are the fingerprint components; each is defensible in a specific context, none should survive a brand-swap test.

**Contextual risk** — legitimate patterns with genuine, documented trade-offs; valid only when justified by the brief, the content, or measured product behavior. Carousels (user-controlled), hidden mobile nav, dark mode, animation density, icon-only buttons *with* accessible names, cards vs lists, gradient text with verified contrast, infinite scroll on serendipitous feeds, fake-door tests that are measured and disclosed. The entry states the conditions under which the pattern is earned.

The research streams converged on one cross-cutting rule, adopted here: **classify by condition of use, not by existence.** Jakob's Law and processing-fluency research mean familiar patterns reduce learning cost; Goree et al. themselves note conventions raise the usability floor. Patterns fail when (a) they carry fabricated content, (b) they remove user control or accessibility, (c) they substitute for product-specific content, or (d) they are simply the unexamined default. Only (a) and (b) are hard failures.

## 3. Pattern Ledger

65 patterns, deduplicated across the ten streams. IDs: **V**isual, **S**tructural, **B**ehavioral, **C**ontent, **A**ccessibility, **I**mplementation. Ordered within category roughly by importance/severity. Sources are limited to those actually retrieved in the research files.

### Visual (V)

### V-01 "AI Purple": indigo/violet gradient defaults
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** `bg-gradient-to-r from-purple-500 to-blue-500`, `from-indigo-500 to-purple-600`, `from-violet-600 to-indigo-600`, `bg-purple-600` CTAs; hexes #8B5CF6→#3B82F6, #6366F1, #667eea→#764ba2; purple glow shadows; hue band ~240–295° at high saturation reused on H1 + buttons + background.
- **Why it fails:** The strongest color tell in the Reddit complaint corpus (2.3% of on-topic comments; purple+gradient is the strongest co-occurring pair). Traceable mechanism: Tailwind UI's `bg-indigo-500` demo default saturated training data, and models emit it for any "innovative software" brief. Users pattern-match it to slop on sight ("the purple-to-blue gradient is the biggest tell lol" — 373↑ r/ClaudeAI). Anthropic's own frontend-design skill bans "cliched color schemes (particularly purple gradients on white backgrounds)."
- **Detection signals:** Grep `purple|violet|indigo|fuchsia` in class lists; gradient stops in hue 240–300°; >60% of accent tokens in that band; open scanners (unslop-ui `devibe_scan.py`, component-aesthetic-checker, slop-detect rule #2 "VibeCode Purple").
- **When it may be appropriate:** When purple IS the brand palette; internal tools and MVPs. Note: banning just displaces the default (models switch to overusing other tokens) — the fix is a deliberate palette, not a ban list.
- **Better alternatives:** Brand CSS variables; dominant color + one sharp accent; brand-tinted grays; explicit hexes in the prompt.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** vibecoded-design-tells — github.com/JCarterJohnson/vibecoded-design-tells — 2026; Alan West, "Why Every AI-Built Website Looks the Same (Blame Tailwind's Indigo-500)" — dev.to — 2026-03-25; Fountain Institute, "7 Tells that a UI is AI-Generated" — 2026-04; Anthropic frontend-design skill via Tessl / lobsterpack.com — 2026-04-18.

### V-02 Gradient hero text (`bg-clip-text text-transparent`)
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Contextual risk → **Hard failure** when contrast fails WCAG 1.4.3 anywhere along the gradient, when no forced-colors fallback exists, or when text can go invisible if `background-clip: text` is unsupported
- **What it looks like:** `linear-gradient(...) + -webkit-background-clip: text; color: transparent` / Tailwind `bg-gradient-to-r from-blue-600 to-purple-600 bg-clip-text text-transparent` on one H1 `<span>`; typically on the keyword of "Transform/Elevate your X" headlines.
- **Why it fails:** #3 pure-aesthetic tell (2.0% of complaints; verbatim in top-voted slop comments). Per-glyph contrast varies along the gradient and automated checkers evaluate solid backgrounds, so failures evade detection; in Windows High Contrast/forced-colors mode, backgrounds are stripped and `color: transparent` text can disappear entirely; without a solid fallback, unsupported engines render nothing (MDN issue #2638).
- **Detection signals:** Grep `bg-clip-text` + `text-transparent`; DevTools forced-colors emulation; contrast-check BOTH gradient endpoints; check for `@supports` guard or solid `color` fallback.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Large display type (relaxed 3:1 threshold), dark verified endpoints, single-word span, with `@media (forced-colors: active)` fallback; brand-owned identity gradients in the Stripe/Vercel tradition.
- **Better alternatives:** Solid headline + one accent element; highlight/marker styles; gradient on adjacent decoration, not inside text; typographic contrast (weight, scale, serif/sans pairing).
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** vibecoded-design-tells — 2026; cssshowcase.com gradient-text article — 2026; animationpatterns.art gradient-text-sweep + MDN content issue #2638 — 2026; Fountain Institute — 2026-04.

### V-03 Glassmorphism, aurora blobs & colored glows (the decorative blur/glow layer)
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Contextual risk → **Hard failure** when text-on-glass drops below 4.5:1 or heavy blur causes real GPU/scroll failure on low-end devices
- **What it looks like:** `background: rgba(255,255,255,.1); backdrop-filter: blur(10px); border: 1px solid rgba(255,255,255,.2); border-radius: 16px; box-shadow: 0 8px 32px rgba(0,0,0,.37)`; near-black base + 2–4 radial/conic blobs with `filter: blur(80px)` / `blur-3xl`; ≥24px-blur colored box-shadow glows.
- **Why it fails (evidence-based):** An OsloMet user study (18 participants, 3 prototypes) found high-transparency glass increased task times and readability complaints, with most participants preferring no glass; glass over imagery fails WCAG contrast and adds scroll-unstable readability and GPU cost (Axess Lab, New Target). Apple's iOS 26 Liquid Glass beta drew enough legibility criticism that Apple shipped a higher-opacity "Tinted" option (SitePoint). Real-world perf: thirdweb's dashboard issue #703 documents dropped frames from large-element `filter: blur`; shadcn/ui issue #327 documents `backdrop-blur` hurting machines without strong GPUs. **Calibration:** community complaint share is LOW (glassmorphism 0.2%; "mesh/blob/aurora" was rejected as a keyword artifact in the vibecoded dataset; Superdesign measured explicit aurora requests peaking at 0.33% of generations) — the meme exceeds the measured frequency; the real problems are contrast and performance, not sameness.
- **Detection signals:** `backdrop-filter` + alpha < ~0.6 carrying text; blur ≥ 60px on colored positioned elements; radial/conic gradient count > 3; axe over glass regions at multiple scroll positions; FPS on low-end devices; contrast sampling across animated areas.
- **When it may be appropriate:** OS-level materials, nav overlays, text-free decorative panels, semi-opaque scrims behind text; gaming/crypto/AI heroes with static or single-keyframe drift and ≤3–4 hues.
- **Better alternatives:** Solid surface + 1px border; alpha ≥ 0.85 panels; text on panel, never on raw drifting gradient; pre-blurred background images; ONE aurora source max.
- **Confidence:** High (mechanism and harm); Medium-High (AI-specific prevalence — reputation exceeds data)
- **Sources:** OsloMet thesis PDF — c.2025; Axess Lab — 2026-06; SitePoint, iOS 26 Liquid Glass — 2025-11-24; github.com/thirdweb-dev/dashboard/issues/703 — 2022-12 (plus shadcn-ui/ui#327, 2023-05); Superdesign dataset — 2026-06-14.

### V-04 Default sans stack: Inter / Roboto / Geist (+ the serif-italic accent word)
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** Google Fonts Inter link + `font-family:'Inter'` on every page; `font-sans`→Inter; Geist in Vercel-flavored output; Space Grotesk/Instrument Serif pairings; one italic-serif accent word in an otherwise sans hero ("Ship *faster*.").
- **Why it fails:** Distinctiveness failure, NOT usability — Inter is an excellent screen UI face, which is exactly why it dominates training data. On Anthropic's own blacklist ("overused font families (Inter, Roboto, Arial, system fonts)… NEVER converge on common choices"); the heaviest slop-detect rule (#1, weight 8); Krebs: "Inter used for everything, but especially the centered hero headlines… serif italic for one accent word"; 925Studios: Inter-default typography "is a strong signal the design was never intentionally styled."
- **Detection signals:** Font audit — Inter/Roboto/Open Sans/Lato/Montserrat/Geist as sole primary face = flag; single italic-serif inline accent span in the hero; unmapped `font-sans`.
- **When it may be appropriate:** App UI/admin where small-size legibility is the real requirement; Repaint warns that banning Inter means the model "can't handle situations where Inter is the right choice." Choose it deliberately.
- **Better alternatives:** Context-picked pair (Space Grotesk/IBM Plex Sans/Outfit for UI; serif display for editorial); name a font in the prompt ("use DM Sans, not Inter"); one decisive face per project.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Adrian Krebs, "Scoring Show HN submissions for AI design patterns" — adriankrebs.ch/blog/design-slop — 2026-04-20; Anthropic skill via Tessl / lobsterpack — 2026-04-18; slop-detect README — github.com/ravidsrk/slop-detect — 2026; 925Studios AI-slop guide — 2026-03.

### V-05 Dark-mode-by-default + neon-on-dark palette
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Contextual risk → Hard-failure variant: gray-on-near-black low contrast (see A-05)
- **What it looks like:** `bg-[#0a0a0a]`/zinc-950 body; #22d3ee-type neon accents; glow shadows; no light theme or toggle; all-caps section labels; the "Linear clone" look.
- **Why it fails:** NN/g's literature review (Budiu, 2020): light mode wins visual-acuity AND proofreading tasks across ages, the advantage grows as font shrinks, and even at night light mode won glanceable reading — so dark-by-default is a measurable readability regression for long-form, general-audience content. Recognizability: Fountain tell #1/#2; Reddit #6 (0.7%); HN: "It's insane how many dark mode websites with purple there are right now." Fits dev tools, feels "off-brand on a luxury goods site" (Metabole).
- **Detection signals:** Body luminance < 0.2 with no light variant/toggle; gray-on-black contrast audit; high-chroma accent count ≥ 5; `text-transform: uppercase` on section labels.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Dev tools, monitoring dashboards, media/gaming, night-first products; cataract/cloudy-media users genuinely do better dark (Legge 1985, via NN/g). NN/g's guidance: OFFER dark mode, don't DEFAULT to it for general audiences.
- **Better alternatives:** Light-first + `prefers-color-scheme` + toggle; if dark: #121212 grays (not pure black), one accent, ≥4.5:1, redesigned rather than inverted.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** NN/g, dark-mode literature review (Budiu) — nngroup.com — 2020-02; Fountain Institute — 2026-04; vibecoded-design-tells — 2026; Metabole Studio — 2026-04-21.

### V-06 Surface monotony: fully-rounded everything + uniform elevation
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** `rounded-full`/`9999px` on all buttons and chips; radius ≥ 24px on every card; identical radius at every hierarchy level; `shadow-xl/2xl` or `0 20px 50px rgba(0,0,0,.15)` on every card; glow shadows on buttons; shadow+border+glass simultaneously; cards inside cards ("everything goes in a card. Even cards themselves sometimes go in cards" — Fountain).
- **Why it fails:** No accessibility harm; pure convergence signal (Reddit #5 rounded corners, 0.8%; "homogenized, rounded-off version of web design"). Radius and elevation are brand levers; uniform max-rounding and uniform shadows mean "no decision made" — everything elevated = nothing elevated ("no cost function for visual weight"). Engineering cost: large-blur shadows are repaint-heavy; animating `box-shadow` triggers repaint (MDN: animate transform/opacity only).
- **Detection signals:** 1–2 distinct radius values, both large; `rounded-full` primary CTA on an otherwise generic page; blur ≥ 30px shadow count per page; nested shadow+border+radius; `box-shadow` inside transitions.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Playful/consumer brands; deliberate soft-geometry token systems; modals, popovers, floating nav, one hero product shot for elevation. Counter-voice in the data: "leave Bento grids and rounded corners alone, I love Bento grids" — taste, not defect.
- **Better alternatives:** Size-tied radius scale (4–8px controls, 12–16px cards); sharp/small-radius for editorial/fintech; border-first cards; flat surfaces with value separation; spacing/typography for grouping.
- **Confidence:** High (rounded); Medium-High (elevation)
- **Sources:** vibecoded-design-tells + quote bank — 2026; Fountain Institute #5 — 2026-04; MDN CSS performance — 2020 (mirror).

### V-07 Animation-everywhere: Framer fade-ins & animated gradients
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Contextual risk → **Hard failure** when flashing >3/sec (WCAG 2.3.1 seizure risk) or non-essential motion runs without a `prefers-reduced-motion` opt-out or pause control (see A-09)
- **What it looks like:** `background-size: 400% 400%; animation: gradient 15s ease infinite` on background-position; `motion.div initial={{opacity:0,y:20}}` on every block; identical `whileInView` fade-up on every section; animated gradient text.
- **Why it fails:** Reddit #4 "too many animations / Framer fade-ins" (1.1%/2.0%); background-position animation repaints every frame (MDN — worst on low-end devices); vestibular strain; every animated element competes with the relevant ones (Nielsen heuristic #8). Motion used as compensation for static content.
- **Detection signals:** Keyframes on background-position/size; absence of reduced-motion media query in the bundle; animated-element count per page; identical duration/delay on all sections.
- **When it may be appropriate:** One orchestrated entrance; subtle transform-based drift gated by `@media (prefers-reduced-motion: no-preference)`; loading/progress (essential, exempt).
- **Better alternatives:** Static gradient; transform/opacity-only motion; 150–500ms tokens; IntersectionObserver pausing; `motion-safe:` variants.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** vibecoded-design-tells — 2026; MDN CSS performance — 2020; dev.to animation guide — 2026-06-12; korrodesign rules — github.com/KorroAi/korrodesign — 2026.

### V-08 Unmodified default shadcn/Tailwind skin
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** `--foreground: 222.2 84% 4.9%`, `--primary: 222.2 47.4% 11.2%` matching published defaults exactly; `border bg-card text-card-foreground`; zinc-950/slate-900; `--radius: 0.5rem` untouched; `rounded-md` everywhere; stock Lucide icons; untinted `bg-gray-50`/`bg-white`.
- **Why it fails:** The #1 CONCRETE tell in the complaint data (2.5%, "confirmed, top concrete cause"). The kit is high-quality and accessible — shipping it unskinned signals no design decision was made. Mirage Security fingerprinted a real production platform as AI-built purely via its unmodified default shadcn Slate theme; r/cursor (43↑): "generic default shadcn themes are just as bad as the purple gradient AI slop." The ecosystem self-acknowledges: shadcn Create (late 2025) exists explicitly "instead of websites looking identical with default components."
- **Detection signals:** Diff `globals.css` HSL vars vs published shadcn defaults; theme-token diff; no custom tokens in `theme.extend`; stock component anatomy.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Internal tools, speed-over-brand MVPs, prototypes; as a base that IS then themed. The sin is skipping token customization, not using the library.
- **Better alternatives:** Themed tokens (ui.shadcn.com/themes, tweakcn, shadcnstudio); brand-tinted neutrals; decisive font; custom type scale.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** vibecoded-design-tells — 2026; Mirage Security, "Inside p1bot" — miragesecurity.ai — 2026-01-24; daily.dev "Best Theo posts" (shadcn Create coverage) — 2025-12; component-aesthetic-checker — claude-plugins.dev — 2025-11.

### V-09 Status dots & rainbow side tabs (color-only signals)
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Contextual risk → **Hard failure** when meaning is conveyed by color alone (WCAG 1.4.1)
- **What it looks like:** `h-2 w-2 rounded-full bg-green-500` spans before text; `border-l-4` rainbow accent per card; dots in 3+ colors per screen; blinking status dots as decoration.
- **Why it fails:** Fountain tells #6/#7: dots "meant 'live' or 'connected'… add them to every UI element in any color you like"; rainbow left borders "add color but do nothing for the taste." Anthropic's Claude Design prompt blacklists "containers using rounded corners with a left-border accent color." Unlabeled colored dots fail WCAG 1.4.1 (Use of Color) for colorblind users.
- **Detection signals:** Rounded-full dot-span count; `border-l` accents in >2 hues; axe color-only checks; dots without adjacent text labels.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Real live status WITH an adjacent text label; a single system accent.
- **Better alternatives:** Labeled badges/chips with icon + text.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High
- **Sources:** Fountain Institute #6/#7 — 2026-04; lobsterpack.com quoting Claude Design prompt — 2026-04-18.

### V-10 "AI-look" & decorative-filler imagery (3D blobs, Corporate Memphis, stock photos)
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** Glossy 3D abstract blobs/orbs floating behind the hero; plastic-smooth pastel renders "slightly too smooth, slightly too symmetrical"; faceless flat-vector people (unDraw/DrawKit/Corporate Memphis style) high-fiving or watering plants; "diverse team at a laptop in an impossibly well-lit office" stock photography.
- **Why it fails:** Recognizability — laypeople spot AI imagery "from the first glance" via saturated colors and inconsistencies (Utrecht MSc study); 925Studios and Vibe Code Kit list "abstract 3D blobs with a plastic quality" as top tells; Corporate Memphis is documented as "generic, overused" with zero differentiation since Facebook's 2017 "Alegria" (Linearity). Zero attention payoff — NN/g eye-tracking: purely decorative "feel-good" images are *completely ignored*; only real people/product photos are scrutinized ("jazzed-up = ignored").
- **Detection signals:** Reverse image search (stock catalog origin); the "remove test" — if deleting the image loses no information, it is filler; image-match against unDraw/DrawKit catalogs; abstract art bearing no relation to product content.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Prototypes; deliberately art-directed abstract brand systems; illustration packs customized to a brand palette inside a consistent system; genuinely relevant photography.
- **Better alternatives:** Real product screenshots; real team/product photography; brand-consistent custom illustration; or no image at all.
- **Confidence:** High (recognizability + NN/g attention evidence); Medium (harm magnitude)
- **Sources:** NN/g, "Photos as Web Content" — nngroup.com — 2010; 925studios.co/blog/ai-slop-web-design-guide — 2026-03; Linearity, corporate illustration history — linearity.io — 2022-02-08; Vitali MSc thesis, Utrecht University — 2025.

### V-11 AI-generated fake "product screenshots"
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Hard failure (misleading)
- **What it looks like:** Hero "screenshots" that are image-model generations of a dashboard — garbled text ("RESTRAUNT"-style misspellings), melted icons, impossible layouts, "Test Customer" data, balanced charts — inside fake browser chrome, presented as the actual product.
- **Why it fails:** A product screenshot's one job is accuracy. Diffusion models treat text as texture: Adobe documents Firefly "cannot spell or recognize legible languages yet"; FLUX/SD/Midjourney smear glyphs (dev.to). Hallucinated UI misrepresents the product — consumer-protection exposure (Rewarx); Apple App Store Guideline 2.3 rejects misleading screenshots, and Apple's marketing guidelines require screens show the app "as it appears when running." Prospects pattern-match fake data instantly.
- **Detection signals:** Zoom 200% and read every screenshot's text (OCR-diff against the real UI); verify shown features exist; AI-image metadata; UI inconsistent between screenshots on one page.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Clearly labeled concept/vision mockups; pre-product teasers that say so; internal stakeholder prototypes.
- **Better alternatives:** Real screenshots at native resolution with real (anonymized) data; an HTML/CSS rebuild of the actual UI for crispness; annotated single screenshot with numbered callouts.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** dev.to, "Why Every AI Image Generator Fails at Text" — 2026-04; allaboutai.com Firefly test incl. Adobe doc quote — 2026-03; developer.apple.com/app-store/marketing/guidelines — retrieved 2026; rewarx.com hallucinated-text guide — 2026-04.

### V-12 Iconography neglect (Lucide-everything, mixed icon styles, emoji-as-icons)
- **Category:** Visual
- **Classification:** Avoid by default → **Hard failure** when an icon (incl. emoji) is the only carrier of meaning with no accessible name
- **What it looks like:** `lucide-react` imports (Rocket/Zap/Shield/CheckCircle) in every feature card; 24px 2px-stroke icons in rounded `bg-primary/10` squares; "one huge rounded Lucide icon centered above a heading"; 3+ icon rendering styles on one page (stroke + fill + 3D + emoji); emoji in feature-card headers, pricing bullets, and `<button>🔍</button>` with no aria-label.
- **Why it fails:** Lucide is baked into `npx shadcn add` components — unmodified use signals "no human decided this" (PkgPulse; Vibe Code Kit). Mixed styles signal no art direction; inconsistencies are how users detect and distrust AI content (Utrecht). Emoji-as-icons is measurably worse: people disagreed on emoji sentiment 25% of the time even on identical renderings, worse cross-platform (Miller et al., ICWSM 2016); screen readers announce Unicode names ("red heart, button"); glyphs can't be styled into a design system.
- **Detection signals:** lucide-* dependency with zero customization; feature cards = icon-in-colored-rounded-square + H3 + gray paragraph ×3–6; regex `\p{Extended_Pictographic}` in headings/nav/buttons/list-markers; inventory of icon rendering styles per page (>2 families = flag).
- **When it may be appropriate:** Developer tools/dashboards where neutrality is right; any product that customizes stroke/size/color into a system; emoji in casual/conversational content with `role="img"` + `aria-label` (decorative: `aria-hidden`).
- **Better alternatives:** Choose the icon family per brand (Phosphor weights, custom set); one consistent SVG family; pair icons with real text labels; use icons only where they add scannability.
- **Confidence:** High (emoji evidence); Medium (Lucide monoculture harm)
- **Sources:** Miller et al., "'Blissfully happy' or 'ready to fight'" — grouplens.org — 2016; pkgpulse.com icon library comparison — 2026-03; vibecodekit.dev/ai-slop-design — 2026; ux-audit-fork 5 non-negotiables — GitHub — 2026-04.

### Structural (S)

### S-01 The Monoculture Skeleton (canonical section conveyor belt)
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** navbar → centered hero → logo cloud → 3-col features → stats band → testimonials → 3-tier pricing → FAQ → CTA banner → mega-footer; components literally named `Hero`, `LogoCloud`, `Features`, `Stats`, `Testimonials`, `Pricing`, `FAQ`, `CTA`, `Footer`; identical `max-w-7xl mx-auto px-4 sm:px-6 lg:px-8` wrapper on every section; alternating `bg-white`/`bg-gray-50` stripes; every section = eyebrow + centered h2 + subtitle + grid.
- **Why it fails:** The single most-replicated structure in template kits and training corpora — Tailwind Plus's marketing category codifies exactly this inventory (Heroes 12, Feature Sections 15, CTA 11, Pricing 12, Stats 8, Testimonials 8, Logo Clouds 6, FAQs 7, Footers 7). CoffeeBytes gave Bolt, Lovable, and v0 the same prompt and all three emitted the identical skeleton. Content gets forced into containers it does not fit (Müller 2018: "content-agnostic form is the opposite of design"); "cookie-cutter" is the #2 complaint (6.1%) in the Reddit corpus.
- **Detection signals:** DOM order matches the canonical list ≥80%; same container class and `py-16`/`py-24` padding on every section; strict background alternation; 1:1 mapping to a known block library; ≥8 of 11 canonical sections present regardless of site type.
- **When it may be appropriate:** MVPs, prototypes, internal tools where speed > differentiation; conventional B2B categories where buyers expect the map (Jakob's Law). The skeleton demonstrably reduces cognitive load for standard SaaS — keep the backbone, change the surface.
- **Better alternatives:** Derive section order from the buyer's decision questions; delete contentless sections ("Empty is better than fake" — slop-detect); add one product-specific section (live demo, real data); vary layout family per section.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Becker, "Same Same but Different" — saschb2b.com — 2026; Tailwind Plus components page — 2026; CoffeeBytes, Bolt vs Lovable vs v0 — coffeebytes.dev — 2026; UX Planet, "How To Spot AI-Generated Design" — 2026-05-19.

### S-02 Centered hero package: eyebrow pill + giant H1 + dual CTAs (+ false floor)
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** Contextual risk
- **What it looks like:** `rounded-full border px-3 py-1 text-xs` pill ("✨ Introducing X 2.0 →"); headline `text-5xl md:text-7xl font-bold tracking-tight` centered with one gradient-text word; vague subline; exactly two buttons (filled + outlined); optional tilted dashboard screenshot in faux browser chrome; `min-h-screen` with no scroll signpost.
- **Why it fails:** The composition is the fingerprint — Becker: "The dark hero with the radial glow. The centered headline in Inter. The two buttons, one filled, one outlined." Vague headlines fail the 5-second clarity test ("Build the future of work"-type headlines "say nothing about the actual product because AI generates them by averaging every headline it has seen" — 925Studios). The pill hijacks the first fixation for non-information. Ad-like treatment triggers banner blindness (NN/g: users ignore animation, colored backgrounds, and top-of-page placement; Benway & Lane: banner-style links found 58% of the time vs 94% for standard links). The `h-screen` variant creates a "false floor" — NN/g (57,453 fixations): content 100px above the fold is viewed ~102% more than 100px below; users scroll only when the top promises value.
- **Detection signals:** H1 ≥ 48px AND centered AND exactly 2 CTAs AND badge present; pill matching /new|beta|introducing|v\d/i with no href or `href="#"`; verbs from {transform, revolutionize, supercharge, elevate, unlock, seamless}; no concrete user, task, or number; next section starting below 100vh at common viewports; scroll-hint arrows present.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Genuine launch announcements (pill links to a real changelog); developer tools with one dominant CTA; specific headlines ("Financial infrastructure for the internet" — Stripe); earned bigness where typography is the deliverable.
- **Better alternatives:** Specific value-prop headline naming user + outcome; one primary CTA; real product screenshot or live demo as hero visual; asymmetric/split hero; hero at ~85–90vh so the next section peeks.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Becker — saschb2b.com — 2026; 925Studios — 2026-03; NN/g, "The Fold Manifesto" — nngroup.com — 2015; NN/g, "Banner Blindness Revisited" (Pernice) — 2018.

### S-03 Three-column icon feature-card grid
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** Avoid by default (icon-only, no-evidence variant)
- **What it looks like:** `grid grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-3 gap-6/8`; Lucide icon in `h-10 w-10 rounded-lg bg-indigo-500/10 text-indigo-500` chip; identical padding/radius/shadow; copy like "Lightning Fast — Blazing performance for your team"; exactly 3, 6, or 9 cards; zero `<img>`/screenshot in the whole features section.
- **Why it fails:** The model's favorite way to fill space without product knowledge; features are invented to fill the grid and describe category table-stakes, not differentiators. Top-voted critique (373↑): "Every section is just a bunch of cards with icons, NO images at all. This makes it very monotonous." NN/g: cards suit browsing heterogeneous items and lose to lists for search/scan/compare — a grid of marketing "features" is pseudo-content. Web Anatomy's 286-section corpus: 79% skip persona grouping, 72% are dead ends with no links, 56% weight every item equally. Anthropic's own cookbook bans the "three-boxes-with-icons cliché."
- **Detection signals:** Exactly 3n cards; icon-only visuals; 1–3-word generic titles; description lengths varying <50% (LLM length-matching); no metric/link/detail page per feature; `bg-primary/10` icon chips; `text-muted-foreground` bodies.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Genuinely parallel, discrete capabilities a browsing user scans; docs/plugin galleries; a real product with three honest pillars; ≤4 benefit-led cards where every card links to a feature page.
- **Better alternatives:** Screenshot/GIF per feature (Loom/Notion/Amplitude "actual workspace" visuals); vary card sizes; dense list with specifics; one deep feature story over six shallow cards; hero-feature + de-prioritized tail; persona grouping.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** vibecoded-design-tells — 2026; NN/g, "Cards: UI-Component Definition" — nngroup.com — 2016; Web Anatomy, 286 Features Sections — webanatomy.ai — 2026; 925Studios — 2026-03.

### S-04 Logo cloud ("Trusted by" band)
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** Contextual risk → **Hard failure** when logos are fabricated/unauthorized (see C-04) or the marquee auto-scrolls without pause (WCAG 2.2.2)
- **What it looks like:** `Trusted by 10,000+ companies` + 5–8 gray SVG logos at uniform ~24–32px height; `grayscale opacity-60 hover:grayscale-0`; `animate-marquee` infinite scroll; placed ≤1 section below the hero; no outbound links.
- **Why it fails:** Recognition is the entire mechanism — unknown logos transfer zero trust; one real person beats 15+ logos (Stacked Marketer). DoWhatWorks A/B-test archives: "customer logos lost in most tests" (Jotform, Navan, Dropbox pricing tests) due to logo blindness, logo fit, logo lies, and placement before the value proposition exists. Ubiquity flipped the signal: the row now reads "low-investment template" (AYDesign). Faking is industrialized — fakelogo.com sells 200 fictional logos via API, rating logo walls "Ease of faking 10/10."
- **Detection signals:** Logos with no link/case study; marquee without pause-on-hover/pause button and no `prefers-reduced-motion`; unsourced count claims; logos inconsistent with company age/stage; same logo set reused across unrelated sites.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Real, current, relevant customers placed *after* the value prop; logos linked to case studies (Clay) or segmented by industry (7shifts); enterprise vendor-confidence signals; honestly labeled "Integrates with" rows.
- **Better alternatives:** Static, named, linked customer stories with numbers; one deep case study; real press mentions with article links; relationship-segmented rows (customers / partners / integrations / press).
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** DoWhatWorks, "The Problem With Social Proof" — 2023; W3C, Understanding SC 2.2.2 — w3.org; fakelogo.com/social-proof-examples — 2026; AYDesign, SaaS Patterns 2026 — aydesign.ai — 2026.

### S-05 Bento grid
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** Contextual risk (slop reputation is over-memed)
- **What it looks like:** `grid-cols-4 grid-rows-2` with `col-span-2 row-span-2` cells; each cell a rounded card with mini-visual (sparkline, map, avatar stack); mixed-span "Apple keynote" walls as the default feature section.
- **Why it (can) fail:** The pattern's one rule is size = hierarchy; AI output assigns spans for visual variety while every tile holds the same icon+title+text formula, so heterogeneity is decorative and hierarchy is obscured (aardig.amsterdam: "minimizing content can cause relevant information to be lost or everything will scream for attention"). Sequential content loses ordering. **Calibration:** bento registered only 0.1% of complaints; one would-be tell was rejected as a keyword artifact; the unslop-ui scanner "deliberately ignores bento grids and glassmorphism. They get repeated as memes but barely register as real complaints"; Tailwind Plus ships only 3 bento components vs 15 feature sections.
- **Detection signals:** Grid with col/row spans where all tiles share one content template; largest tile's heading is not the primary value proposition; bento as the third different-looking section on an otherwise monoculture page.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Dashboards; genuinely mixed-media showcases with one clear hero tile; keynote-style feature tours; dense B2B products needing many compact proofs.
- **Better alternatives:** Homogeneous content → simple grid or list; one dominant feature → full-width section; hero-feature block plus supporting list; ensure logical reading order.
- **Confidence:** Medium (strong evidence of over-flagging; moderate evidence of real misuse)
- **Sources:** aardig.amsterdam — 2024; vibecoded-design-tells incl. scanner README — 2026; Metabole Studio — 2026-04-21; SaaSFrame bento guide — saasframe.io — 2026.

### S-06 Three-tier pricing with highlighted "Most Popular" middle tier
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** Contextual risk (research-backed conversion pattern) → **Hard failure** when the "Most Popular" badge is fabricated
- **What it looks like:** Exactly 3 cards (Free/Pro/Enterprise); middle `border-indigo-500 shadow-xl scale-105` + "Most Popular" badge; annual-default toggle with "-20%"; green checks/gray crosses; enterprise "Contact us."
- **Why it can fail:** The psychology is documented — center-stage effect, anchoring, decoy effect, default-option bias (Digital Applied 2026; Monolit 2026; adapty 2026) — and the same sources flag backfire conditions: the badged tier isn't actually popular; a real fourth segment gets buried; rows repeated across all tiers fake segmentation. AI generators invent tiers, prices, and the popularity badge wholesale — a fabricated claim about other customers' behavior, i.e., deceptive-advertising territory (FTC logic, see C-02). The annual-default toggle quoting per-month prices is drip-pricing's cousin.
- **Detection signals:** "Most Popular"/"Best Value" badge on a pre-launch product or with no sales data; superset phrasing ("Everything in X, plus…"); feature lists differing only in quantity caps; card count ≠ purchasable-plan count (highest-signal test); price without "billed annually" framing.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Real three-segment packaging; middle tier genuinely the volume seller; anchoring tier real and purchasable; honest dual cadences.
- **Better alternatives:** Price on the value metric; honest "best for" labels instead of fake popularity; single-plan or usage-based pricing when segmentation is fake; comparison table.
- **Confidence:** High (both legitimacy and misuse conditions)
- **Sources:** Digital Applied, "Pricing Page Psychology 2026" — 2026; Monolit — 2026; adapty — 2026; PainOnSocial pricing complaints — painonsocial.com — 2026.

### S-07 Stats band with big round numbers
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** Avoid by default when vague/unverifiable → **Hard failure** when fabricated (see C-03)
- **What it looks like:** Full-width tinted band with 3–4 huge numbers: `text-4xl md:text-5xl font-bold` — "10,000+ users · 99.9% uptime · 4.9/5 rating · $2M saved"; round numbers with "+" or "%"; no source/date/methodology.
- **Why it fails:** The skeleton expects stats, so the model invents them (Becker). Self-asserted numbers with no source are assertions, not evidence (NN/g "healthy skepticism"); implausible precision backfires ("99.9% uptime" is self-measured marketing theater ≈8.76h/yr at ping level); numbers impossible given the product's age ("99.9% uptime" on a week-old MVP) destroy the trust they simulate. Fabricated performance claims create deceptive-advertising exposure under the same FTC principles as fake reviews.
- **Detection signals:** Implausibly round numbers; the "99.9% / 10k+ / 4.9★" triple; stats unchanged between regenerations; no link to status page or methodology; hardcoded numbers on a waitlist product.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Real, current, sourced metrics — live status page for uptime; dated, methodology-linked figures ("2,847 marketing teams as of Q2 2026").
- **Better alternatives:** One precise honest metric with context ("'10,000+ customers' is vague. '2,847 marketing teams' is real" — Miracle Concepts); dynamically updated counts; drop the band if you have no numbers.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High
- **Sources:** Miracle Concepts — 2026; Becker — saschb2b.com — 2026; WebHostMost, "What 99.9% Actually Means" — 2026; fakelogo.com demo stat bar — 2026.

### S-08 FAQ accordion section (SEO-filler variant)
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** Avoid by default as a landing-page fixture
- **What it looks like:** Bottom-of-page accordion with 5–8 generic Q&As ("What is X? How does X work? Is there a free trial?"); shadcn `Accordion` with chevron; answers repeating hero copy; `FAQPage` JSON-LD; placed between pricing and footer.
- **Why it fails — three independent evidence lines:** (1) Content design — GDS: FAQs are "too slow" (questions can't be front-loaded), cause duplication, and are "tonally wrong"; Canada.ca: "almost always… FAQs are not effective"; NZ government: "FAQs — do not use them." (2) UX — NN/g: accordions "diminish content visibility and increase interaction cost"; users frequently never open panels. (3) The SEO incentive is dead — FAQ rich results were restricted to gov/health sites in Aug 2023 after mass schema abuse, then fully deprecated May 2026 (Search Engine Journal). Landing-page FAQs added "for SEO" now buy nothing.
- **Detection signals:** Questions containing the product-category keyword; answers duplicating above-the-fold copy; `FAQPage` JSON-LD on a marketing page; questions not traceable to real support data; ≥60% of questions on the generic ladder.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Genuine, frequently-asked questions (support centers, post-purchase logistics); long content-heavy pages needing progressive disclosure (NN/g: forms/wizards, mobile); a *dedicated* FAQ/help page — not a landing-page band.
- **Better alternatives:** Answer objections inline where they arise (near pricing, near signup); descriptive headings; searchable help center; correct `<details>`/ARIA semantics if truly needed.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** GDS blog — 2013; Canada.ca — 2020 + NZ dns.govt.nz — 2024; NN/g, "Accordions on Desktop" — nngroup.com; Search Engine Journal — 2026-05.

### S-09 Auto-rotating carousel (hero/feature/testimonial slider)
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** **Hard failure** when auto-rotating without user control; Contextual risk when manual
- **What it looks like:** `setInterval`-driven slide changes; dots + arrows; absolute-positioned slides with opacity/transform transitions; no pause button; no `prefers-reduced-motion`; 3–5 marketing messages competing for one slot.
- **Why it fails — the strongest evidence base in this report:** NN/g's Siemens study: a user failed to find a washing-machine deal displayed in 98-pt type in frame 1 because the panel auto-rotated ("It keeps flashing too quickly"); causes: banner blindness, loss of control, reduced per-item visibility, harm to motor-impaired/low-literacy/non-native readers. Erik Runyon's Notre Dame tracking (3.75M visits): ~1% of visitors clicked any feature; 84–89% of clicks on position 1. Peer-reviewed confirmation (First Monday): static images beat carousels decisively (40.53% vs 2.06% CTR). WCAG 2.2.2 requires pause/stop/hide for auto-moving content >5s. Even as usage declines (52%→28% of sites, Baymard data), AI keeps emitting it by default.
- **Detection signals:** Timed advancement in JS; `autoplay`/`loop` props on swiper/embla; dots <24px; multiple "primary" hero messages; no pause control; carousel as the only path to key content.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Manual, user-initiated carousels for true media sets (product photo galleries); mobile card rails with visible overflow hints.
- **Better alternatives:** One hero, one message, one CTA (NN/g recommends a static hero); static stacked sections (Baymard); rotate content over time via an editorial calendar; give each message its own section.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** NN/g, "Auto-Forwarding Carousels and Accordions Annoy Users and Reduce Visibility" — nngroup.com — 2013; Erik Runyon, "Carousel Interaction Stats" — erikrunyon.com — 2013; First Monday, carousel study — firstmonday.org; W3C, Understanding SC 2.2.2 — w3.org.

### S-10 Template rhythm: uniform containers, zigzag splits, alternating stripes, metronome CTA bands, dead-link mega-footer
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** Contextual risk (each element defensible; uniform mechanical repetition is the tell)
- **What it looks like:** One wrapper class `max-w-7xl mx-auto` repeated on every section; 3+ consecutive `md:flex-row`/`md:flex-row-reverse` 50/50 splits; backgrounds alternating white/gray-50 with period exactly 1; the identical full-width gradient CTA band ("Ready to get started?") re-emitted after every section; 4-column footer (Product/Company/Resources/Legal) with links to `#`/404.
- **Why it fails:** Mostly via aggregation — how the skeleton stays invisible-until-you-see-it. Zigzag with *decorative* images is measurably harmful: NN/g eyetracking found zigzag made decorative images harder to ignore ("images as obstacle course"), while aligned layouts let users skip them efficiently. Metronome CTA bands fail by banner blindness: a full-width gradient band with centered text is indistinguishable from a display ad (Benway & Lane: 58% vs 94% found), and the same CTA visible twice in one viewport reads as nagging (Metrifi). Dead footer furniture signals an unfinished product. Monotone pacing destroys information scent: users assume below-fold content repeats what's above and stop scrolling (Spool's "Iceberg Syndrome").
- **Detection signals:** Identical container class ≥5 times; ≥3 consecutive reversed splits; strict alternation; >3 visually identical CTA bands; same button label ≥4 times; footer links to `#`/404; every row ending in "Learn more →".
- **When it may be appropriate:** Alternating splits genuinely aid scanning of paired feature stories with *informational* images (Z-pattern evidence; Apple's product pages); consistent containers aid rhythm; fat footers are useful on content-heavy sites; CTA repetition at 2–3 real decision points is earned (GoodUI test: 7% → 12.8%).
- **Better alternatives:** Break the pattern deliberately (full-bleed section, vertical stack, different layout family every few sections); aligned image-text lists for decorative imagery (NN/g's direct recommendation); decision-point CTA repetition with escalating risk-reducers; ship only footer links that resolve.
- **Confidence:** Medium (Low for "fails" per se; High for "recognizable tell"); High for the zigzag-with-decorative-images and CTA-band variants
- **Sources:** NN/g, "Zigzag Image–Text Layouts" — nngroup.com; NN/g, "Banner Blindness" + Benway & Lane — nngroup.com; Metrifi, "Repeat Your CTA" — metrifi.com; Müller 2018 / Becker 2026.

### S-11 Navigation discoverability failures (hidden desktop nav + mystery-meat labels)
- **Category:** Structural
- **Classification:** **Hard failure** for desktop primary navigation hidden behind a hamburger or unlabeled icons; Contextual on mobile
- **What it looks like:** Desktop header = logo + hamburger only (`lg:hidden` on the entire nav at 1440px); nav destinations as bare lucide SVGs with no text/`aria-label`; clever one-word labels ("Solutions / Platform / Resources"); hover-only tooltips.
- **Why it fails:** NN/g's quantitative study (179 participants, 6 sites): desktop hidden menus were used in only 27% of cases vs 48–50% for visible/combo nav; content discoverability dropped >20%; task difficulty rose 21%; desktop users took 5–7s longer to reach nav; some users said the site "didn't have a menu." Mystery-meat navigation (Flanders, 1998) kills discoverability: NN/g found "universal" icons are rare (a clock icon for history got zero clicks — "obscure icon = wasted feature"); labels must be visible at all times. Violates recognition-over-recall (heuristic #6).
- **Detection signals:** No visible nav links ≥1024px; drawer-only navigation on desktop; `<a>`/`<button>` containing only SVG; hover-only tooltips (fail on touch); abstract-noun nav labels.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Mobile (learned convention — though combo nav still beats fully hidden: 86% vs 57% usage); secondary/utility nav; expert toolbars after research; genuinely standard icons (home, print, search).
- **Better alternatives:** Visible top nav; combo nav with labeled "More" overflow (the label alone raised interactions ~61%); icon + always-visible label; outcome-worded labels ("Pricing", "Docs").
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** NN/g, "Hamburger Menus and Hidden Navigation Hurt UX Metrics" — nngroup.com — 2016; NN/g, "Icon Usability" — 2014; IxDF, "Mystery Meat Navigation" (citing Flanders 1998) — interaction-design.org.

### Behavioral (B)

*Cross-cutting evidence for this category: WebGen-Bench (NeurIPS 2025, CUHK MMLab) ran 647 interaction test cases against LLM-agent-generated sites — the best tool/model combo (Bolt.diy + DeepSeek-R1) passed only 27.8%, and Functional Testing scored far below Design Validation (Claude-3.5-Sonnet: 17.1% functional vs 52.0% design). Practitioner audits agree: "The first 80% looks amazing. The last 20%… is either missing or broken" (Pravin Kumar, ~12 vibe-coded sites, 2026-04). The decisive modifier is intent + honesty: the same artifact is legitimate in a labeled prototype/experiment and a hard failure in shipped production.*

### B-01 Dead links & CTAs: `href="#"` placeholders and routes that 404
- **Category:** Behavioral
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** `<a href="#">Docs</a>`, `<a href="">Learn more</a>`, `<a href="javascript:void(0);">`; footers of social/legal links all `#`; anchors with no `href` (drop out of tab order); hero/nav CTAs pointing at routes that don't exist; SPA router missing the path.
- **Why it fails:** Affordance without destination. Dead clicks produce rage clicks, drop-offs, and lost trust (Mouseflow, 2025-03); anchors without `href` aren't keyboard-focusable or announced as links (Stanford UIT; Evinced); "Links lead to 404… Buttons don't work… visitors unconsciously categorize you as… potentially fraudulent" (Eclipse Digital, 2026-03). Structural cause: spec drift — the model invents plausible routes without creating pages. A dead primary CTA kills the most valuable click on the page and pattern-matches scam sites.
- **Detection signals:** Grep/AST for `href="#"`, `href=""`, `javascript:void(0)`; jsx-a11y `anchor-is-valid`; link crawler asserting 200s; route-coverage check (referenced ⊆ defined); CI click-test of every CTA.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Clearly-labeled prototypes only; measured, time-boxed "404 test" fake doors (see B-07).
- **Better alternatives:** Omit the link; `<button>` for actions; real routes for navigation; point the CTA at a real flow, even a one-field waitlist.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Mouseflow, dead-click harm — 2025-03; Stanford UIT, Links & Buttons — 2026-05; Eclipse Digital — 2026-03; MockHunter SKILL.md — 2026-05.

### B-02 Dead controls: NO-OP buttons, decorative search bars, placeholder modals
- **Category:** Behavioral
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** Buttons with no `onClick` or `onClick={() => {}}`; "Search box: NO-OP (zero network requests)" in a shipped Lovable dashboard (MockHunter); prominent `<input type="search">` with no form/onSubmit where Enter does nothing; "Settings"/"Edit" modals full of lorem ipsum, dummy fields ("John Doe", "user@example.com"), unwired toggles that don't persist.
- **Why it fails:** The core fake-UI unit. Dead clicks → rage clicks → abandonment (Mouseflow); WebGen-Bench functional pass rates of 4.7–29.1% vs design scores up to 56% show the gap is systemic. A dead search bar wastes the user's highest-intent action and is often an a11y double-failure (unlabeled inputs are among the most common WCAG 4.1.2/3.3.2 violations). Models render "one static screen" with common filler and no state layer (unslop-preflight): "the UI may look complete but the data layer often is not."
- **Detection signals:** Playwright click-test asserting DOM/network change per control; static handler analysis; type + Enter in search → assert URL/network/list mutation; open every modal → check state binding + real Save mutation; grep filler literals ("Lorem", "John Doe", "Item 1/2/3").
- **When it may be appropriate:** Disabled-with-reason controls; labeled prototypes.
- **Better alternatives:** Wire it or remove it; loading/disabled states; client-side filter over a rendered list; fewer real settings; honest "nothing to configure yet" state.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** WebGen-Bench — arXiv:2505.03733 — 2025; MockHunter — 2026-05; Techpoint Africa — 2025-08; unslop-preflight ai-failure-patterns.md — 2026.

### B-03 Forms with no backend / fake submit
- **Category:** Behavioral
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** `<form>` with no action; `onSubmit={e => e.preventDefault()}` then nothing (or straight to a success toast); inputs without `name`; no POST on submit; contact/signup/newsletter forms that render fully but route data nowhere.
- **Why it fails:** Silently destroys user data and trust — tops Pravin Kumar's audit ("Forms that don't connect to anything"); "A contact form that accepts the submission but never routes the data… reads as: this platform cannot be trusted" (Frugal Testing). A hands-on v0 test: "the signup form returned no feedback after submission" — the reviewer had to read a raw console auth error. Violates Nielsen heuristics #1 (system status), #5 (error prevention), #9 (recovery).
- **Detection signals:** Submit test data → assert network + persistence; empty-submit validation test; verify the success path isn't unconditional; CRUCiBLE's scanner explicitly checks "Dead forms (no action / no submit handler)."
- **When it may be appropriate:** Static sites posting to real third-party endpoints are fine — the form must route somewhere real.
- **Better alternatives:** Real endpoint (API route, Supabase, Formspree); `mailto:` link; labeled waitlist that actually stores emails; visible email address if no backend exists.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Pravin Kumar — pravinkumar.co — 2026-04; Frugal Testing — 2026; HostAdvice hands-on v0/Bolt/Lovable test — 2026-07; CRUCiBLE — 2026.

### B-04 Toast theater / fake success feedback
- **Category:** Behavioral
- **Classification:** Hard failure when the toast claims success for an action that didn't occur; `alert()/confirm()` as product UI is Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** `onClick={() => toast.success("Saved!")}` with no await; `alert("This feature is coming soon!")`; `setTimeout(() => setSuccess(true), 500)` with no request; canned success toasts regardless of outcome.
- **Why it fails:** Documented as "Gaslighting Mocks — they claim success but do nothing real" (robotics-mcp). A production audit found "HTTP 403 errors… treated as successful scrapes… errors were swallowed, garbage data flowed downstream, and the system reported success" (fromtheprism, 2026-02). Fake success is worse than honest failure because it destroys the user's ability to detect that anything is wrong.
- **Detection signals:** Grep `alert(/confirm(/prompt(`; trace toasts to a resolved real mutation; click-test asserting the state change behind the toast; grep catch blocks that only `console.log`.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Optimistic UI with a real request + rollback; honest labeled "coming soon" (B-07).
- **Better alternatives:** Toast on real resolution; error toasts with recovery copy; in-app dialogs.
- **Confidence:** High (fake success); Medium (alert() as uniquely AI-slop)
- **Sources:** robotics-mcp DEEP_ANALYSIS.md — 2026-02; fromtheprism.com — 2026-02; unslop-preflight — 2026.

### B-05 Fabricated functionality: hardcoded "live" dashboards & fake chat widgets
- **Category:** Behavioral
- **Classification:** Hard failure when shipped to users; Contextual for labeled demos
- **What it looks like:** Dashboard metrics/charts/badges/activity feeds as string literals or `Math.random()` output presented as live data ("$42,850", "+12% vs last month" as JSX literals; zero `/api|supabase|axios` calls in the bundle); Intercom-style chat bubbles whose backend is absent — identical canned reply to every message, typing indicator on a timer, model-selector UI with no model calls.
- **Why it fails:** Fabricated data presented as real — dangerous in decision contexts (a public-health tracker case documented fabricated numbers). MockHunter's field example: a Lovable admin dashboard scored 23 HARDCODED, 1 BROKEN, 1 NO-OP, 0 REAL. One benchmark model shipped `render json: { response: "This is a mock response…" }` as its entire "integration" (akitaonrails). Fake "AI assistants" are deceptive squared — users may act on fabricated answers. Wizard-of-Oz is the legitimate version and requires humans actually answering.
- **Detection signals:** MockHunter decision tree (value in no network response but present in DOM/JSX → HARDCODED); uniformity heuristics (all % round, timestamps clustered, <3 unique values across 10+ rows); send a chat message → check network + response variance; grep hardcoded reply arrays.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Labeled prototypes/demos; disclosed Wizard-of-Oz pilots; a bubble that honestly opens a contact form.
- **Better alternatives:** Honest zero-data empty states; real queries; visible "Sample data" labels; a real (even small) LLM endpoint; link to real support.
- **Confidence:** High (hardcoded dashboards); Medium-High (fake chat prevalence — one benchmark example + one parody + category-level data)
- **Sources:** MockHunter — 2026-05; akitaonrails llm-coding-benchmark — 2026-04; JS Labs public-health tracker case — 2026-05; Open3DLab "LLEmmy" parody — 2026-04.

### B-06 Non-functional composite controls (tabs, carousels, accordions, toggles)
- **Category:** Behavioral
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** Tab bars that never switch panels; carousel arrows that don't scroll; accordions that don't expand; dark-mode toggles with no bound state variable; clicking never changes `aria-selected`/panel/scroll offset/root class.
- **Why it fails:** "Lovable delivered a clean admin dashboard with detailed tabs, but some buttons didn't work" (Techpoint, 2025-08); "Missed functional details (…tab state management)" in v0 output (EPAM, 2026-04). These are exactly WebGen-Bench's worst-scoring functional tests — composite widgets are where the "looks complete vs works" gap is widest.
- **Detection signals:** Click each control → assert ARIA/panel/scroll/class actually changes; verify the handler calls a setter the panel consumes.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Fine when all states are wired; a tab bar with one real section is worse than no tabs.
- **Better alternatives:** Render only existing views; Radix/shadcn primitives with real state binding.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Techpoint Africa — 2025-08; EPAM — 2026-04; WebGen-Bench — 2025.

### B-07 "Coming soon" / fake-door buttons (the contextual carve-out)
- **Category:** Behavioral
- **Classification:** Contextual risk
- **What it looks like:** UI for unbuilt features used to measure demand: "Coming Soon" + "Notify Me" capture; click instrumentation behind a feature card.
- **Why it can be right:** Fake-door testing is a legitimate named method — "add the UX elements… but rather than actually delivering the feature, provide a notification that the feature's coming soon. See how many people use your 'fake door'" (The Product Book, 2nd ed.); "painted door / 404 test / landing page test" (Learning Loop, 2019).
- **Why it becomes slop:** AI generators produce the same artifact unintentionally, without measurement or disclosure — the difference between an experiment and a lie is intent + honesty. Slop when accidental, unlabeled, or permanent.
- **Detection signals:** Is the click instrumented? Does it disclose unavailability? Has it persisted for months? (No/No/Yes → slop.)
- **When it may be appropriate:** Intentional, measured, honest-on-click, time-boxed demand tests.
- **Better alternatives:** Build the feature or remove the affordance; honest waitlist copy.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** The Product Book, 2nd ed.; Learning Loop — 2019-01; lyndonkl/claude pretotyping template — 2025-10.

### B-08 Missing UI states: loading / empty / error (happy-path-only UI)
- **Category:** Behavioral
- **Classification:** Hard failure in production; Contextual in prototypes
- **What it looks like:** `data.map(...)` with no `isLoading`/`isError` branches; no skeletons on async surfaces; empty tables rendering blank (or mocking chrome — sort controls over zero rows); catch blocks that `console.log` and continue; errors "logged only to the console."
- **Why it fails:** NN/g (primary): "Do not default to totally empty states. This approach creates confusion for users, who may be left wondering if the system is still loading information or if errors have occurred." Missing edge-case/empty handling is "the most common and most dangerous AI code flaw… AI trains on happy-path examples" (techdebt.works) — "the other states never appear in a single training example" (unslop-preflight). Real failures present as nothing-happened or false success (see B-04). Maps to heuristics #1/#5/#9 and WCAG 2.4.7's keyboard counterpart.
- **Detection signals:** Cold-start (zero-data) test; kill the API and click through; grep empty catch blocks; JSX `.map(` without zero-length conditional; no Suspense/skeleton/ErrorBoundary; Slow-3G throttle shows blank viewport.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Static content with no data dependency legitimately has no loading state.
- **Better alternatives:** Layout-preserving skeletons; distinct designed empty states per cause (first-use vs no-results vs error), each with one CTA; Error Boundaries + retry; error copy with next steps.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** NN/g, empty-state design (Kaplan) — nngroup.com — 2021-09-19; techdebt.works — 2026-02; fromtheprism.com — 2026-02; Carbon Design System empty-states pattern — carbondesignsystem.com.

### B-09 Infinite scroll without pagination/URL fallback
- **Category:** Behavioral
- **Classification:** Contextual risk (avoid by default for goal-oriented content)
- **What it looks like:** `IntersectionObserver` appending to a single route; no `<a href="?page=2">`; no History API updates; scrollbar lying about length; unreachable footer; back button loses position.
- **Why it fails:** NN/g (Loranger, 2014): wrong for goal-oriented finding — inefficient relocation, scroll-bar deception, "information abyss," "can cause inaction (and lower conversions)." Accessibility failures: focus lost on back-navigation, keyboard/screen-reader users forced to re-navigate everything, footer inaccessible (Sharma & Murano). Deep content undiscoverable without paginated URLs; DOM accumulation inflates memory and degrades mobile INP.
- **Detection signals:** Paginated XHR while the URL never changes; no page links in the DOM; JS disabled → page 2 unreachable.
- **When it may be appropriate:** True serendipitous activity feeds; even Pinterest pairs infinite scroll with unique URLs + fallback pagination.
- **Better alternatives:** "Load more" button; classic pagination with canonicals; or infinite scroll + History API + pagination fallback + persistent footer.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** NN/g, "Infinite Scrolling" (Loranger) — nngroup.com — 2014; Sharma & Murano, IJCA usability review — pietromurano.org; Dyno Mapper, pagination vs infinite scroll — 2026-04.

### Content (C)

### C-01 Buzzword salad / generic marketing-speak
- **Category:** Content
- **Classification:** Avoid by default → shades into Hard failure when puffery becomes unsubstantiated superlatives ("#1", "revolutionary results")
- **What it looks like:** Headline formula `[Imperative verb] + your/the + [abstract noun]` ("Supercharge your productivity", "Elevate your workflow", "Unlock the power of"); "in today's fast-paced world" openers; adjective stacks ("powerful, intuitive, and seamless"); vocab clusters: delve, leverage, seamless, elevate, unlock, empower, robust, streamline, cutting-edge, game-changer, landscape, testament, tapestry, pivotal.
- **Why it fails:** LLMs regress to the mean, replacing specific facts with "generic, positive descriptions" (Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing). Corpus studies document the vocabulary explosion post-ChatGPT: delve +1,500%, underscore +1,000%, intricate +700% in academic papers 2022→2024 (Thelwall; Kobak/Geng-Trotta line). The harm predates AI: Nielsen & Morkes found "marketese" imposed a measurable cognitive burden; concise/scannable/objective rewrites improved measured usability 47–58% (NN/g 1997–98). The copy could be pasted onto any competitor's site unchanged.
- **Detection signals:** Banned-word linter with density threshold (≥3 flagged terms/500 words); the swap test ("could this headline sit on a competitor's site?"); vagueness ratio (adjectives per concrete noun/number); "In today's [adjective] world/landscape" openers.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Legitimate category conventions ("single sign-on", "uptime"); short ad copy where genre brevity wins; an established brand tagline predating the cliché cycle.
- **Better alternatives:** Concrete, quantified, product-specific claims ("Import 10,000 rows in 4 seconds"); voice-of-customer language mined from real reviews; NN/g's concise/scannable/objective guidelines.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing — en.wikipedia.org — retrieved 2026-07; Thelwall, arXiv:2509.09596 — 2025-09; NN/g, "How Users Read on the Web" / "Applying Writing Guidelines" — nngroup.com — 1997/1998.

### C-02 Fabricated testimonials & reviewer identities
- **Category:** Content
- **Classification:** Hard failure (illegal in the US; the trust-category-defining slop pattern)
- **What it looks like:** 2–3 quote cards with 5 gold stars, circular avatar, "Sarah K., Product Manager"; specificity-free quotes ("game-changer", "This tool changed how we work!"); headshots from i.pravatar.cc, randomuser.me, ui-avatars.com, thispersondoesnotexist/StyleGAN; companies with zero LinkedIn footprint; quotes of suspiciously uniform length; generated for products with zero customers. Real-world instance: a production hosting site shipped an AI testimonial signed **"I Am Batman, Manager"** (PBN.LTD review, 2026-05).
- **Why it fails:** **Legal** — FTC 16 CFR Part 465 (announced Aug 14 2024, effective Oct 21 2024) prohibits reviews/testimonials "from someone who does not exist," explicitly including AI-generated ones; civil penalties up to $51,744/violation (2024), $53,088 (2025-adjusted); enforcement began 2025. **Documented scandal** — Sports Illustrated published reviews under fabricated authors "Drew Ortiz" and "Sora Tanaka" with AI-headshot photos (Futurism, Nov 2023); the same contractor supplied Gannett/Reviewed and McClatchy. **Counter-productive** — purchase likelihood peaks at 4.2–4.7 stars and declines approaching 5.0 (Spiegel Research Center); 46% of shoppers distrust perfect ratings (PowerReviews); generic testimonials lose A/B tests (DoWhatWorks); fake-looking testimonials are "worse than no testimonial at all because they signal dishonesty" (PagePulse). Over-polished synthetic headshots sit one visual cue away from documented scam-site signatures (MalwareTips).
- **Detection signals:** Reverse image search on avatars; search the quoted sentence in quotes (appears on dozens of sites); check whether named people/companies exist on LinkedIn; uniform quote-length stdev ≈ 0; all-5-star with no platform link; testimonials on a pre-launch product.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never for real claims. Fictional quotes only if clearly labeled illustrative (rare). Real, permissioned, specific testimonials with full name/role/company are fine and effective.
- **Better alternatives:** 3–5 specific, attributed, verifiable testimonials; link to third-party platforms (G2, Trustpilot); case studies with measurable outcomes ("we were spending $1500–2500/mo… eliminated after the first quarter"); or omit the section — "three authentic testimonials beat ten fake ones."
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** FTC press release — ftc.gov — 2024-08-14; Sidley Data Matters — datamatters.sidley.com — 2024-08-30; Futurism via Daring Fireball / Press Gazette scandal tracker — 2023-11; Foundry CRO, Proof Benchmarks 2026 (Spiegel/PowerReviews synthesis) — foundrycro.com — 2026.

### C-03 Invented statistics & vanity metrics
- **Category:** Content
- **Classification:** Hard failure when fabricated; Avoid by default for vague-unverifiable claims
- **What it looks like:** "99.99% uptime", "Trusted by 10,000+ teams", "4.9/5 rating", "Boosts productivity by 37%" — no source, no methodology, no linked evidence; "Studies show…" / "Industry reports indicate…" weasel attributions; stat ribbons where no counter has a source link; ratings with no platform attribution.
- **Why it fails:** LLMs fabricate plausible numbers by default — the site-builder ecosystem now ships explicit "do not invent statistics" guardrails (anoda.mobi; skills.ws Landing Page Builder skill bans invented "10,000+ teams" claims — an admission fabrication is the default). Icons8 rebuilt its generator after observing Claude-generated sites where "the copy is completely made up." Real-world correction cascades: CNET published 77 AI-written finance articles; over half (41+) required corrections (CNN/Futurism). In high-stakes domains, fabrication is systemic: >1,000 court decisions logged involving AI-fabricated material (Charlotin database, via Nordia Law). Deceptive-advertising exposure: FTC Act §5; Consumer Review Rule if framed as ratings.
- **Detection signals:** Every stat without a hyperlinked source gets flagged; cross-check domain age (WHOIS) vs claimed customer counts; search the exact phrase + number (duplicated across sites = template); impossible precision (99.99% = 52 min downtime/yr — does the startup even have a status page?).
- **When it may be appropriate:** Real, measured, dated, sourced numbers ("4,847 customers" beats "thousands"); aspirational copy clearly framed as goals.
- **Better alternatives:** Publish methodology (status page, case study PDF); link third-party ratings; use verifiable qualitative claims when metrics don't exist yet.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Icons8 blog, "We built a website generator that can't hallucinate" — icons8.com — 2026-06; AgentPostmortem APM-0052 citing CNN (CNET) — agentpostmortem.com; Nordia Law summarizing the Charlotin hallucination database — nordialaw.com — 2026-07; Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing, "Vague attributions" — 2026.

### C-04 Fake / unauthorized customer logo clouds
- **Category:** Content
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** 4–6 logo strip (Google, Meta, Netflix, Amazon…) directly under the hero on a weeks-old startup; styled-text pseudo-logos ("TechCorp", "ScaleUp", "DataFlow"); monochrome SVGs from public brand-asset packs; no linked case study for any logo; the same logo set on unrelated generated sites; courseware literally instructing 'Social proof bar: "Trusted by 500+ startups" with fake logos' (claude-code-course).
- **Why it fails:** Trademark infringement + false endorsement + deceptive advertising; nominative fair use is narrow and doesn't cover implying partnership (Aaron Hall; Chase Law Group). Trivially falsifiable by a single email to the "customer." Regulators pursue misleading trust labels (Singapore CCCS vs Agoda's "trusted and verified" badge, May 2026). "If you only have 5 customers, don't fake it with 50 logo ghosts. Specificity builds trust. Vagueness destroys it" (tonik).
- **Detection signals:** Logo strip on sites <6 months old with no press/case-study trail; famous logos + zero customers on third-party review sites; logo names failing WHOIS/search checks; generator-service fictional sets.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Real customers with written permission; logos of tools genuinely integrated ("Works with Slack") under brand guidelines; press logos for actual coverage with links.
- **Better alternatives:** Named customer quotes with permission; link real case studies; "Backed by [real VC]" when true; otherwise drop the strip.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Aaron Hall, "Problems With Using Logos on Client Testimonial Pages" — aaronhall.com — 2025-08; tonik conversion-mistakes guide — tonik.com — 2026; github.com/amanaiproduct/claude-code-course — 2026; marketing-interactive.com CCCS/Agoda — 2026-05.

### C-05 Lorem ipsum & placeholder leftovers in production
- **Category:** Content
- **Classification:** Hard failure (broken deliverable; reputational + SEO harm)
- **What it looks like:** "Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet" in footers/about/cards; "John Doe"/"Jane Doe"/"Acme Corp" names; bracketed merge-fields (`[COMPANY NAME]`, "Insert tagline here"); default form labels; "Your text here."
- **Why it fails:** Audits of vibe-coded sites find "generic placeholder content" among the most consistent defects (Pravin Kumar, ~12 sites); the vibe-check detector lists it as a core vibe-coded-site signature. Search engines treat dummy text as low-quality content; launch checklists for AI builders explicitly include "Replace placeholder text with real copy" as a required step. Instant credibility destruction — proof the output was never reviewed end-to-end.
- **Detection signals:** Trivially lintable: grep `lorem ipsum`, `dolor sit amet`, `John Doe`, `Jane Doe`, `Acme`, `example.com`, `[A-Z]+ HERE` bracket patterns in rendered DOM; CI gate `grep -r "Lorem ipsum" ./src && exit 1`.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never in production; fine in private prototypes/wireframes.
- **Better alternatives:** Content-first generation (build copy only from real material); pre-launch checklist gate; CI placeholder lint.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** pravinkumar.co vibe-coding audit — 2026-04; GitHub ashish-jabble/vibe-check — 2026-03; kleap.co lorem-ipsum FAQ + FindBeam — 2026; thejord.it CI guard — 2025-12.

### C-06 LLM meta-text leftovers ("As an AI language model…")
- **Category:** Content
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** Assistant politeness wrappers in body text ("Certainly! Here's a…", "As an AI language model", "I hope this helps"); markdown artifacts (`**bold**` literals) in rendered pages; "Would you like me to…" closers; pasted user instructions ("make it snappier").
- **Why it fails:** Direct proof of unreviewed copy-paste; destroys credibility instantly. Documented newsroom cases: Pakistan's *Dawn* printed a raw ChatGPT exchange including the "make it snappier" instruction (Nov 2025); the Chicago Sun-Times/Philadelphia Inquirer "summer reading list" recommended nonexistent books via a syndicated AI insert (May 2025); an NZ MP accidentally forwarded ChatGPT prompts to a political opponent (Dec 2025).
- **Detection signals:** Regex for `as an ai`, `certainly!`, `i hope this`, `would you like`, `here'?s (a|the)`, literal `**`; unmatched markdown in HTML output.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never (outside content genuinely about AI, quoted deliberately).
- **Better alternatives:** Human review gate before publish; strip-assistant-artifacts lint in CI.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** AiReadyCareer, Dawn ChatGPT prompt case — aireadycareer.com — 2025-11; Fox 10/AP, nonexistent summer reading list — fox10phoenix.com — 2025-05; Otago Daily Times, Leary blunder — odt.co.nz — 2025-12.

### C-07 Hallucinated product/policy claims
- **Category:** Content
- **Classification:** Hard failure (creates binding legal liability)
- **What it looks like:** Copy asserting features, integrations, certifications, or pricing that don't exist ("SOC-2 certified", "HIPAA compliant", "free forever", invented refund terms); compliance badges never earned; docs linking to nonexistent pages; chatbot/copy stating policies absent from terms.
- **Why it fails:** The precedent case: *Moffatt v. Air Canada* — the airline's website chatbot invented a retroactive bereavement-refund policy; the BC Civil Resolution Tribunal rejected the "chatbot is a separate legal entity" defense and held Air Canada liable (Feb 14, 2024; CAN$812.02 awarded). The structural cause: LLMs optimize for fluency, not truth — "confident guessing over admitting uncertainty" is rewarded by eval metrics. CNET's 41+ corrected AI articles show the same failure in editorial copy.
- **Detection signals:** Diff every factual claim (features, certifications, prices, dates) against source-of-truth docs; claim inventory in review checklist; certification-badge verification.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never. Aspirational roadmap items must be labeled as roadmap.
- **Better alternatives:** Ground generation in real product docs (RAG with citations); human fact-check pass; conservative copy ("Currently supports X").
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** swept.ai, Air Canada chatbot case — swept.ai — 2024; Nordia Law AI-hallucination business guide — nordialaw.com — 2026-07; consuly.ai liability analysis — 2026-05-31.

### C-08 Rhetorical & rhythm tells: em-dash overuse, rule of three, negative parallelism, metronomic cadence
- **Category:** Content
- **Classification:** Contextual risk (frequency is the tell; never proof alone)
- **What it looks like:** >1 em dash per 100 words ("claim — pivot" rhythm every sentence); triads ("Fast. Secure. Scalable."); "It's not just X, it's Y"; "No fluff. No bloat. Just results."; unearned profundity ("Something shifted."); mid-sentence questions ("The solution? Simple."); paragraphs where every sentence is 12–20 words with identical clause structure (low burstiness).
- **Why it fails (as a tell):** WP:RO3 and WP:AIPARALLEL on Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing; "the single most recognizable AI writing tic" (red-pen skill); the Field Guide to AI Slop catalogs snappy triads, unearned profundity, and em-dash overload; OpenAI itself treated the em-dash habit as a bug (Altman announced ChatGPT would honor "don't use em-dashes" instructions, Nov 2025). Low burstiness is one of the two foundational AI-detection metrics (GPTZero lineage). These devices "make superficial analyses appear more comprehensive" (Wikipedia).
- **Counter-evidence (important):** Em dashes predate AI by ~200 years; tricolons are classical oratory; and Stanford (Liang et al., Patterns 2023) found 7 detectors misclassified **61.3%** of human-written TOEFL essays as AI — rhythm-based flagging punishes non-native and careful-formal writers. Use as one weak signal among several, never as sole evidence.
- **Detection signals:** Dash density per 100 words; regex `not (just|only) .* but`; trigram counts in `X, Y, and Z` form; sentence-length stdev/mean ratio; paragraph-length uniformity.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Deliberate rhetoric in speeches/keynotes; literary/journalistic house styles; specs/legal/SOP writing where uniformity aids comprehension.
- **Better alternatives:** Periods, colons, parentheses; state what the product *is* rather than what it isn't; let one strong claim stand alone; deliberate rhythm variation (Gary Provost's advice).
- **Confidence:** High (as a tell); Medium-to-weak (as evidence of AI in any given instance)
- **Sources:** Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing — en.wikipedia.org — 2026; The Field Guide to AI Slop — ignorance.ai — 2025-10; Liang et al. (Stanford), arXiv:2304.02819 — 2023; The Bridge Chronicle (Altman em-dash fix) — 2025-11.

### C-09 Formatting tells: emoji bullets, mechanical boldface & list-soup
- **Category:** Content
- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** Lists where every item opens with a "meaningful" emoji (✅ features, 🚀 benefits, 💡 tips); `<strong>` on 5+ phrases per paragraph; bolded lead-in label + colon on every list item ("**Speed:** Blazing fast…"); every section rendered as bullets; Unicode 𝗯𝗼𝗹𝗱/𝘪𝘵𝘢𝘭𝘪𝘤 pasted into platforms without formatting.
- **Why it fails:** Explicitly catalogued AI tells: "No sane person I know is regularly formatting their work emails with a list of emoji-led bullet points" (Field Guide to AI Slop); WP:AIBOLD — "excessive, mechanical manner… inherited from readmes, fan wikis, how-tos, sales pitches, slide decks" (Wikipedia). Random bolding "doesn't emphasize key points — it's just sort of *there*"; prose shattered into bullets destroys typographic hierarchy; emoji "checkmarks" make screen readers announce "white heavy check mark" before every item. Mismatch with a serious B2B/regulated brand is the actual failure.
- **Detection signals:** Emoji at line-start of `<li>` elements; emoji density per page; bold-density ratio; heading/bullet vs prose ratio; Unicode math-alphanumeric block detection.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Casual contexts: changelogs, Discord/community posts, playful consumer brands, social media; genuine key-takeaway boxes and spec sheets where bolding follows a consistent editorial rule.
- **Better alternatives:** Real typographic bullets; custom icon system with alt text; reserve bold for genuine key terms; use headings/spacing for hierarchy; write prose where prose reads better.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High
- **Sources:** The Field Guide to AI Slop — ignorance.ai — 2025-10; Wikipedia:Signs of AI writing (WP:AIBOLD) — 2026; DeGPT LinkedIn AI tells — 2026-01; humanize-writing npm skill — 2026-02.

### Accessibility (A)

*Cross-cutting evidence: three peer-reviewed studies audit LLM-generated web code directly — Aljedaani et al. (W4A 2024; 1,883 axe/WAVE issues in ChatGPT-4 output; 74/88 generated sites — 84% — had detectable violations), Suh et al. (2025; "LLMs surpass humans in basic web accessibility but struggle with complex ARIA"), Abu Doush & Kassem (UAIS 2025; 1,000 outputs, 5 LLMs), and Gurita & Vatavu (W4A 2025; barriers "persist in structure, focus order, and error prevention" even under accessibility prompting). WebAIM Million provides deployment-scale ground truth: 94.8% of home pages have detectable WCAG failures — AI models trained on that corpus reproduce its top errors faithfully, plus one new failure class (ARIA over-application, hallucinated alt text). The architectural exception is v0: shadcn/ui wraps Radix primitives, so focus traps, keyboard nav, and aria states ship from the library — "the exception, not the rule" (Frontend Masters).*

### A-01 Clickable `<div>`/`<span>` instead of semantic controls ("div soup")
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** `<div onClick={...}>`, `<span onClick={...}>`, Tailwind `cursor-pointer` on non-interactive elements; clickable card wrappers without a real link/button inside. A11y tree: `role: generic`, `name: none`, `focusable: false`.
- **Why it fails:** Per-instance violation of WCAG 2.1.1 Keyboard (A) and 4.1.2 Name, Role, Value (A); W3C formal failure technique F59 covers exactly this. Screen readers announce nothing actionable; Enter/Space don't fire; focus is invisible. Found "in the vast majority of the interactive components generated by these tools" (Frontend Masters teardown — one 29-line ChatGPT sidebar contained 10 distinct failures); listed among the most common failures in AI-generated app submissions (Sunpeak). Visual judges can't see the missing interaction contract — screenshot-based evaluation even rewards functionally broken but complex-looking code (Qwen team, "The Verification Horizon," 2026).
- **Detection signals:** eslint-plugin-jsx-a11y `click-events-have-key-events`, `no-static-element-interactions`, `interactive-supports-focus`; Playwright a11y-tree snapshot. axe does NOT reliably catch this — needs the lint layer or tree inspection.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Practically never for the interactive element itself (divs are fine as layout around a real button/link).
- **Better alternatives:** `<button type="button">` for actions; `<a href>` for navigation; stretched-link pattern for clickable cards; Radix primitives.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** W3C WAI, Failure F59 — w3.org; Frontend Masters, "AI-Generated UI Is Inaccessible by Default" — frontendmasters.com — 2026-04; Stanford UIT — 2026-05; Sunpeak — sunpeak.ai — 2026-05.

### A-02 Fake composite widgets (modal/select/disclosure without the APG contract)
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** A "modal" that is a `fixed inset-0 z-50` overlay div with no `role="dialog"`, no `aria-modal`, no focus trap, no Escape handler; a "dropdown" that doesn't manage focus or announce state; a custom `<select>` built from `<ul>`; triggers without `aria-expanded`/`aria-controls`.
- **Why it fails:** Violates 2.1.1 and 4.1.2 per instance; modals also implicate 2.4.3 Focus Order. The ARIA Authoring Practices Guide: "a role is a promise" — these components don't even make the promise. Focus escapes behind the overlay; screen-reader users don't know a dialog opened; Escape doesn't close. LLMs struggle with complex ARIA widgets specifically (Suh et al.); WebAIM 2026: 22% of ARIA menu implementations introduce new barriers; the 2026 systematic review names "broken or incorrect ARIA usage" a recurring barrier.
- **Detection signals:** Grep `fixed inset-0`/`z-50` + absence of `role="dialog"`/`aria-modal`; Playwright: open widget, test Tab containment, Escape, `aria-expanded`; axe catches missing roles on some patterns.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never ship the fake version (visually custom widgets are fine IF they implement the full APG pattern — hand-rolled AI output doesn't).
- **Better alternatives:** Native `<dialog>`; Radix UI, Headless UI, React Aria, shadcn/ui (wraps Radix); instruct the LLM to use them in the prompt.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** W3C ARIA Authoring Practices Guide — w3.org; Frontend Masters — 2026-04; Suh, Zheng & Latulipe, arXiv:2503.15885 — 2025-03; WebAIM Million 2026 — webaim.org/projects/million.

### A-03 Unnamed & ambiguous interactive elements (icon-only buttons, empty links, "Learn more" farms)
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure for unnamed elements; ambiguous link text is Contextual leaning Hard (fails only AAA 2.4.9, but a documented high-frequency barrier)
- **What it looks like:** `<button class="p-2"><svg/></button>` with no aria-label (hamburgers, close X's, trash, gear); `<a href><svg/></a>`; card grids each ending in an identical "Learn more →" / "Read more" / "Click here."
- **Why it fails:** Screen-reader users hear "button" with no purpose; voice-control users can't target it; SR users navigating by links list hear "Learn more, Learn more, Learn more" — purpose unknowable. Violates 4.1.2 (A) and 2.4.4/2.4.6; Deque classifies missing button names as Critical. WebAIM 2026: 30.6% of home pages had empty buttons, 46.3% empty links; ambiguous links on 13–18% of pages. Aljedaani: 54 empty anchors + 54 link-purpose failures in ChatGPT output.
- **Detection signals:** axe `button-name` and `link-name` catch these reliably; Playwright tree: `role: button, name: none`; grep `>Learn more<`, `>Read more<`, `>Click here<` in JSX.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Icon-only buttons are legitimate WHEN they carry an accessible name, ideally with a visible affordance (tooltip); "Read more" is acceptable when programmatically associated with context (`aria-label="Read more about X"` or inside `<article aria-labelledby>`).
- **Better alternatives:** `aria-label` on the button; visually-hidden `<span>`; prefer visible text labels where space allows; descriptive link text ("Read the Q3 report").
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Deque University, axe button-name — dequeuniversity.com; WebAIM Million 2026 — webaim.org; Aljedaani et al., W4A '24 — wajdialjedaani.com — 2024-05; Sunpeak — 2026-05.

### A-04 Missing/unassociated form labels; placeholder-as-label
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** `<input placeholder="Email">` with no label; `<label>` without `htmlFor`/`id` pairing; label near input but not wrapping/referencing it; floating-label patterns relying on placeholder-shown CSS.
- **Why it fails:** Violates 1.3.1 (A) and 3.3.2 (A); per-instance, machine-detectable, no legitimate-context exception. Aljedaani's top cluster: 69 missing labels + 62 unassociated + 14 placeholder-as-label + 11 empty labels. WebAIM 2025: 34.2% of all form inputs unlabeled. NN/g documents seven harms of placeholder-as-label even for sighted users (memory strain; no pre-submit review; broken error recovery; filled fields are LESS noticeable — eyetracking shows eyes go to empty fields; placeholders mistaken for pre-filled data). The HTML spec itself: "The placeholder attribute should not be used as a replacement for a label."
- **Detection signals:** axe `label`; WAVE missing/orphaned form label; Playwright tree `role: textbox, name: none`; grep `<input` lines lacking both `aria-label` and an `id` referenced by a `label`; placeholder contrast <4.5:1.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never omit an accessible name. Visually-hidden labels acceptable; single search box / simple login with visible label; placeholders fine as supplemental hints alongside a real label.
- **Better alternatives:** `<label htmlFor={id}>` + `<input id={id}>`; `sr-only` label class for minimal designs; visible label above field + persistent hint/error text (NN/g "Best").
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Aljedaani et al., W4A '24 — 2024-05; NN/g, "Placeholders in Form Fields Are Harmful" (Sherwin) — nngroup.com — 2014; WebAIM Million 2025 — webaim.org; Web Axe quoting W3C HTML spec — webaxe.org.

### A-05 Gray-on-gray low-contrast text (the "muted" aesthetic)
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure when below 4.5:1 (WCAG 1.4.3); the muted instinct itself is Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** `text-gray-400` (#9CA3AF on white ≈ 2.5:1 — fails everything), `text-gray-500` (≈4.8:1 — passes only large text), `text-muted-foreground` on `bg-white`/`bg-gray-50`; `placeholder:text-gray-400`; `font-weight: 300` body copy; perma-dark with ~#777-on-#111; light-on-light hero subtext. (Ratios computed from Tailwind v3 palette by the a11y stream.)
- **Why it fails:** The #1 accessibility error on the web — 83.9% of home pages, seven consecutive years (WebAIM) — and AI models trained on that corpus reproduce it faithfully: contrast was the #2 category in ChatGPT output (232 very-low + 38 low instances, Aljedaani); Copilot INTRODUCED contrast failures in a hover state (Mowar et al.); "poor contrast ratios" was the most frequently flagged WAVE issue in generated banking UIs (Gurita & Vatavu). NN/g: low-contrast text is "illegible, undiscoverable, and inaccessible" — users blame themselves and abandon; dimmed text creates false affordances (reads as disabled); people also trust hard-to-read text less. HN discourse ranks this the most-cited *harm* (not taste): "dark grey text on a different dark grey background so you don't see what you're typing even with 20/20 sight."
- **Detection signals:** axe `color-contrast`; WAVE contrast flags; grep `text-gray-[34]00`, `text-slate-[34]00`, `text-neutral-[34]00` on light backgrounds; Lighthouse.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never for essential text. Disabled controls and purely decorative text exempt; large text (18pt+/14pt bold+) gets the relaxed 3:1 threshold; tertiary metadata is achievable at ≥4.5:1 with darker grays.
- **Better alternatives:** `text-gray-600` (≈7.5:1) or darker; lower prominence via size/position/density, not contrast (NN/g's strategies); test every pair against 4.5:1 (AA normal) / 3:1 (AA large).
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** WebAIM Million 2026 — webaim.org; Aljedaani et al., W4A '24 — 2024-05; NN/g, "Low-Contrast Text Is Not the Answer" — nngroup.com — 2015; HN thread on Krebs study — news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47864393 — 2026-04-22.

### A-06 Missing/empty/wrong alt text (incl. hallucinated AI alt)
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure for missing/empty-on-informative; hallucinated alt is a hard failure when it occurs (prevalence unquantified)
- **What it looks like:** `<img src="hero.png">` with no alt; `<img alt="">` on product photos/charts; `alt="image"`/`alt="photo"`; AI-written alt describing objects not in the image.
- **Why it fails:** Violates 1.1.1 Non-text Content (A). Aljedaani: 56 missing alt texts (4th-largest cluster); WebAIM 2025: 53.1% of home pages had images missing alt; 9.6% of images had unhelpful alt like "image"/"graphic". Developers shipped empty alt on informative images DESPITE Copilot nudging toward alternatives (Mowar et al., CHI '25) — and blank alt on informative images is "not even flagged by automated accessibility checkers." Decorative-vs-informative judgment is context-dependent and LLMs get it wrong both directions; the 2026 systematic review names "hallucinated image descriptions" an emergent LLM-specific risk.
- **Detection signals:** axe `image-alt`; WAVE missing/empty alt flags; grep `<img` without `alt`; cross-check `alt=""` images for decorativeness; hallucination requires manual spot-check (no automated check exists).
- **When it may be appropriate:** Empty `alt=""` IS correct for purely decorative images — the problem is AI making that judgment wrong. Missing `alt` entirely: never.
- **Better alternatives:** Short, function-oriented alt (what the image DOES); human review of AI-drafted alt; text/data-table alternative for charts.
- **Confidence:** High (missing/empty prevalence); Medium (quantified hallucinated-alt rate)
- **Sources:** WebAIM Million 2025 — webaim.org; Aljedaani et al., W4A '24 — 2024-05; Mowar et al., CodeA11y, CHI '25 — peyajm29.github.io — 2025; LLM-accessibility SLR, arXiv:2605.13873 — 2026.

### A-07 ARIA spam and misuse
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Avoid by default (hard failure when misused)
- **What it looks like:** `aria-label` on `<div>`/`<span>`/`<p>` without a role (naming prohibited/ignored); `role="button"` on a div; redundant `role="navigation"` on `<nav>`; `aria-hidden="true"` on focusable elements; visible text conflicting with aria-label.
- **Why it fails:** Reflexive, unverified ARIA violates 4.1.2 and the first rule of ARIA (use native HTML instead). WebAIM 2026: ARIA usage jumped 27% YoY to 133 attributes/page; pages with ARIA averaged 59.1 detected errors vs 42 without (caveat: correlation, not pure causation — ARIA marks complex pages; the causal claim is specifically about INCORRECT ARIA). APG: "No ARIA is better than Bad ARIA." Leonie Watson/TPGi: aria-label on divs/spans without roles "generally won't work." LLMs are weak on complex ARIA; iterative prompting reaches only partial remediation (Suh et al.).
- **Detection signals:** axe `aria-allowed-attr`, `aria-allowed-role`, duplicate-ids; grep `aria-label` on elements without `role=`; count ARIA attributes per component vs native semantics.
- **When it may be appropriate:** ARIA is REQUIRED for genuinely custom widgets (comboboxes, tabs, live regions, icon-button names) — the failure is using it as decoration or instead of native HTML.
- **Better alternatives:** Native elements first (`<button>`, `<nav>`, `<dialog>`, `<label>`); when ARIA is needed, follow the APG pattern exactly and test with a screen reader.
- **Confidence:** High (with correlation caveat)
- **Sources:** W3C APG, "Read Me First" — w3.org; WebAIM Million 2026 — webaim.org; Vispero/TPGi + tink.uk (Watson) — 2026; Suh et al., arXiv:2503.15885 — 2025-03.

### A-08 Removed focus indicators (`outline-none` without replacement)
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** `focus:outline-none` without a paired `focus:ring`/`focus-visible:` style; `*: { outline: none }` resets; `tabIndex={-1}` on interactive elements.
- **Why it fails:** Violates 2.4.7 Focus Visible (AA): invisible focus = criterion fails; keyboard users lose all position feedback. The Tailwind docs idiom teaches removal PAIRED with a ring replacement (`focus:outline-none focus:ring-2`) — generated code frequently copies only the removal half (documented in frontend-skill blocklists; Frontend Masters' example rules: "never remove outlines without a replacement"). Counter-evidence: shadcn/ui and v0 defaults ship `focus-visible:ring` — this is a default-Tailwind/hand-rolled failure more than a shadcn-stack failure.
- **Detection signals:** Grep `outline-none`/`outline: none`/`outline:0`, verify each has an adjacent `focus-visible`/`focus:ring` replacement; Tab walkthrough; axe does not fully catch this — manual/Playwright keyboard test needed.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Removing the DEFAULT outline is fine only when a clearly visible custom focus style replaces it (≥3:1 against adjacent colors).
- **Better alternatives:** Keep browser defaults; `focus-visible:ring-2 focus-visible:ring-offset-2`; `:focus-visible` so mouse users don't see rings but keyboard users do.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High (mechanism certain; half-copied idiom documented; no AI-specific quantified prevalence)
- **Sources:** Tailwind CSS v3 docs, focus states — v3.tailwindcss.com; romiluz13/frontend-patterns blocklist — github.com — 2025; Frontend Masters — 2026-04.

### A-09 Motion and autoplay without `prefers-reduced-motion`
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure when motion autoplays >5s without pause/stop controls (WCAG 2.2.2, A) or flashes >3/sec (2.3.1); otherwise Contextual risk
- **What it looks like:** `animate-shimmer`/`animate-spin`/`animate-pulse` with infinite repeat; zero `@media (prefers-reduced-motion)` anywhere in the project; Framer Motion/GSAP without `useReducedMotion`/`MotionConfig reducedMotion="user"`; `<video autoplay loop>` heroes; Lottie `autoplay loop`; `scroll-behavior: smooth` without opt-out.
- **Why it fails:** Vestibular effects — flashing/spinning/scaling/large-movement animation triggers dizziness, nausea, headaches (Esri ArcUser); a documented AI-review finding ruled a 2s repeating countdown an explicit WCAG 2.2.2 violation (focused-tube #117); real libraries in this idiom needed dedicated fixes (raystack/apsara #677 catalogs shimmer/sheet/dialog transitions playing "regardless of user preference"; framer/motion #401); Sunpeak lists animations/skeletons "without prefers-reduced-motion support" among the most common AI-app failures. Non-interference makes 2.2.2 a page-level conformance issue.
- **Detection signals:** Animations present but zero `prefers-reduced-motion`/`motion-safe`/`useReducedMotion` grep hits — the absence is the tell; grep `animate-`, `autoplay`, `loop`, `Infinity`; DevTools emulate reduce → animations still play.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Motion is fine when user-triggered, short, small-scale, or gated behind `prefers-reduced-motion: no-preference`. Autoplaying >5s content without controls: never.
- **Better alternatives:** Reduce (not eliminate) motion under the media query; `motion-safe:animate-*`; global `@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)` guard; Framer Motion `useReducedMotion()`; pause/stop controls on >5s autoplay.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High (documented in practitioner sources and real issues; no systematic prevalence measurement for AI-generated code)
- **Sources:** W3C, Understanding SC 2.2.2 — w3.org; raystack/apsara issue #677 — github.com — 2026-03; Josh Comeau, "Accessible Animations with prefers-reduced-motion" — joshwcomeau.com — 2020; Sunpeak — 2026-05.

### A-10 Semantic structure collapse (headings-as-divs, skipped levels, missing landmarks)
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure for headings-as-styled-divs and missing landmarks (1.3.1, A); Contextual for skipped heading levels (best practice rather than strict WCAG failure)
- **What it looks like:** `<div class="text-2xl font-bold">` acting as a heading; `<h1>` then `<h3>` with no `<h2>`; multiple `<h1>`s; whole page in `<div>`/`<section>` with no `<main>`/`<header>`/`<nav>`/`<footer>`; card lists as bare divs instead of `<ul>`.
- **Why it fails:** 60%+ of screen-reader users navigate primarily by headings (WebAIM). Aljedaani: "Info and Relationships" was the #3 violated category (146 instances). Gurita & Vatavu: semantic-structure barriers persisted across all models even under accessibility prompting. Frontend Masters' ChatGPT sidebar: no `<nav>`, no `<h2>`, no `<ul>` — "a screen reader announces it as 'one group containing 9 items.'" WebAIM 2025: 39% of home pages skipped levels, 16.3% had multiple `<h1>`s, 9.8% no headings at all, only 46.1% have a `<main>`.
- **Detection signals:** axe `landmark-one-main`, `page-has-heading-one`; WAVE structure panel; Playwright tree; grep `text-(xl|2xl|3xl) font-` on non-heading tags.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Divs with heading STYLING are fine for non-heading text; skipped levels tolerable when reflecting genuine hierarchy transitions. Missing `<main>` / headings-as-divs: never.
- **Better alternatives:** One `<h1>`, logical descending levels, `<header>/<nav>/<main>/<footer>` landmarks, `<ul>` for repeated items.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** WebAIM Million 2025/2026 — webaim.org; Aljedaani et al., W4A '24 — 2024-05; Gurita & Vatavu, W4A '25 — dl.acm.org — 2025-04; Frontend Masters — 2026-04.

### A-11 Undersized/cramped click targets
- **Category:** Accessibility
- **Classification:** Hard failure (WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8, Level AA)
- **What it looks like:** 16–20px icon buttons; ~8px carousel dots; `p-1` on 16px SVGs; adjacent icon buttons with zero gap; packed card-action icons.
- **Why it fails:** WCAG 2.5.8 requires targets ≥24×24 CSS px (or spacing such that 24px circles don't intersect) — users with tremors/motor impairments and touch users mis-tap neighbors; AAA enhanced = 44×44. Fitts's law (1954): acquisition time scales with distance/size — small targets are slower and error-prone for everyone.
- **Detection signals:** Computed boxes <24×24px; Lighthouse "tap targets too small/too close."
- **When it may be appropriate:** Inline sentence links (exempt); UA defaults; maps (essential exception).
- **Better alternatives:** 24px hit area via padding even with 16px glyphs; 44px primary touch CTAs.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** W3C, Understanding WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8 Target Size (Minimum) — w3.org; Laws of UX, Fitts's Law (Yablonski) — lawsofux.com.

### I-01 Production console noise (emoji console.logs, debug blocks, verbose error copy)
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Avoid by default → **Hard failure** when logs leak tokens/PII/internals
- **What it looks like:** Emoji-prefixed `console.log("✅ ...")`; box-drawing debug banners (─ │ ┌); essay-length error messages; `console.error(err)` catch-and-continue; one documented production codebase shipped 139 emoji-prefixed console.logs + 132 box-drawing debug blocks.
- **Why it fails:** Mirage Security extracted stack intelligence from a live platform purely via uncleaned client debug output, calling these "a signature pattern of Claude-generated code." "Overly verbose error handling: catch blocks that log and continue" is red flag #1 of AI output (techdebt.works); vibe-coding security checklists flag sensitive data in logs.
- **Detection signals:** `grep -E "console\.(log|debug)\(" dist/*.js`; emoji/box-drawing chars in bundles; error monitoring absence.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Server-side structured logs; dev-only logging stripped by the build.
- **Better alternatives:** Strip console in prod builds; error monitoring (Sentry); short actionable user-facing error copy.
- **Confidence:** High (the pattern); Medium (as a reliable model-attribution fingerprint — single-vendor observation)
- **Sources:** Mirage Security, "Inside p1bot" — miragesecurity.ai — 2026-01-24; techdebt.works/ai-code-review — 2026-02-24; nxcode.io vibe-coding checklist — 2026-06-23; benavlabs/vibe-check — github.com — 2026-04.

### I-02 Hydration mismatch errors on first paint
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** "Hydration failed because the initial UI does not match what was rendered on the server" / "Text content does not match server-rendered HTML"; `Date.now()`, `Math.random()`, `localStorage`, `window.innerWidth`, `toLocaleTimeString()` read during SSR render; theme read from localStorage in render.
- **Why it fails:** Runtime error breaking interactivity, causing visible glitches, potentially corrupting SEO indexing; React 19/Next 15 surface these as hard errors. Bolt launch audits list "console errors on first paint from hydration mismatch" as a common skip; the non-deterministic-value-in-SSR cause is documented as AI-typical (LogRocket; dailydevpost; generalistprogrammer).
- **Detection signals:** Console on first load; React errors #418/#423/#425; grep `Date.now()`/`Math.random()`/`localStorage` in SSR render paths; missing `'use client'`.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never; `suppressHydrationWarning` is a narrow escape hatch, not a fix.
- **Better alternatives:** Client-only logic in `useEffect`/client components; `dynamic(..., {ssr:false})`; SSR-safe defaults.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** launchauditor.com/for/bolt — 2026; LogRocket, resolving hydration mismatch — blog.logrocket.com — 2024-08-15; dailydevpost.com — 2026-02-12; generalistprogrammer.com — 2026-05-23.

### I-03 Scaffold head debris (default title, missing OG/meta, stock favicon)
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Hard failure (misleading identity + degraded sharing/SEO; favicon variant low-severity)
- **What it looks like:** `<title>Vite + React</title>` in production; absent `meta description`, `og:title/og:description/og:image`; shares render as naked URLs; `/favicon.ico` 404s or the builder platform's own default icon on a customer's product.
- **Why it fails:** Bolt audits list "Missing meta title — still showing 'Vite + React' in tab" and "No favicon or OG image" as standard findings; Lovable generated only basic title/description/OG by default until its April 2026 SEO upgrade (canonical, schema, favicon, sitemap needed explicit prompts). Missing OG produces "inaccurate or incomplete social media previews, potentially diminishing click-through rates." Lovable projects "ship with a default Lovable favicon," and replacement routinely fails via wrong /public paths and aggressive caching (RapidDevelopers).
- **Detection signals:** curl/view-source for scaffold-default `<title>`; absent `og:` tags; social card validators; DevTools Network favicon 404; missing `<link rel="icon">`/`apple-touch-icon`.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never for public pages.
- **Better alternatives:** Per-route metadata (Next.js Metadata API); generated OG images; canonical + sitemap; real icon set in /public (ico + PNGs + 180×180 touch icon + site.webmanifest); verify incognito.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** launchauditor.com/for/bolt — 2026; amoenos.com, SEO for Lovable — 2025-05 (updated 2026); rapidevelopers.com, Lovable favicon guide — 2026-03-25.

### I-04 Fixed pixel widths / desktop-only layout → mobile horizontal overflow
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Hard failure (direct WCAG 1.4.10 Reflow violation, AA)
- **What it looks like:** `w-[1200px]`, `width:1200px; margin:0 auto` without `max-w-full`; grids without responsive variants; no `sm:`/`md:` prefixes; overflowing tables/carousels; "Login/signup flows broken on mobile viewport" (Bolt audit list).
- **Why it fails:** WCAG 1.4.10 (AA) requires reflow at 320 CSS px without two-dimensional scrolling; fixed-width layout is the canonical failure example (Deacon; BrowseCheck). Generators are structurally desktop-first — the mobile render is a mechanical `grid-cols-1` byproduct, not a redesign; Chakra UI's AI-review skill explicitly checks generated code for "hardcoded pixel widths, missing breakpoints, desktop only layouts."
- **Detection signals:** 320–375px viewport → horizontal scrollbar; `scrollWidth > innerWidth`; grep `w-\[\d{3,4}px\]` on layout containers; class lists lacking responsive prefixes.
- **When it may be appropriate:** WCAG-exempt only when 2-D layout is essential (maps, true data tables); fixed widths fine in micro-components.
- **Better alternatives:** Mobile-first utilities (`w-full max-w-7xl mx-auto px-4`, `grid-cols-1 md:grid-cols-3`); test at 320px and 400% zoom.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** BrowseCheck, WCAG 1.4.10 reflow guide — browsecheck.com — 2026-03-23; Dennis Deacon, testing reflow — dennisdeacon.com — 2025-09; launchauditor.com/for/bolt — 2026; chakra-ui.com AI skills docs.

### I-05 Codebase structure debt: god components, arbitrary-value spam, unused imports/dead code
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Avoid by default (maintainability smell; failure-adjacent at scale)
- **What it looks like:** One `page.tsx`/`App.tsx` containing hero→footer (300–1000+ lines); data fetching + transforms + validation + every section's markup in one file; dozens of unique Tailwind arbitrary values per file (`w-[823px]`, `mt-[13px]`, `bg-[#1e293b]`, `p-[7px]`); zero-reference imports; commented-out blocks; "just in case" components; ~20% bundle reduction from one pruning pass on Bolt projects.
- **Why it fails:** "AI tends to generate monolithic functions… impossible to unit test" (techdebt.works); "AI often includes imports for libraries it planned to use but ultimately did not… bloats bundle sizes… pulls in dependencies with known vulnerabilities"; arbitrary values "make consistent theming harder" and break dark mode (MakersDen); dead code is a common model refactor failure (Scale SWE-Atlas). Also degrades the AI tools themselves: Bolt projects reportedly hit deploy failures past ~20 components (vendor claim).
- **Detection signals:** File >300–500 lines; single exported component per route; ESLint `max-lines`/`complexity`/`no-unused-vars`; TS `noUnusedLocals`; `npx knip`; arbitrary-class ratio per file; eslint-plugin-tailwindcss.
- **When it may be appropriate:** True one-pagers/prototypes where a single file is honest scope; genuinely one-off arbitrary values (Tailwind officially supports the syntax — pervasiveness is the failure).
- **Better alternatives:** Section components + custom hooks; route-level code splitting; extend theme tokens; extract repeated combos; prune pass before merge; CI gate on knip.
- **Confidence:** High (dead code/arbitrary values); Medium-High (god component; "20-component ceiling" is vendor-sourced)
- **Sources:** techdebt.works/ai-code-review — 2026-02-24; AfterBuild Labs — afterbuildlabs.com — 2026-04-15; MakersDen Tailwind maintainability — makersden.io — 2025-04-12; Scale SWE-Atlas — scale.com — 2026-05-07.

### I-06 Placeholder-image services & dead stock hotlinks in production
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** `source.unsplash.com/random...` URLs returning Heroku error pages; `placehold.co/600x400` grey boxes with dimension text on live marketing sites; `via.placeholder.com`, `picsum.photos`, `loremflickr`, `dummyimage` in production markup; hotlinked `images.unsplash.com` with no license hygiene.
- **Why it fails:** Broken images are objectively broken UI: source.unsplash.com is **completely deprecated**, serving a generic Heroku application error — every such image is broken (paul.af; dev.to reported 500s in 2023); nuxt/website-v2 issues #2276/#2278 (Apr 2026) document 503 breakage in production; placehold.it died earlier with an invalid certificate (2021); OpenReplay (Dec 2025) calls via.placeholder.com "now unreliable." Hotlinked third-party assets carry no SLA. Grey "600×400" boxes presented as content are fabricated filler.
- **Detection signals:** Grep built HTML for `placehold.co|via.placeholder|placehold.it|picsum.photos|source.unsplash|loremflickr|dummyimage`; 4xx/5xx image requests in DevTools.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Wireframes, staging, storybook fixtures — never production.
- **Better alternatives:** Real owned assets; locally saved AI-generated images; official Unsplash API with attribution; self-hosted licensed stock; framework image components.
- **Confidence:** High (service deaths verified); Medium (frequency among AI sites — anecdotal)
- **Sources:** paul.af, "RIP Unsplash Source" — 2024-07-03; nuxt/website-v2 issues #2276/#2278 — github.com — 2026-04; OpenReplay placeholder-services review — 2025-12; dev.to hotlinking report — 2023-06-29.

### I-07 Hardcoded config values & hallucinated URLs/endpoints
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Contextual risk → Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** `fetch("https://api.myapp.com/v1/...")` inline in JSX; `const TIMEOUT = 3000`; env-specific hosts hardcoded; plausible API endpoints the AI invented that don't exist in the infrastructure.
- **Why it fails:** "AI sometimes invents plausible-looking URLs or API endpoints that do not exist in your infrastructure" (techdebt.works); breaks across environments; fabricates nonexistent infrastructure that later code trusts. v0 review guidance: "Move hardcoded values to environment variables." (Hardcoded *secrets* in client bundles are a separate hard failure — see I-13.)
- **Detection signals:** Grep `https?://` literals outside config; magic-number timeouts; inconsistent env access.
- **When it may be appropriate:** True constants; single-environment throwaway prototypes.
- **Better alternatives:** `.env` + typed config module; named constants; verify generated endpoints against real infra.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High
- **Sources:** techdebt.works — 2026-02; Vibe App Scanner, v0 review guidance — vibeappscanner.com — 2026-02.

### I-08 CSR SPA shell + monolithic bundle for static content
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Hard failure when the page's job is content delivery/SEO/lead capture
- **What it looks like:** `view-source` shows an empty `<div id="root"></div>` + one `<script type="module" src="/assets/index-[hash].js">`; all text/alt/JSON-LD renders post-JS; `App.tsx` with plain `import Dashboard from './pages/Dashboard'` for every route; zero `import()`; single `index-[hash].js` of 1–several MB; `import _ from 'lodash'` / `import moment from 'moment'` / `import * as Icons from 'lucide-react'`.
- **Why it fails:** web.dev's *Rendering on the Web* explicitly recommends server/static rendering over full client re-rendering; CSR raises TBT/INP and ties FCP to bundle download+parse. Documented SEO impact: two-wave indexing ("Crawled – currently not indexed"), ~9× slower indexing for JS-heavy pages, broken social previews by default — and AI crawlers (ChatGPT/Claude/Perplexity) largely don't execute JS at all. Hoko finds the no-code-splitting pattern *systematically*: a login-page visitor downloads every page's code; with global UI imports (Radix, Lucide, Recharts) bundles reach several MB. Baseline: median page already ships 558KB JS with ~44% unused (Web Almanac 2024). Whole-library imports are the #1 anti-pattern in the Hashnode audit of AI-generated React apps (moment ≈290KB with locales for one date format). KrabArena's one-shot benchmark: "a 25/25 checklist run can easily score 50/100 on Lighthouse."
- **Detection signals:** Fetch HTML with JS disabled → near-empty body; single large chunk in build output; no dynamic import in router; Lighthouse "Reduce unused JavaScript"; bundle-analyzer treemap.
- **When it may be appropriate:** App-like surfaces behind login; tiny single-view tools. **v0 is the documented exception** among AI builders — outputs server-rendered Next.js with metadata/OG/JSON-LD by default (Studio Slate review; vendor claim but the architecture supports it).
- **Better alternatives:** SSG/SSR (Astro, Next static export, plain HTML+CSS); islands; prerendering; route-level lazy + Suspense; vendor manualChunks; `date-fns`/`Intl.DateTimeFormat`; `lodash/<method>`; named icon imports.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** web.dev, "Rendering on the Web" — web.dev — updated 2026-01; Hoko Vibe Code Audit — hoko.team — 2026-02; Studio Slate AI Website Builders review — studioslate.com.au — 2026-07; Hashnode, "Why Your AI-Generated React App Fails Lighthouse" — 2026-04.

### I-09 Unoptimized hero media (multi-MB images & video; no priority; missing dimensions)
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Hard failure for LCP-breaking hero media; missing-dimensions CLS is Contextual (see counter-evidence)
- **What it looks like:** `<img src="/hero.jpg">` at 400KB–4MB with no compression, no AVIF/WebP, no `srcset`, no `fetchpriority="high"`; hero injected by JS or CSS `background-image` (late discovery); `<video autoplay loop src="hero.mp4">` multi-MB single-format with no `poster`/`playsinline`/`muted`; multi-MB demo GIFs used as videos; dimension-less `<img>` in fluid layouts.
- **Why it fails:** LCP target is ≤2.5s for 75% of visits (web.dev); DevNova measured median LCP 6.6s / 70% poor on AI-builder sites, attributing it to full frameworks, animation libraries, and "full-size images with no compression"; the hero fix is "the single biggest LCP improvement most AI-generated apps need" (Hashnode). web.dev's GIF→video data: a 3.7MB GIF becomes a 551KB MP4 / 341KB WebM. **Counter-evidence (matters):** DevNova found 93% of AI-builder sites had GOOD CLS — "this they get right" — so missing-dimensions layout shift is a real mechanism but NOT the dominant measured failure; flag when found, don't assume.
- **Detection signals:** Lighthouse next-gen-formats/properly-size/LCP-element audits; hero fetched after bundle execution in the network panel; >1MB video above the fold; "Use video formats for animated content" (flags GIFs); DevTools Layout Shift events.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Disciplined <500KB muted video loop with poster is defensible where motion explains the product; essentially never unoptimized above the fold.
- **Better alternatives:** `next/image` with explicit dimensions + `priority`; `<picture>` AVIF/WebP + `srcset`/`sizes`; image CDN; `fetchpriority="high"`; static optimized hero image (fastest); WebM+MP4 replacing GIF; `preload="none"` below the fold; `width`+`height` attrs or CSS `aspect-ratio`.
- **Confidence:** High (LCP mechanism + measurement); Medium (CLS prevalence — contradicted by the one measured dataset)
- **Sources:** web.dev, "Optimize LCP" — web.dev — updated 2025-03; DevNova AI-builder study — devnova.eu — 2026-06; web.dev, "Replace GIFs with videos" + "Optimize CLS" — web.dev; Hashnode — 2026-04.

### I-10 Web font bloat & font-swap handling
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Contextual risk
- **What it looks like:** `@import url('...css2?family=Inter:wght@300;400;500;600;700;800&family=Poppins...')` in global CSS; multiple families/weights loaded site-wide; no subsetting/preload; non-WOFF2 formats; icon fonts instead of SVG; `font-display: swap` with metric-mismatched fallback.
- **Why it fails:** web.dev font best practices: unloaded fonts delay FCP and can delay LCP; Chromium/Firefox block text up to 3s (Safari indefinitely); font swap is a named CLS cause; WOFF2 is ~30% smaller than WOFF; subsetting (`unicode-range`) is the canonical fix. Consistent with the documented Inter-default tell (V-04), though AI-specific weight-count bloat isn't separately measured.
- **Detection signals:** >2 families or >3 weights in requests; gstatic without preconnect; "Preload key requests" naming fonts; CLS localized to text.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Brand typography — one subset variable WOFF2 with preconnect is defensible.
- **Better alternatives:** `system-ui` body stack; single variable font; subset; preload one critical file; `font-display: optional`; SVG over icon fonts.
- **Confidence:** Medium-High (mechanism); Medium (AI-prevalence)
- **Sources:** web.dev, "Font best practices" — web.dev; dev.to, "Why Every AI-Generated Landing Page Looks the Same" — 2026-03.

### I-11 No Error Boundary / white-screen crash (production resilience)
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** Zero `componentDidCatch`/`react-error-boundary` hits; no Sentry/LogRocket import; any runtime JS error unmounts the whole SPA to a blank white page; missing env var → crash after boot; thousands of console.log in the prod bundle.
- **Why it fails:** Hoko's systematic finding: "When a JavaScript error occurs, the entire application crashes with a white screen. No graceful recovery. No fallback UI" — plus no error monitoring and no startup env validation. In a one-shot generated codebase with no tests, the absence of a crash boundary converts any minor runtime error into total product failure.
- **Detection signals:** Grep ErrorBoundary → 0; disable one API endpoint and load → white screen; prod bundle contains console.log; missing env validation at boot.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Throwaway prototypes (the legitimate vibe-coding case).
- **Better alternatives:** Root + per-route ErrorBoundaries with real fallback UI; Sentry; zod env validation at boot; gated logging.
- **Confidence:** High
- **Sources:** Hoko Vibe Code Audit — hoko.team — 2026-02.

### I-12 Network-waterfall neglect: render-blocking third-party scripts, client-side fetch waterfalls, missing cache policy
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **What it looks like:** `<script src="https://cdn.../analytics.js">` in `<head>` without async/defer; GTM + chat + pixel stacked before content; `useEffect(() => { fetch(...).then(setUser) }, [id])` + `if (!user) return <Spinner/>` per route; `await fetchUser(); await fetchProjects(user.id)` chains; React Query installed but unused (Hoko found exactly this); `_next/static/*` served without `max-age=31536000, immutable`.
- **Why it fails:** Each blocking script adds 200–800ms to FCP (Hashnode); even async scripts compete with the LCP image for bandwidth/CPU; behavior-tracking scripts are named INP drivers (2025 Web Almanac: median mobile TBT 1,916ms, +58% YoY, "much of that blocking from third-party JavaScript"). Sequential 200ms fetches compound before any HTML streams; per-mount fetching refires on every navigation with no caching/dedup — spinner flashes everywhere; fingerprinted assets re-fetched every visit punish returning users.
- **Detection signals:** Script tags lacking async/defer in initial HTML; Lighthouse third-party/render-blocking/efficient-cache-policy audits; serial dependent requests in the network waterfall; same endpoint refetched on back-nav; `useEffect`+`fetch(` co-occurrence; `depcheck` output.
- **When it may be appropriate:** Pre-paint A/B testing or consent (accept cost deliberately); one-shot mutations; truly uncacheable real-time data; HTML/API routes should indeed be `no-store` — the failure is absent/uniform policy, not caching itself.
- **Better alternatives:** async/defer; `next/script` strategies; facade (load widget on interaction); Partytown; server-side tagging; parallel fetch + streaming Suspense; TanStack Query/SWR with `staleTime`; route loaders with prefetch-on-intent; per-asset-class cache headers.
- **Confidence:** High (render-blocking); Medium-High (waterfalls/cache — fewer independent sources)
- **Sources:** Hashnode — 2026-04; corewebvitals.io, limiting third-party scripts — 2026-03; Hoko — 2026-02.

### I-13 Insecure-by-default full-stack output (missing RLS, client-exposed keys, client-only auth)
- **Category:** Implementation
- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **What it looks like:** Supabase tables without Row-Level Security policies; anon_key treated as a security boundary; API/service keys in client bundles; authorization checks only in the frontend (API routes trusting `userId` from the request body); `.env` committed then gitignored; missing CSP.
- **Why it fails:** The clearest "consistently harmful" pattern in the program — a CVE exists. CVE-2025-48757 (NVD, published 2025-05-29, CVSS 9.3 Critical, disputed-by-vendor): "An insufficient database Row-Level Security policy in Lovable through 2025-04-15 allows remote unauthenticated attackers to read or write to arbitrary database tables of generated sites." Researcher Matt Palmer found 303 exposed endpoints across 170 projects; an r/vibecoding scan of 1,645 Lovable apps found 170+ fully exposed databases (home addresses, financial data, API keys). Root cause: authentication vs authorization confusion — "the anon_key is public… The ONLY thing stopping unauthorized access is RLS." None of Lovable/v0/Bolt/Figma Make "hands you production code you can ship untouched" (managed-code.com).
- **Detection signals:** Query the Supabase REST endpoint anonymously (`/rest/v1/<table>` with the anon key); static scan for RLS policies and key placement (vibe-code-triage, cso-vibecheck).
- **When it may be appropriate:** Never; a generation-toolchain gap that must be closed before launch.
- **Better alternatives:** Server-side authorization; RLS on every table; secrets server-only; pre-launch security scan; treat AI builders as "strong starting point," not production code.
- **Confidence:** High (core claim — CVE + corroborating scans); Medium (surrounding aggregate stats from vendor-affiliated security blogs — see §5)
- **Sources:** superdesign.dev, Lovable Review (CVE-2025-48757, Palmer research) — 2026-06-14; VibeEval, Lovable Security Report — 2026-06-08; github.com/TreRB/vibe-code-triage — 2026-04-20; managed-code.com — 2026-07-03.

## 4. Cross-Category Findings

Failures do not occur in isolation. The same generation process that samples a visual default also samples the structural, behavioral, and content defaults — which is why single tells are weak evidence but *clusters* are diagnostic. The measured false-positive rate for a single deterministic tell is ~5–10% (Krebs's scanner); cards, rounded corners, and em dashes all predate AI; and rhythm-based copy detection misclassifies 61.3% of human non-native English writing (Stanford, Liang et al. 2023). The following co-occurrence clusters are the report's practical detection units.

**Cluster 1 — The Default Theme Fingerprint (V-01 × V-02 × V-04 × V-08 × V-12 × S-01 × S-03).** Unmodified shadcn/Tailwind tokens + Inter/Geist + indigo→purple gradients + gradient-text hero span + Lucide icons in `bg-primary/10` chips + the monoculture skeleton + the 3-column icon-card grid. Each element is individually defensible; together they are one provenance marker — the "AI default skin." Mirage Security fingerprinted a production platform as AI-built from the stock CSS variables alone; Krebs's scanner, slop-detect, and vibecheck.fail all operationalize exactly this cluster. It resolves to nearly zero false positives because no human art director ships all seven defaults at once.

**Cluster 2 — Gradient Text × Contrast Failure (V-02 × V-03 × A-05).** The decorative layer is where the accessibility failures hide. `bg-clip-text` gradients defeat automated contrast checkers (they evaluate solid backgrounds), glassmorphism puts text over drifting imagery, and both ride on the muted-gray aesthetic — the web's #1 measured WCAG failure (83.9% of home pages, WebAIM), which models reproduce from training data. A page can pass a visual review and an axe scan and still be unreadable. Detection must sample contrast at both gradient endpoints, across scroll positions, and under forced-colors emulation.

**Cluster 3 — Desktop-First Skeleton × Mobile Overflow (S-01 × S-02 × I-04).** Generators are structurally desktop-first: the canonical skeleton is emitted at desktop width with fixed pixel containers, and the mobile render is a mechanical `grid-cols-1` byproduct rather than a redesign. On a phone the failures compound — `text-6xl` headlines wrap one word per line, two side-by-side CTAs overflow, the `h-screen` hero jumps as browser chrome collapses, tilted mockups shrink UI text to unreadable texture, and the duplicated CTA bands and decorative stats blocks consume the highest-attention viewport. WCAG 1.4.10 makes the fixed-width variant an objective hard failure.

**Cluster 4 — Fabricated Social Proof × Fake Avatars × Stock/AI Imagery (C-02 × C-03 × C-04 × V-10).** The trust cluster is fabricated as a unit: invented testimonials arrive with pravatar/GAN faces, invented stats, invented logo walls, and placeholder "Test Customer" screenshots — because the skeleton has slots for proof and the model fills them. This is the highest-stakes cluster: FTC 16 CFR Part 465 (fake reviews, up to $51,744–$53,088/violation), trademark/false-endorsement exposure (logo walls), tribunal precedent (Air Canada), and the Spiegel/PowerReviews finding that uniform 5-star uniformity *reduces* trust anyway. It is also the cluster where fakery is industrialized (fakelogo.com's API; avatar services; courseware teaching "fake logos"). And it visually converges with documented scam-site signatures — over-polished synthetic headshots plus glowing generic praise is the exact pattern consumers are being trained to spot.

**Cluster 5 — Fake UI × Missing States × Console Noise (B-01…B-06 × B-08 × I-01 × I-11).** The "hollow demo" anatomy: controls render but aren't wired (dead buttons, NO-OP search, non-functional tabs), only the happy path exists (no loading/empty/error states), failures are swallowed into `console.log` and fake success toasts, debug scaffolding ships to production, and with no Error Boundary the first runtime error whites-out the whole app. WebGen-Bench quantified the gap (functional pass rates 4.7–29.1% vs design scores up to 56%): these artifacts are optimized to *look* complete, and visual QA is structurally incapable of catching it — screenshot judges even reward broken-but-complex-looking output. Detection requires interaction tests, not screenshots: click every control, submit every form, kill the API, cold-start with zero data.

**Cluster 6 — AI Imagery × Alt-Text Failure × Heavy Assets (V-10 × V-11 × A-06 × I-09).** Generated hero art arrives unoptimized (multi-MB, no `srcset`, no `fetchpriority` — the driver of the measured 6.6s median LCP), without honest alt text (missing, empty-on-informative, or hallucinated by the same pipeline), and bearing no information (decorative filler users ignore per NN/g eye-tracking). One asset class, three failure categories.

**Cluster 7 — Carousel × Accessibility × Performance (S-09 × A-03 × A-09 × A-11).** The auto-rotating carousel is the best-documented compound failure in interface design: ~1% engagement with 84–89% of clicks on slide 1 (Runyon), banner blindness (NN/g), WCAG 2.2.2 pause-control violations, <24px dot targets failing SC 2.5.8, vestibular risk without reduced-motion handling, duplicated tracks spamming screen readers, and late-loading slides contributing layout shift. It persists in AI output despite declining human usage (52%→28% of sites) because it is overrepresented in the tutorial corpus.

**Cluster 8 — Insecure Full-Stack × Dead UI Surface (I-13 × B-03 × B-05).** The Lovable/Bolt pattern: a convincing working-looking frontend wired to a Supabase backend where the *appearance* of an app (auth UI, dashboards, forms) exists but the *authorization layer* does not (no RLS, client-exposed keys). The frontend passes a demo; the backend is open to anonymous read/write (CVE-2025-48757). Form-shaped objects, not working forms — at the data layer.

**The meta-rule: fingerprint clusters, not single tells.** Detection heuristics in the discourse (slop-detect's 27+9 weighted rules, vibecheck.fail's 45+ heuristics, Krebs's 16-pattern scanner) all converge on weighted co-occurrence. "A single pattern doesn't necessarily make a site AI-generated" (Krebs); the tell is density and co-occurrence — "where there is one, there are likely others." Reviewers should score clusters; linters should require ≥2–3 independent signals before flagging; and every flag should map to the underlying harm (misleading, inaccessible, broken, fabricated) rather than to the aesthetic itself — "we grade the vibe, not the toolchain" (vibecheck.fail).

## 5. Research Gaps

Every stream filed an unverified-claims list. Consolidated, by gap type:

**1. Frequency percentages resting on single, non-peer-reviewed datasets.** (a) The "67% of Show HN submissions carry AI fingerprints" figure (chyshkala/slop-detect) does not match Krebs's own published numbers (22% high / 32% medium = 54% with ≥2 patterns; 78% with ≥1) — provenance unclear; this report uses Krebs's numbers. (b) The vibecoded-design-tells Reddit corpus (3.2M posts) uses a keyword lexicon with sarcasm blindness and vocal-minority proxying — trust ordering, not exact percentages (authors' own caveat). (c) Superdesign's style frequencies (e.g., aurora at 0.33%) measure *prompt mentions*, not unasked model output; public output-frequency data per style does not appear to exist. (d) Web Anatomy's 286-section corpus and Navattic's 18%-interactive-demo adoption are single-vendor samples. (e) No systematic peer-reviewed audit of deployed v0/Lovable/Bolt sites exists for accessibility or functionality — the strongest evidence is single-author teardowns (Frontend Masters) and practitioner blogs (Sunpeak, hoko.team's unpublished "avg 97 problems per site").

**2. Vendor-specific and conflict-of-interest claims.** DevNova (sells custom-code studio services), Hoko (sells audits), Hashnode/Flowjam/leadcapture (sell alternatives or services), Mirage Security (sells security), fakelogo.com ("87% of startups use logo walls" — satirical/promotional), slop-detect/vibecheck.fail (monetize anti-slop), AfterBuild Labs ("Bolt 20-component ceiling"), vibe-code-triage ("70% of Lovable apps shipped RLS disabled"), cso-vibecheck ("40–62% vulnerable", "91.5% hallucination flaw"), VibeEval ("18,000 users exposed"). Technical claims from these sources generally align with vendor-neutral primaries; *magnitude* claims should be re-verified before being treated as load-bearing. 925Studios' attributions ("40–62% of AI code has security/design flaws — Second Talent 2026"; "human content outperforms AI 94.12% — Neil Patel") could not be traced to primaries. techdebt.works' headline stats (GitClear 72%, "Stanford 41%", Snyk 4x) likewise.

**3. Meme tells with low measured frequency.** Bento grids (0.1% of complaints), glassmorphism (0.2%), and aurora/mesh backgrounds (0.3%, one rejected as a keyword artifact) register far below their discourse salience — the unslop-ui scanner "deliberately ignores" them as meme artifacts. Em-dash usage is culturally salient but weak evidence per-instance. CLS/missing-image-dimensions is *contradicted* as a dominant AI failure by the one measured dataset (DevNova: 93% good CLS). Dark mode and hidden mobile nav have real NN/g evidence but their AI-specific prevalence is unmeasured.

**4. Mechanism claims that are inferred, not proven.** (a) Whether v0/Lovable/Bolt system prompts explicitly encode the monoculture skeleton — inferred from outputs and stack defaults; no vendor documentation confirms a hardcoded section order. (b) Fountain's and others' "why AI loves it" training-data explanations are hypotheses, not corpus analyses. (c) The RLHF-explains-emoji-bullets hypothesis is the Field Guide author's, not established fact. (d) The Wathan `bg-indigo-500` apology tweet (Aug 2025) is real per multiple secondary citations, but X itself was not fetched; the "demo default → training median" causal chain is well-argued inference. (e) The Artificial Hivemind / verbalized-sampling / Kirk RLHF papers were read via Becker's synthesis and arXiv abstracts, not full texts; the NeurIPS 2025 Best Paper claim comes from Becker. (f) Model-collapse feedback loops (Shumailov et al.) are proven for model quality, not specifically for web-design aesthetics.

**5. Populations and markets beyond Western SaaS landing pages.** Evidence skews to English-language B2B SaaS marketing pages; editorial, portfolio, e-commerce, local-business, and non-Western markets are under-sourced (one juejin.cn article confirms the critique exists in Chinese discourse; nothing measured). Mobile-specific compounding is inferred from desktop evidence. Behavioral sameness (the "functional gap") is argued from observation but not yet quantified the way visual sameness is. No controlled study isolates visual sameness from copy/clarity effects on conversion or brand recall — "generic is worse than ugly" rests on practitioner consensus.

**6. Detection-bias and measurement gaps.** AI-text detection has non-trivial error rates (heavy LLM users ~90% accuracy, casual readers near chance); detectors built on rhythm/perplexity punish non-native writers (61.3% false positives on TOEFL essays). Lint thresholds in the briefs (>3 CTA bands, 3n card grids, ≥90% checkmark parity, proof-to-claim ratios) are derived heuristics, not validated on labeled data. No quantified prevalence exists for div-onClick, missing prefers-reduced-motion, hallucinated alt text, or hydration mismatches in AI-generated code specifically. Whether hydration errors are more common in AI than human code: plausible, unstudied.

**7. Source-access gaps.** Primary threads (HN Algolia, Reddit, X) were partly unreachable; several figures survive only secondhand (Brad Frost's carousel A/B test 1.96% vs 43.03% — used here in its peer-reviewed First Monday form 2.06% vs 40.53%; the "1% carousel click" stat is Notre-Dame-specific and often misattributed to NN/g; NN/g "80% of time above the fold" figures circulating in blogs are dated and were not re-verified; the Microsoft "best model passes WCAG ~1/3 of the time" figure and the Nielsen "AI just violates principles faster" quote could not be traced to primaries and were excluded). The "WebAIM 98% fail contrast" claim circulating in blogs is wrong against WebAIM's own data (83.9% low-contrast text) and was corrected here. FTC 2025 enforcement specifics (Florida supplement suit, $2M settlement) rest on a single secondary source (babl.ai) — the rule itself and penalty schedule are verified. Merriam-Webster's "slop" 2025 Word of the Year is secondary-only. Wired UK's Corporate Memphis article 404'd. The alleged "University of Washington em-dash study" and "Buffer 52M-post analysis" could not be located and were excluded.

**Resolution priorities:** (1) a labeled, re-crawled corpus of AI-built sites with fingerprint rules run against it; (2) interaction-test audits at WebGen-Bench scale for deployed (not benchmark) products; (3) controlled A/B designs holding copy constant while varying surface distinctiveness; (4) calibration of lint thresholds against pages with known outcomes; (5) retrieval of the flagged primaries before any figure above becomes load-bearing.
