# Visual Identity Slop in AI-Generated Websites

*Research report — 2026-07-18*

## 1. Key Findings

This report audits the recurring visual-identity failures of AI-generated websites — the output of v0, Lovable, Bolt, Cursor, and raw LLM codegen, which overwhelmingly ships React + Tailwind + shadcn/ui. It synthesizes nine research briefs; every contrast figure cited was recomputed locally with the official WCAG 2.x luminance formula rather than copied from secondary sources. The mechanism behind the sameness is documented, not speculative: models converge toward the statistical median of their training corpus — Anthropic's own frontend-aesthetics guidance concedes convergence toward generic, "on distribution" outputs — so an unconstrained prompt returns the modal Tailwind-era page. Because that mechanism produces genuine defects alongside harmless-but-ubiquitous defaults, every pattern here is sorted by the taxonomy below.

### The three-tier taxonomy

- **Hard failure** — objectively breaks usability, accessibility, or function: WCAG violations, rendering-performance jank, unreadable or invisible text. A pattern earns the tier on evidence, never on prevalence.
- **Avoid by default** — no functional breakage, but genuine brand-identity harm or a strong unexamined-default signal: the page is interchangeable with thousands of other generated pages and carries no brand information.
- **Contextual risk** — legitimate when the brief supports it (genre conventions, task-focused tools, deliberate brand identities). Oversaturation alone is not harm.

Two rules follow. First, only functional harm justifies a Hard failure; brand sameness caps at Avoid by default. Second, flag the unexamined default rather than the hue, font, or theme itself: purple on Stripe and glass in Apple's and Microsoft's systems are established identities — copying them is the risk; being them is not.

### Ten cross-cutting findings

1. **The canonical AI button fails WCAG AA by three-hundredths of a point.** White text on `bg-indigo-500` (#6366f1) measures 4.47:1 against the required 4.5:1 — WCAG forbids rounding — and the family around it is worse: white on violet-500 is 4.23:1, on blue-500 3.68:1, on the 400-scale badge fills 1.67–2.69:1; the muted-text companion, `text-gray-400` on white, is 2.54:1. Low-contrast text is the web's most common failure class (83.9% of top-one-million home pages; WebAIM Million 2026), and the most-copied button in AI codegen sits just under the line.

2. **Runtime blur is a reproducible scroll-jank machine.** `filter: blur()` and `backdrop-filter` costs scale with radius × area × repaint frequency, with public reproductions three years running: thirdweb's dashboard dropped frames from blurred aurora elements (#703, 2022); shadcn/ui traced modal jank to `backdrop-blur-sm` — "not JavaScript… a CSS rendering/painting problem" (#327, 2023); Foundry VTT measured GPU/FPS hits from a 5px backdrop blur (#10400, 2024). AI heroes stack aurora blur, glass cards, and animated gradients in one viewport, compounding borderline costs into dropped frames on non-premium hardware.

3. **Display headings inherit line-height 1 and collide when they wrap.** Tailwind bakes `line-height: 1` into every size from `text-5xl` (48px) upward, generators emit them verbatim, so wrapped headlines end up with descenders touching ascenders — or clipped under the WCAG 1.4.12 text-spacing override (failure F104). Tight leading suits guaranteed single-line display text; the failure is mechanical — 1.0 leading on text that wraps.

4. **Body copy spans the full container width.** Hero subheads and feature paragraphs regularly render 120+ characters per line inside `max-w-7xl` wrappers, against a 45–75-character typographic consensus (Ruder; Bringhurst), an 80-character WCAG 1.4.8 ceiling, Baymard's large-scale testing, and comprehension experiments (55 cpl beats 100; Dyson & Haselgrove 2001). The fix is a single `max-w-prose` the model almost never emits.

5. **Emoji-as-icon and icon-only controls fail screen readers and recognition alike.** Screen readers announce an emoji's literal Unicode name, not the intended meaning — folded hands reads as "hands pressed together" (Tigwell, Gorman & Menzies, CHI 2020). Icon-only buttons without accessible names announce as bare "button" (axe's `button-name` rule), and per NN/g, tooltips do not rescue them: universal icons are rare, and in one study not a single participant clicked a clock icon used for "history."

6. **Token drift is manufactured even when a design system exists.** Handed a real token file, Claude Design still hardcoded `#F79009` where `--warning-light` existed and invented off-system greens for a chart — roughly 20 detached hex values and 15 off-scale values in one generation (Design Systems Collective, April 2026). Models read token syntax, not the semantic constraints on when a token is correct; drift converts rebrands, dark mode, and contrast fixes into find-and-replace archaeology.

7. **Per-page regeneration violates WCAG Consistent Identification.** AI sessions are scoped per page, so shared components re-emerge with different paddings, hover states, and labels — "Search" here, "Find" there. Identical-function components must be identified consistently under WCAG 2.2 SC 3.2.4 (Level AA; failure F31), and NN/g documents button-position flips causing users to accidentally abort multi-page flows.

8. **Dark-mode surfaces lean on invisible boundaries and dead shadows.** The stock dark card — `bg-white/5` ringed by `border-white/10` — puts a ~1.3:1 hairline where WCAG SC 1.4.11 requires 3:1 whenever that border is the only way to perceive a control's bounds. Its companion, `shadow-xl` on near-black, is a luminance cue with no headroom below the surface; Material and Atlassian both replace it with tonal elevation steps on dark themes.

9. **Font loading and gradient text ship without fallbacks.** A bare `font-family: 'Inter'` with no generic terminator, no `font-display`, and no metric-matched fallback yields up to three seconds of invisible text — indefinitely on Safari — plus swap-induced layout shift. Gradient headline text is worse: `background-clip: text` requires `color: transparent`, so where the clip is unsupported or backgrounds are stripped (forced-colors mode), the headline renders as nothing.

10. **Decorative imagery carries the highest cost and the lowest value.** Full-viewport AI-rendered heroes are the Largest Contentful Paint element while NN/g eye-tracking shows purely decorative "feel-good" images are ignored; missing `alt` attributes leave screen readers reading filenames aloud (WCAG 1.1.1, Level A). One asset class is at once a performance liability, an accessibility failure, and dead weight.

### Calibration: three harm channels, and what did not verify

The findings above are all channel one: functional harm, objectively testable. Most of what the community calls "AI slop" is channel two — differentiation harm to the brand, real but strategic, capping at Avoid by default. Channel three is ecosystem homogenization, documented (the average layout difference between websites, measured across 227,000+ site screenshots, declined over 40% between 2010 and 2019; Goree et al., CHI 2021) but a market-level effect: no individual site fails because of it. Slop discourse conflates these channels; this report does not.

The discipline runs in both directions. Several popular claims failed verification and are excluded from the hard-failure lists: gray-400 text on dark backgrounds passes comfortably (7.93:1 on gray-950, AAA); neon on near-black passes by wide margins (cyan-400 on #0a0a0a is 10.96:1) — the defensible neon claims are light-surface token reuse, halation, and the APCA critique, not contrast; and shadcn/ui's stock theme is accessible (muted-foreground 4.76:1), its sin being recognizability. Conversely, the generic-signal claims — indigo defaults, Lucide monoculture, unDraw's #6C63FF, "the AI look erodes trust and conversion" — rest on community consensus and fingerprint studies (1,590 Show HN pages scored against 16 patterns; a 3.2M-post Reddit corpus whose authors say to trust the ordering, not the percentages), not controlled evidence of user harm. Even the choice-overload folklore behind CTA-count advice failed meta-analytic replication (Scheibehenne, Greifeneder & Todd 2010), and content-farm reading-speed percentages are unquotable.

### Visual hierarchy and spacing: the connective tissue

Hierarchy patterns are folded into this section and §4 rather than given their own chapter, yet they carry the corpus's strongest controlled-study harm evidence: 79% of NN/g's test users scan new pages rather than read them, and a scannable version of the same content measured 47% better usability — 124% when concise and objective as well. Every documented scanning pattern (F, layer-cake, spotted, marking) depends on structural differentiation, exactly what the AI-default page removes: identical section recipes, equal-weight CTAs, uniform `py-24` rhythm, font ratios under 2× (slop-detect's flat-hierarchy rule). The everything-centered hero compounds the loss, spending the page's most-attended region — 57% of viewing time lands above the fold — on the least differentiated composition. Spacing is implicated: gestalt proximity makes spacing *relationships* the user's pre-attentive grouping signal, and whitespace measurably affects reading performance (Lin 2004). §4 details the layout-level failures.

## 2. Color and Palette Failures

Color is where AI codegen's training-data inheritance is most directly visible: the same indigo buttons, purple-to-blue gradients, and cyan-on-black "cyber" palettes recur across tools and prompts because models reproduce the modal palette of the Tailwind-era corpus they were trained on. The patterns below separate two harms that slop discourse routinely conflates — differentiation harm (a brand-strategy failure, classified "avoid by default") and functional harm (a measured WCAG violation, classified "hard failure") — and the section's anchor datum is a computation rather than an opinion: white text on Tailwind `indigo-500` (`#6366f1`), the most-copied button in AI-generated UI, measures 4.47:1 and fails WCAG 2.1 AA for normal text by 0.03. Equally deliberate are the claims this section does *not* make: neon accents on near-black pass luminance contrast by wide margins, and `gray-400` text fails on light surfaces but passes on dark, so both are labeled with their evidence tier instead of being swept into a failure list. Gradient backgrounds, glow effects, glassmorphism, framework-default component themes, and stock typography are treated elsewhere in this report and appear here only where they intersect with palette decisions.

### The Tailwind indigo/violet default palette ("the AI purple")

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** Primary buttons, links, active states, focus rings, and icon accents all drawn from the Tailwind indigo→violet→purple range (`#6366f1`, `#4f46e5`, `#8b5cf6`, `#7c3aed`, `#a78bfa`). Reviewers register "that purple" before reading a word; the page is visually interchangeable with thousands of other AI-generated pages.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** The mechanism is documented, not speculative. Tailwind UI used `bg-indigo-500` as its demo default around 2020; tutorials, templates, and scraped repositories copied it at scale; models trained on that corpus learned "modern web = indigo." Tailwind creator Adam Wathan publicly apologized on X on 2025-08-07 — "I'd like to formally apologize for making every button in Tailwind UI `bg-indigo-500` five years ago, leading to every AI generated UI on earth also being indigo" — a post reported to have passed 1M views (the original post was not retrieved during this research pass; treat it as well-attested community lore rather than a primary source). The largest quantitative study of slop discourse — 3,214,533 Reddit posts across 47 subreddits scanned, 3,033 on-topic comments analyzed — ranks the "default shadcn/Tailwind look" and the "AI purple" gradient as the top two feature-level tells, with "they all look the same" / "screams AI" each appearing in roughly 13% of on-topic posts (the study's authors caution: trust the ordering, not the exact percentages). The harm itself is branding, not usability: an unchosen default hue carries zero brand information, cannot differentiate the product, and functions as a reliable "AI-generated with no art direction" signal to design-literate visitors. Indigo is a fine color when chosen; the failure is the absence of the choice. Note that this palette's most common deployment — white text on a 500-scale fill — is additionally a measured accessibility failure, treated in its own block below.
- **Detection:** In a design: the accent sits in the indigo/violet band with no other brand expression — no custom tokens, no supporting palette, no derivation from anything product-specific. In a codebase: grep `bg-indigo-500`, `text-indigo-600`, `bg-violet-500`, `from-indigo-500`, `to-purple-600`; hex-sweep for `#6366f1 #4f46e5 #4338ca #8b5cf6 #7c3aed #6d28d9 #a78bfa #818cf8`; a `--primary` token set to a stock indigo/violet value with no brand derivation; the default palette used verbatim with no custom `--color-brand` token. Automated scanners catalog exactly these signatures (Kill AI Slop's 32-tell catalog; the unslop-ui `devibe_scan.py` grep scanner; Hallmark's slop-test gates; tastecheck's color audits).
- **Valid exceptions:** A brand whose identity genuinely is indigo/violet — Stripe spent years building equity in a blurple-adjacent palette with high-craft custom execution, and Shopify Polaris and Linear ("Magic Blue" `#5E6AD2`) anchor legitimate indigo identities; developer-tool products where the Linear-style dark+indigo convention is a deliberate genre signal to the audience; products intentionally targeting the Tailwind-dev-tool aesthetic; internal tools where differentiation is out of scope.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Derive the accent from something true about the product, then commit to one chosen dominant hue with a full 50–900 ramp generated as a custom scale (e.g., in OKLCH) rather than shipping stock Tailwind stops. Run interview-style forcing questions ("warm or cool — pick a side") before code generation (tastecheck's `design-system-interview`).

### Purple-to-blue gradients as a brand stand-in ("the tech gradient")

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** Hero backgrounds and especially headline text rendered as indigo→violet→blue (or cyan→fuchsia) gradients; gradient pill buttons; gradient icon fills. Frequently combined with the default palette above.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Listed as tell #1 ("tech gradient — blue/violet/indigo glossy gradient on everything") in the Claude Design slop diagnostic, explicitly forbidden in AI-output style guides (visual-explainer: "Gradient text on headings … screams AI-generated"), named verbatim as the `#6366f1 → #a855f7` diagonal in Kill AI Slop's 32-tell catalog, and ranked the #2 feature-level tell in the Reddit-corpus study. Compositionally, a full-bleed gradient is a texture decision the model made instead of the designer: it adds visual noise behind type, and gradient-clipped headline text (`background-clip: text`) makes the most important words on the page lower-contrast at one end of the ramp — the indigo→violet midpoint (~`#8661f8`) measures 4.13:1 against white, failing AA — and un-renderable when backgrounds are dropped, as forced-colors / high-contrast modes remove background images.
- **Detection:** In a design: gradient-filled headlines, gradient hero washes in the purple/blue band, gradient pill buttons. In a codebase: `bg-gradient-to-r from-indigo-500 via-purple-500 to-pink-500`; the regex `bg-gradient-to-[trlb]{1,2}\s+from-(indigo|purple|violet|blue|pink)-[4-6]00`; raw CSS such as `linear-gradient(135deg, #667eea, #764ba2)`; gradient text via `background: linear-gradient(...)` + `-webkit-background-clip: text` + `color: transparent`, or Tailwind `bg-clip-text text-transparent` on an `<h1>`. (Overlapping `radial-gradient` "aurora" background blobs are cataloged in the backgrounds section.)
- **Valid exceptions:** Brands with a real gradient identity (Instagram, Firefox; Stripe's gradient aesthetic, which was licensed-type, hand-built-illustration custom work, not a default); data-viz ramps encoding magnitude; briefs explicitly calling for expressive campaign pages.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A flat chosen accent plus a strong neutral ramp. If a gradient is wanted, use a subtle two-stop ramp within one hue family at low saturation, on decorative (non-text) surfaces only; if text ever sits on a gradient, test contrast against the gradient midpoint, not the ends.

### Neon accents on near-black backgrounds

- **Classification:** Contextual risk
- **Visible symptoms:** "Cyberpunk dashboard" dark mode: `#050505`–`#0a0a0a` surfaces with neon cyan (`#00e5ff`, `#22d3ee`), neon green (`#00ff9d`, `#39ff14`), hot pink/magenta accents, colored glow shadows (`box-shadow: 0 0 20px <accent>`), and multi-blob radial "neon haze." Common in AI-generated dev-tool, crypto, gaming, and "futuristic AI product" pages.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** The evidence cuts both ways, so precision matters.
  - **Contrast is usually not the problem on dark backgrounds.** Computed WCAG 2.x ratios: cyan-400 `#22d3ee` on `#0a0a0a` = 10.96:1 (passes AAA); `#00e5ff` on `#050505` = 13.25:1; `#00ff9d` on `#050505` = 15.33:1. Bright saturated accents are *light* colors, so luminance contrast against near-black is high. Claims that neon-on-dark "fails WCAG" are usually numerically wrong, and this report does not make them.
  - **The real failures are elsewhere:** (a) the same neon accent tokens reused on light surfaces fail catastrophically — cyan-500 `#06b6d4` on white = 2.43:1, amber-400 `#fbbf24` on white = 1.67:1 — and AI codegen frequently shares one accent token across themes; (b) extreme saturation produces halation/bloom on dark backgrounds and vibrates against adjacent saturated hues (the classic human-factors warning against using high-chroma, spectrally extreme colors simultaneously); (c) glow shadows and radial haze are explicit AI-slop tells per the blocklists; (d) WCAG 2.x luminance math is hue-blind and criticized for overestimating some saturated pairs (the APCA/WCAG3 critique), so a numeric pass is not the whole story for small neon text.
  - **Readability evidence:** NN/g's literature review found that for normal-vision users, light mode wins visual-acuity and proofreading tasks across ages, with the advantage growing as type gets smaller; a 2026 Springer pilot (n=28) replicated the tradeoff (light mode: faster reading, higher perceived readability, lower cognitive workload; dark mode: greater comfort, less reported eye strain). NN/g's recommendation: do not default a general-audience site to dark; offer a toggle. Defaulting a marketing page full of body copy to neon-on-black is therefore a measurable readability liability, not merely a cliché.
  - **Genericness:** cyan-on-black "hacker" styling is itself an AI default for anything prompted "futuristic / AI / dashboard" — a second cliché after purple.
- **Detection:** In a design: near-black surfaces with maximally saturated cyan/green/magenta accents and visible bloom. In a codebase: near-black backgrounds (`#000`–`#111827`) plus accents `#00e5ff #22d3ee #06b6d4 #00ff9d #39ff14 #d946ef #f472b6`; `box-shadow: 0 0 Npx` with a *colored* shadow; multiple `radial-gradient` glow layers; accent tokens shared verbatim between light and dark themes.
- **Valid exceptions:** Gaming/esports, synthwave/cyberpunk, nightlife/music, and security/CTF tooling where the aesthetic is the product (a genre contract the audience reads as authenticity); media/entertainment products used in dark rooms — Spotify, Netflix, Prime Video, and Adobe apps default to dark to enhance content perception and reduce glare; developer tools and IDEs where dark UI is occupational convention; glanceable monitoring dashboards; neon restricted to large decorative elements.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Dark surfaces with one desaturated accent; deep desaturated neutrals (petrol, forest, warm charcoal) instead of pure black when the brief wants "moody"; off-white text (`#e5e5e5`, not `#fff`); glow replaced by real elevation; neon reserved for at most one semantic role; separate light-theme accent values verified at 4.5:1; both modes shipped with a persistent toggle.

### Over-saturated accents failing WCAG contrast (the canonical AI button)

- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **Visible symptoms:** White text on mid-saturation accent buttons that looks "fine" but measures under 4.5:1; pastel accent text (`violet-400`, `indigo-400`) on white; green "success"/accent text on white; colored icons and borders under 3:1.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Objectively, with numbers. All ratios below were recomputed locally with the official WCAG 2.x relative-luminance formula; WCAG explicitly says not to round, so 4.47 fails.

  | Combination | Ratio | WCAG 2.1 AA result |
  |---|---|---|
  | White text on `bg-indigo-500 #6366f1` (the canonical AI button) | 4.47:1 | **Fails AA for normal text** (passes only as large text / UI component at 3:1) |
  | White text on `bg-violet-500 #8b5cf6` | 4.23:1 | Fails AA normal text |
  | White text on `bg-purple-500 #a855f7` | 3.96:1 | Fails AA normal text |
  | White text on `bg-blue-500 #3b82f6` | 3.68:1 | Fails AA normal text |
  | `text-violet-400 #a78bfa` on white | 2.72:1 | Fails AA |
  | `text-indigo-400 #818cf8` on white | 2.98:1 | Fails AA |
  | `text-green-500 #22c55e` on white | 2.28:1 | Fails AA |
  | `text-cyan-500 #06b6d4` on white | 2.43:1 | Fails AA |
  | `text-red-500 #ef4444` on white | 3.76:1 | Large text only |
  | `text-gray-500 #6b7280` on white | 4.83:1 | Passes AA (contrast case) |

  Low-contrast text is the most common WCAG failure class on the real web: 94.8% of home pages have detectable WCAG 2 failures (WebAIM Million 2025), and low-contrast text alone appears on 83.9% of the top 1M home pages (WebAIM Million, February 2026 data). The irony is concrete: the single most-copied button in AI codegen sits *just* under the AA line for normal-size text, and it passes only as "large text" (≥18pt, or 14pt bold) — which many button labels are not.
- **Detection:** In a design: run any contrast checker over the primary button and accent text; treat "looks fine" as unmeasured, not passing. In a codebase: `text-white` paired with `bg-{indigo,violet,purple,blue}-{400,500}`; `text-{color}-400` on light surfaces; `border-{accent}-300` as the sole input boundary (SC 1.4.11 requires 3:1 for UI components and graphical objects). Runtime axe/Lighthouse contrast audits catch these on computed styles; verify per token, per theme.
- **Valid exceptions:** None for normal text — WCAG AA is the floor. Large text has a 3:1 threshold; decorative and inactive elements are exempt. If a brand insists on the stock 500-scale fill, near-black text on `indigo-500` measures 4.70:1 and passes.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Shift accents one step darker for text and action roles — `indigo-600 #4f46e5` with white measures ~6:1 (verify per case); keep saturated 400-level hues for large decorative shapes only; re-test every token in both themes.

### White text on 400-scale accent fills

- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **Visible symptoms:** Pastel badges and pills (`bg-emerald-400`, `bg-amber-400`) carrying white microcopy; cyan `bg-sky-400` buttons with white labels; pastel chips with white text.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Computed white-on-`sky-400` (`#38bdf8`) = 2.14:1; `emerald-400` (`#34d399`) = 1.92:1; `rose-400` (`#fb7185`) = 2.69:1; `amber-400` (`#fbbf24`) = 1.67:1. All fail even the 3:1 large-text threshold — a larger-margin violation than the 500-scale buttons above. Models pick the 400 scale because it reads "vivid" in the swatch; nobody checked the foreground.
- **Detection:** In a design: white labels on pastel fills anywhere in badges, pills, chips, or buttons. In a codebase: `bg-(sky|emerald|rose|amber|lime|cyan)-(300|400)` co-occurring with `text-white`; runtime axe/Lighthouse contrast rules catch it on computed styles.
- **Valid exceptions:** Dark text on the same fills (black on `amber-400` ≈ 10.9:1, passes AAA); decorative non-text chips.
- **Stronger alternatives:** 600/700-scale fills with white text, or 100-scale fills with 900-scale text (Tailwind's own badge convention).
- **Supporting sources:**
  - WCAG 2.1 (W3C Recommendation), SC 1.4.3 — https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG21/
  - Contrast ratios computed locally during the research pass (official sRGB relative-luminance formula).
- **Confidence:** High — normative standard plus direct computation.

### Light-gray "muted" text misuse (placeholder gray as body gray)

- **Classification:** Contextual risk — Hard failure in the light-mode form; the dark-mode form passes and must not be flagged.
- **Visible symptoms:** Secondary copy in pale gray disappearing on white; `text-gray-400` paragraphs inside light sections; placeholder-gray styling leaked onto real body copy.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** It fails only where it fails. Computed `gray-400` (`#9ca3af`) on white = 2.54:1, FAIL; `gray-300` (`#d1d5db`) on white = 1.47:1, FAIL; `text-black/50` on white (~`#808080`) = 3.95:1, FAIL for normal text. These come from models applying dark-mode muted conventions to light surfaces. The counter-evidence is what keeps this section honest: `gray-400` on dark backgrounds passes comfortably — on `gray-950` (`#030712`) = 7.93:1 (AAA); on `gray-900` (`#111827`) = 6.99:1; on `slate-900` = 7.03:1; `text-white/60` on `slate-900` = 6.99:1, PASS. "Gray text on dark" is not a contrast failure, and flagging it as one would be a false positive.
- **Detection:** In a design: squint-test secondary copy on light sections; measure rather than eyeball. In a codebase: grep `text-gray-400|text-slate-400` and check the *computed* background behind each instance (light section = violation); `text-black/(40|50|60)` on white; placeholder classes leaked onto real `<p>` copy.
- **Valid exceptions:** True placeholders; disabled states (formally exempt from SC 1.4.3); large text at 3:1 or better; muted gray text on dark surfaces, per the computed passes above.
- **Stronger alternatives:** `gray-500` on white = 4.83:1 (passes AA); `gray-600` for a comfortable margin; on dark surfaces, verified `gray-400`.
- **Supporting sources:**
  - WCAG 2.1 (W3C Recommendation), SC 1.4.3 — https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG21/
  - Contrast ratios computed locally during the research pass (official sRGB relative-luminance formula; alpha-blended colors composited onto the stated background before measuring).
- **Confidence:** High — computed; the dark-passes/light-fails nuance is the detection research's most defensible contribution.

### Single-accent monoculture (one hue does everything)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** One hue — usually the default-palette indigo — simultaneously serves as primary button fill, link color, icon color, active nav state, focus ring, badge fill, chart series, highlight/selection, and decorative blob. Nothing else on the page has color.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** When everything is the accent, nothing is. Color can no longer encode hierarchy, state, or category, so the page falls back to size and weight alone — a real compositional loss, not a taste judgment. Human-factors guidance converges on limited, consistently coded palettes (Marcus: at most 3–7 meaningful colors; same color = related things). Zietz's "color is operational" point matters here: users interpret any color difference as semantic, so a single accent spread across clickable and decorative elements teaches wrong affordances — colored-but-inert icons read as links. The critique is about *commitment*, not monochrome per se: one dominant hue is fine when neutrals, semantic colors, and elevation still carry their own jobs (tastecheck's forcing question: "One dominant color. Not five pastels. Not indigo→violet.").
- **Detection:** In a design: count distinct hues; if every colored element is the same hue, color is doing no informational work. In a codebase: one `--primary` token consumed by `button, a, svg, .badge, .highlight, ::selection, :focus-visible`; all icons `text-indigo-500`; charts whose series are all shades of the accent.
- **Valid exceptions:** Deliberate monochrome identities (brutalist or luxury briefs) — but these use value and weight contrast inside one hue, not one flat 500-level token everywhere.
- **Stronger alternatives:** The standard token split: a neutral ramp (surfaces/text/borders) + one brand accent (actions) + a semantic set (success/warning/error/info, used only semantically) + optional 1–2 supporting hues for data-viz. Reserve the accent for interactive elements so color regains affordance meaning.
- **Supporting sources:**
  - LukeW, "Use and Misuse of color" (Marcus commandments; Zietz: more design errors committed with color than any other UI aspect; "color is operational") — https://www.lukew.com/ff/entry.asp?11
  - KyaniteLabs/tastecheck README, GitHub — https://github.com/KyaniteLabs/tastecheck (2026-06-05)
  - Claude Design slop diagnostic ("Accent rail — decoration pretending to be organization"), Hermes Agent docs — https://hermes-agent.nousresearch.com/docs/user-guide/skills/bundled/creative/creative-claude-design
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — grounded in long-standing human-factors guidance and token-system practice; the AI-specific frequency claim is community observation.

### Absence of a neutral ramp (a two-color palette)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** The palette is one accent + `#000000` + `#ffffff` and nothing between. Body text is pure black or the accent; dark mode is pure black with pure white text; "secondary" text is just primary text at `opacity-60`; borders are a lone `gray-200`. No 50–900 neutral steps exist in the token set.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Neutrals do the actual work of a UI — surfaces, elevation, dividers, secondary text, disabled states. Without a ramp the design has two value levels, hierarchy is crude, and state styling (hover/disabled/selected) has nowhere to go; opacity-hacked grays wash out differently per background and frequently fail contrast (see the muted-text block above). Pure black-on-white (or its inverse) is the maximum 21:1 contrast (computed), harsh for long reading and prone to smearing/halation on OLED. Design-system practice — Refactoring UI's color chapter, SAP Fiori, every major token system — treats a full neutral scale, often slightly tinted rather than pure gray, as a prerequisite. For AI output specifically, a two-color palette is evidence that no color system was designed at all.
- **Detection:** In a design: only two value levels across the whole page; hover and disabled states that merely fade. In a codebase: fewer than ~6 neutral steps between near-black and near-white in tokens; literals `#000` / `rgb(0,0,0)` as page background; `text-black`/`text-white` with `opacity-*` as the entire secondary-text strategy; no `gray|slate|zinc|neutral|stone` scale in the Tailwind config.
- **Valid exceptions:** True high-contrast themes (accessibility modes, e-ink, print).
- **Stronger alternatives:** Adopt or derive a 10-step neutral ramp (Tailwind `slate`/`zinc` are fine); off-white `#fafafa` backgrounds and `#171717`–`#1a1a1a` dark surfaces; secondary text at `gray-600` on light, checked `gray-400` on dark; tint neutrals 2–5% toward the brand hue.
- **Supporting sources:**
  - Wathan & Schoger, *Refactoring UI* (2018) — define color shades up front; grays and semantic colors as system primitives (as cited in the UI-design thesis at kc.umn.ac.id) — https://kc.umn.ac.id/id/eprint/35939/3/BAB_II.pdf
  - Apple *Human Interface Guidelines* (1987) — begin design in black and white; limit and structure color use (historical but foundational) — https://andymatuschak.org/files/papers/Apple Human Interface Guidelines 1987.pdf
  - Local computation during the research pass: white-on-black = 21.00:1; `gray-400`-on-white = 2.54:1.
- **Confidence:** Medium — universal design-system consensus and well-understood ergonomics, but no formal study ties the *absence of a ramp* to measured user harm; it is a craft standard.

### Semantic color misuse (red/green as decoration)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default — escalates to Hard failure when decorative red/green sits next to real status styling and the two become indistinguishable.
- **Visible symptoms:** Green used for non-success decoration ("eco" vibes, random badges, brand accent); red used for non-error, non-destructive emphasis; success/error tokens reused as chart categories or marketing accents in a product that also has real status states.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Color conventions are learned and near-universal in UI: red = stop/error/destructive, yellow = warning, green = go/success (Apple HIG 1987; SAP Fiori: semantic hues "preferably only used when conveying the meaning of a value state"). Users interpret any red or green element as status; decorative use creates false alarms and desensitizes users to real ones. It also collides with WCAG 1.4.1 (color must not be the sole carrier of meaning): AI codegen typically adds no icon or label redundancy, so decorative-vs-semantic ambiguity is an accessibility gap as well. Cultural caveat from HIG: in finance red = loss; in China red = gains/celebration — "misuse" is convention-relative, which strengthens the case for *reserving* the hues rather than decorating with them.
- **Detection:** In a design: any red or green element that is not communicating status. In a codebase: `bg-green-500` / `text-red-500` on elements with no status role; `--success` / `--error` values aliased to brand tokens or used in marketing sections; red and green as the two series colors in charts (also a red-green color-vision-deficiency problem, affecting ~8% of men).
- **Valid exceptions:** Brands whose identity is red (YouTube) or green (Spotify) — with the burden that in-product error/success styling must then be disambiguated via icons, copy, and different value/saturation; finance UIs following domain conventions; holiday themes.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Reserve green/red/amber for status only; build brand palettes from hues without strong UI semantics (teal, orange, blue, warm neutrals); always pair status color with icon + text (WCAG 1.4.1 redundancy).
- **Supporting sources:**
  - Apple *Human Interface Guidelines* (1987), "Standard uses of color" — https://andymatuschak.org/files/papers/Apple Human Interface Guidelines 1987.pdf
  - SAP Fiori semantic-colors guidance as summarized in the kc.umn.ac.id UI-design thesis (figure credited to Wathan & Schoger 2018) — https://kc.umn.ac.id/id/eprint/35939/3/BAB_II.pdf
  - LukeW, "Use and Misuse of color" — https://www.lukew.com/ff/entry.asp?11
  - W3C, WCAG 2.1 Understanding SC 1.4.1 Use of Color — https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/use-of-color.html
- **Confidence:** Medium-High — the convention and rationale are documented across decades of HIG and design-system literature; the claim that AI codegen does this *frequently* is community observation, not measured.

## 3. Typography Failures

Typography is the highest-coverage brand surface on any web page, and it is where AI-generated sites are most uniform: the same font, the same flat scale, the same centered hero, reproduced from the Tailwind / shadcn/ui / Next.js starter templates that dominate model training data. The eleven patterns below fall into two distinct harm channels — brand indistinctness (the site is interchangeable with every other AI-built site) and, in a minority of cases, measurable readability and accessibility damage — and each pattern states which channel it carries. An honesty note applies to the whole section: most typography "slop" is not a usability bug, and several statistics that circulate in slop discourse (reading-speed percentages for all-caps and centered text) failed verification during research and are excluded rather than repeated.

### Default Font Monoculture (Inter / Geist / Roboto / Space Grotesk)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** Every AI-generated SaaS page, landing site, and dashboard renders in the same neutral neo-grotesque sans. Inter arrives via `next/font/google` or a Google Fonts link; Geist (Vercel's typeface) appears on anything scaffolded from Next.js 15+ or v0; Roboto shows up in Material-flavored output; Space Grotesk is the tell that the model was reaching for an "edgy startup" register. Heading, body, label, and button are all cut from the same family.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** The harm is branding, not usability — Inter and Geist are objectively competent screen fonts, and the failure is the *defaulting*, not the fonts. Community discourse is unusually consistent. Alan West's autopsy of the AI look names "Inter for everything, with the occasional font-bold for headings... technically readable and entirely forgettable"; The Uncanny Valley of AI Frontends observes that AI "only knows two fonts: Inter and Geist... that's probably why they're so embedded in the training data"; MindStudio's anti-slop guide lists "Inter or Geist as the default sans-serif" among the top visual signatures of AI output; and an anti-slop "premium web design" skill for Claude Code maintains an explicit font blacklist (Inter, Poppins, Montserrat, Raleway, Space Grotesk, Outfit). The mechanism is documented rather than mystical: shadcn-ecosystem starters settled on Inter early (GitHub issue kirimase#178, "Set Inter as the base font for Shadcn"; the standard Inter import in shadcn-ui discussion #3083), and Next.js has progressively re-defaulted its own surfaces to Geist (the 16.2 release notes record even ImageResponse's default font moving from Noto Sans to Geist Sans). Models average over that corpus. The visibility math compounds it: the HTTP Archive Web Almanac 2024 measured web fonts on roughly 87% of sites and Google Fonts on roughly 57% of desktop pages, so any single popular Google font is enormously exposed; one branding playbook counted 414 billion Google Fonts requests for Inter in the year ending May 2025. An unmodified workhorse font, as the Logggos SaaS logo report put it, "communicates that the brand did not invest in its identity." The brand-voice application of this same evidence — a default-font wordmark as a logo-less identity — is treated in §6's "Default Font Stack as Brand Voice" pattern (see also).
- **Detection:** Code: `import { Inter } from 'next/font/google'`; `const inter = Inter({ subsets: ['latin'] })`; `geist/font/sans` or `GeistSans.className`; Google Fonts links or `@import` URLs containing only `family=Inter`, `family=Roboto`, or `family=Space+Grotesk`. CSS: `--font-sans: 'Inter', ...` or `font-family: 'Inter', sans-serif` as the *only* family in the project, with no display, serif, or mono accent face anywhere. Rendered: tall x-height and neutral apertures everywhere; Geist reads slightly narrower and more geometric than Inter.
- **Valid exceptions:** Dense developer tools, dashboards, and internal admin UIs where neutrality and small-size legibility are the actual brief — Inter was literally designed for this (Rasmus Andersson built it for user interfaces, with a tall x-height for small-size legibility; GitHub and Mozilla use it), and practitioners defend it as "almost boring in its perfection, which is precisely what you want for user interfaces." Products explicitly adopting the Vercel/shadcn aesthetic as brand identity; Inter-as-body under a distinctive display face, which is a defensible pairing; and Inter-as-deliberate-choice with a documented decision — the Raycast middle path, which ships Inter site-wide but enables `font-feature-settings: "ss03"`, making the single-story "g" a signature detail rather than generic Inter. Tellingly, Anthropic's own frontend-aesthetics prompt instructs the model to avoid Inter/Roboto/Arial *for marketing-style outputs* — vendor-level confirmation that this is a defaults problem, not a font-quality problem.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Choose type from the brand brief first. Community anti-slop guidance converges on a three-voice system: a characterful display/serif for headings (Fraunces, DM Serif Display, Instrument Serif, Playfair), a neutral-but-not-default body (IBM Plex Sans, Manrope, Work Sans, Instrument Sans, Inter Tight), and a monospace accent for labels and code (JetBrains Mono, IBM Plex Mono). System font stacks (`system-ui`) are a legitimate zero-latency anti-monoculture choice for body text. If staying in Inter on a utility product, differentiate through scale, weight contrast, tracking, and OpenType features — a decision record, not a default.

- **Confidence:** High that the monoculture exists and is widely recognized (multiple independent 2024–2026 sources plus starter-template provenance); Medium that it constitutes harm — the harm is indistinctness, and reasonable practitioners defend Inter as correct-for-purpose on UI surfaces. Space Grotesk's membership in the slop set rests on one explicit blacklist plus general trend sentiment: medium confidence.

### Single Typeface, No Type Voice (No Pairing, No Hierarchy of Voices)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** One family — usually Inter — set at 400/500/700 across the entire page: headings, body, labels, buttons, code. No display face, no serif, no monospace accent, no italic voice. Hierarchy is carried by size and weight alone, so a code snippet, a quotation, and a navigation label are all rendered in the same cut.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Typeface choice is the cheapest, highest-leverage brand signal a site has; skipping the pairing decision means skipping the brand decision. Material Design's own guidance limits interfaces to two families ("one for headings, one for body") precisely because a single flat voice reads as undifferentiated, and dev.to's AI-look autopsy names this exact failure mode. There is a functional edge as well: code snippets, data labels, and quotations genuinely benefit from different voices (mono, serif) — a single family forces fake distinctions via color or weight, which are weaker cues and frequently lower-contrast ones.
- **Detection:** One `@font-face` or `next/font` import in the repository; grep finds no `font-serif`, `font-mono`, or second `font-family` declaration; the Tailwind config defines only `fontFamily.sans`. On the rendered page, buttons, H1s, code, and body are all in the same typeface.
- **Valid exceptions:** Brutalist or strictly systematic briefs where a single grotesque *is* the concept (Vercel's own marketing); extremely dense enterprise UIs where a second family adds noise; performance-constrained pages deliberately shipping one variable font.
- **Stronger alternatives:** The classic two-voice system — a display face with personality plus a neutral body face, with weights within each family doing the rest. If staying single-family, exploit the family's full width: optical sizes, a true display cut (Inter Tight/Display versus Inter), italics, small caps, and OpenType features.
- **Supporting sources:** "How to fix the 'AI-generated' look in your frontend" — https://dev.to/alanwest/how-to-fix-the-ai-generated-look-in-your-frontend-1ahh, 2026-05-18; "Premium Web Design — Skills for Claude Code" — https://www.aitmpl.com/component/skills/creative-design/premium-web-design, accessed 2026; "Building a typography system? Make sure you've covered the minimum" — https://www.designsystemscollective.com/building-a-typography-system-make-sure-youve-covered-the-minimum-06fcbf8b12f0, 2025-06-16.
- **Confidence:** High — consistent community consensus plus mainstream design-system practice; the harm is branding and expressiveness, documented as such.

### Tight Leading on Display Text (line-height: 1 on Multi-Line Headings)

- **Classification:** Hard failure when the heading wraps to two or more lines (collision/clipping risk, and it breaks under user spacing overrides); Contextual risk for guaranteed single-line display text
- **Visible symptoms:** Large hero headings where the descenders of line one visually touch the ascenders of line two; clipped or overlapping text when users zoom, substitute fonts, or apply accessibility spacing overrides.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** This failure is mechanical and reproducible. Tailwind's default scale ships `line-height: 1` baked into every size from `text-5xl` (48px) upward (verified in the Tailwind documentation). AI tools emit `text-5xl md:text-7xl` hero headings verbatim and rarely add a `/` line-height modifier, so multi-line hero copy inherits exactly 1.0 leading. WCAG SC 1.4.12 requires content to survive a user override of line height to 1.5× without loss of content or functionality — failure F104 is literally "clipped or overlapped content when text spacing is adjusted" — and fixed-height hero containers plus 1.0 leading fail this routinely. Design-system guidance agrees on the direction: USWDS allows headings of one or two lines between 1.0 and 1.35 but requires more for anything longer; the EC Component Library sets headings at 1.2.
- **Detection:** Tailwind classes `text-5xl` through `text-9xl`, or `leading-none`/`leading-tight`, on elements whose text wraps at common viewports (test at 1280px and 375px); CSS `line-height: 1` on `font-size >= 36px`; a computed-style check for line-height/font-size ratios below 1.1 on multi-line text. Accessibility test: apply the WCAG 1.4.12 bookmarklet metrics (line-height 1.5, letter-spacing 0.12em, word-spacing 0.16em) and look for clipping.
- **Valid exceptions:** Single-line display headlines, big numerals and stat callouts, logotype-style hero words — tight leading (even 0.95–1.05) is correct practice there, because typographers *tighten* leading as size increases. Tim Brown sets his 190px display heading at `line-height: 1` deliberately; that is the examined version of the same value.
- **Stronger alternatives:** `text-5xl/tight` through `leading-[1.05]`–`leading-[1.15]` on hero headings; `text-wrap: balance` to control where lines break; fluid sizes via `clamp()` with matching fluid leading.
- **Supporting sources:** Tailwind CSS documentation, Font Size — https://tailwindcss.com/docs/font-size, accessed 2026; WCAG Understanding SC 1.4.12 Text Spacing (incl. failure F104) — https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/text-spacing.html, accessed 2026; USWDS Typography — https://designsystem.digital.gov/components/typography/, accessed 2026; EC Component Library Typography guidelines — https://ec.europa.eu/component-library/v3.13.0/ec/guidelines/typography/, accessed 2026; Tim Brown, "More Meaningful Typography" — https://alistapart.com/article/more-meaningful-typography/, 2011-04-21.
- **Confidence:** High — the Tailwind default and the WCAG failure mode are both documented facts; the failure is mechanical and reproducible.

### One Line-Height for Everything (Uniform Leading Across Sizes)

- **Classification:** Contextual risk
- **Visible symptoms:** Body text and 64px headlines share the same multiplier — usually whatever Tailwind bundled, or a blanket `line-height: 1.5` — so big headings look airy and disconnected, or small text looks cramped. The inverse failure also appears: `leading-relaxed`/`leading-loose` (1.625/2) sprayed onto large headings by "generous spacing" prompting.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Leading must be size-dependent: larger type needs proportionally less leading (display roughly 1.0–1.3), body text needs more (roughly 1.4–1.6). Butterick puts optimal line spacing for most text at 120–145% of point size; WCAG 1.4.12's override metric (1.5×) and USWDS line-height tokens (1.0 for buttons, 1.5 or more for extended reading) encode the same gradient. AI output almost never modulates leading per size — it inherits whatever Tailwind bundled or sets one global value — which is a reliable tell that no typographic decision was made. The harm when violated is real but moderate, so this sits at contextual risk rather than hard failure.
- **Detection:** A single line-height value in global CSS applied to `body` and inherited by all headings; computed line-height/font-size ratios that do not decrease as font-size increases (a 72px heading at ratio 1.6 yields 115px of leading — visibly broken).
- **Valid exceptions:** A blanket 1.5 on body copy only is fine; single-size pages (docs, blogs) barely need modulation.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A leading scale paired to the type scale: display 1.0–1.15, headings 1.1–1.3, body 1.45–1.6, captions/UI 1.2–1.4. In Tailwind, set per-size line-heights in the theme (e.g., `--text-5xl--line-height`) instead of inheriting defaults.
- **Supporting sources:** Butterick, "Line spacing" — https://practicaltypography.com/line-spacing.html, accessed 2026; WCAG Understanding SC 1.4.12 — https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/text-spacing.html, accessed 2026; USWDS Typography (line-height tokens) — https://designsystem.digital.gov/components/typography/, accessed 2026; "Type Scale Guide" — https://www.pacgie.com/guides/what-is-type-scale, 2025-12-17.
- **Confidence:** High as best practice (multiple independent design systems plus WCAG agree); the harm when violated is moderate, hence contextual risk.

### Letter-Spacing Errors (Default Tracking on Display Type; Tight Tracking on Small Text)

- **Classification:** Contextual risk (Hard failure only in the extreme: negative tracking applied to small or body text, or tracking so tight that letters collide)
- **Visible symptoms:** Three recurring shapes: (a) huge headings set with untouched, font-default spacing — slightly loose, unpolished; (b) the opposite AI tic, `tracking-tight`/`tracking-tighter` applied indiscriminately, including to 12–14px UI text and paragraphs; (c) all-caps labels set with zero letter-spacing, reading as dense bricks.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Letter-spacing is size-dependent, and the correct direction flips with size — precisely what default-trained output gets wrong. Butterick: capital letters set together need +5–12% letterspacing (`letter-spacing: 0.05em–0.12em`); lowercase body text needs none; typographers *add* spacing below roughly 9px and *remove* it at headline sizes. WCAG 1.4.12 requires content to survive a +0.12em letter-spacing override, and negatively tracked small text is the most fragile configuration under that override. Anti-slop guidance independently prescribes the correct display-side move: negative letter-spacing of −0.03em to −0.06em on large headlines only.
- **Detection:** Tailwind `tracking-tight`/`tracking-tighter` on elements below 16px or on paragraphs; `uppercase` without an accompanying `tracking-*` class; big headings (`text-5xl` and up) with no tracking class at all (missed polish rather than an error). In CSS: negative `letter-spacing` values on `font-size < 16px`.
- **Valid exceptions:** Negative tracking on display type at roughly 36px and above is correct practice; `tracking-widest` on short uppercase eyebrows is correct practice.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Size-conditional tracking: −0.02em to −0.04em for display, 0 for body, +0.05–0.12em for all-caps/small-caps and tiny text. In Tailwind: pair every `uppercase` with `tracking-wider`/`tracking-widest`; reserve `tracking-tight*` for `text-4xl` and up.
- **Supporting sources:** Butterick, "Letterspacing" — https://practicaltypography.com/letterspacing.html, accessed 2026; Butterick, "All caps" — https://practicaltypography.com/all-caps.html, accessed 2026; WCAG Understanding SC 1.4.12 — https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/text-spacing.html, accessed 2026; "Premium Web Design — Skills for Claude Code" — https://www.aitmpl.com/component/skills/creative-design/premium-web-design, accessed 2026.
- **Confidence:** High on the mechanics (Butterick, WCAG, and independent guides agree on direction); Medium on severity — most instances are polish-level, not blocking.

### Weight Misuse (the 400/500/700 Trinity, or Ultra-Bold Everything)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** Two signature looks. First, every heading at `font-extrabold`/`font-bold` (700–800) at every level, so H1/H2/H3 differ only in size. Second, pages where the only weights used are 400 for body, 500 for "medium" secondary text, and 700 for headings, with 300/600/800 untouched and hierarchy consequently flat. The shadcn-ecosystem heading signature — `text-4xl font-extrabold tracking-tight lg:text-5xl` — is so prevalent in training data that models reproduce it for every heading tier.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Weight is a hierarchy dimension that AI output wastes. When everything is bold, nothing is; emphasis then requires still-larger sizes, which cascades into oversized, shouty pages. Design-system practice instead assigns weights by semantic role: 300–400 for display (large sizes carry light weights well), 400 for body, 500–600 for emphasis, 700+ reserved for the single strongest tier — the EC Component Library's 400/700-by-role assignment is one codified example. There is also a minor rendering tax: heavy weights at small sizes clog counters on non-Retina and Windows GDI rendering, degrading legibility on exactly the wrong elements (buttons, labels).
- **Detection:** Grep Tailwind classes for `font-extrabold`/`font-bold` applied across h1–h4 alike; count distinct font-weight values in computed styles (exactly {400, 500, 700} or {400, 700} is the default signature); check whether weight never decreases as size increases — a 64px heading at weight 800 is nearly always wrong, because large sizes need *less* weight.
- **Valid exceptions:** Bold display headlines are a legitimate expressive register for loud brands (fitness, streetwear, crypto); `font-extrabold tracking-tight` is fine for the single hero headline when the rest of the scale steps down.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Invert the instinct: light weights (300–400) for the largest display text, medium (500–600) for mid-tier headings, bold (700) reserved for one emphasis tier or small UI labels. Weight *contrast* (300 against 700) creates hierarchy more cheaply than size alone — the deliberate-contrast strategy anti-slop guides prescribe ("a 900-weight headline next to a 300-weight body").
- **Supporting sources:** "How to fix the 'AI-generated' look in your frontend" — https://dev.to/alanwest/how-to-fix-the-ai-generated-look-in-your-frontend-1ahh, 2026-05-18; "Building a typography system? Make sure you've covered the minimum" — https://www.designsystemscollective.com/building-a-typography-system-make-sure-youve-covered-the-minimum-06fcbf8b12f0, 2025-06-16; "Premium Web Design — Skills for Claude Code" — https://www.aitmpl.com/component/skills/creative-design/premium-web-design, accessed 2026; EC Component Library Typography guidelines — https://ec.europa.eu/component-library/v3.13.0/ec/guidelines/typography/, accessed 2026.
- **Confidence:** Medium-high — strong community consensus and clear design-system rationale, but "wrong weight" is a craft judgment with no accessibility line; honestly a consensus/aesthetics finding, not research-backed harm.

### Type-Scale Monotony or Chaos (No Modular Scale; Arbitrary Pixel One-Offs)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** Two failure shapes. **Monotony:** H1/H2/H3 at nearly the same size (for example 24/20/18px), so the page reads as an undifferentiated wall. **Chaos:** ten or more unrelated sizes, including arbitrary one-offs (`text-[17px]`, `font-size: 23px`, `text-[15.5px]`) that match nothing else on the page.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** A type scale is a system of *ratios*, and ratio is what makes hierarchy legible at a glance. Tim Brown's canonical A List Apart piece (2011) argues that modular scales — base × ratio, from 1.125 (major second) to 1.618 (golden) — create a "visual harmony not found in layouts that use arbitrary, conventional, or easily divisible numbers." AI output defaults to Tailwind's built-in steps, which are a serviceable scale, but models then either pick adjacent steps for different heading tiers (monotony) or pepper arbitrary values to "fix" spacing locally (chaos). Design-system guidance converges on 6–9 deliberate steps; more steps means system collapse. Chaos carries a maintenance and accessibility cost: one-off pixel values are ungreppable and unthemed, and pixel font sizes ignore browser text-size settings where rem respects them.
- **Detection:** Extract all computed font-size values: more than 8–9 distinct values, or any non-scale arbitrary values (Tailwind `text-[...]` syntax, inline `style="font-size: ..."`, px units instead of rem), indicate chaos. Hierarchy check: a ratio below 1.15 between consecutive heading levels indicates monotony.
- **Valid exceptions:** Data-dense admin UIs legitimately compress the scale (three to four sizes, with weight and color carrying hierarchy); editorial sites legitimately use dramatic ratios (1.5 and above).
- **Stronger alternatives:** Define the scale as tokens before writing components: a 16–18px base multiplied by a chosen ratio (1.25, the major third, is the safe default; 1.333–1.5 for marketing drama), 6–9 steps, in rem, with fluid `clamp()` on the top two or three steps. In Tailwind, encode the scale in `@theme { --text-* }` so utilities cannot drift.
- **Supporting sources:** Tim Brown, "More Meaningful Typography" — https://alistapart.com/article/more-meaningful-typography/, 2011-04-21; "Type Scale Guide" — https://www.pacgie.com/guides/what-is-type-scale, 2025-12-17; "Typography for beginners: type scale, line height & lengths" — https://iamsteve.me/blog/type-scale-line-height-lengths, 2015/2016; EC Component Library Typography guidelines — https://ec.europa.eu/component-library/v3.13.0/ec/guidelines/typography/, accessed 2026.
- **Confidence:** Medium-high — modular scales are a fifteen-year-old stable convention with broad design-system adoption; note honestly that the "harmony" claim is craft tradition (Bringhurst/Brown), not experimental psychology. The rem-versus-px accessibility point is documented practice.

### All-Caps Overuse

- **Classification:** Contextual risk (Hard failure when applied to full sentences, paragraphs, or long button labels)
- **Visible symptoms:** AI landing pages frequently uppercase everything at once: eyebrow label, nav items, buttons, section headings, and card titles. Also common: uppercase *without* the compensating letter-spacing, and `text-transform: uppercase` applied to multi-word headings that then wrap.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Butterick, citing cognitive research, explains the mechanism: lowercase letters have varied contours (tall d/h/k/l, short a/e/n/s, descending g/y/p/q) that aid word recognition, while capitalization homogenizes every word into the same rectangular shape — so all-caps text is measurably harder to read, and the effect compounds with length. All-caps paragraphs are self-defeating typography: readers skim exactly the text you meant to emphasize. Correct use is caps for labels shorter than one line, always with +5–12% letter-spacing and kerning on. **Honesty caveat:** the older experimental literature — Tinker-era mid-20th-century reading-speed studies — is routinely cited for a 10–20% reading-speed penalty; treat that number as community-cited legacy research, not a modern replication, and do not quote the more precise percentages that circulate on content-farm blogs.
- **Detection:** `text-transform: uppercase` or Tailwind `uppercase` on elements containing more than roughly five words, or on any `<p>`; `uppercase` without an adjacent `tracking-*` class; a raw count of uppercase-styled elements per page (eyebrow + nav + buttons + all headings is the overuse signal).
- **Valid exceptions:** Eyebrow/kicker micro-labels, badges, table headers, short nav items, one-word buttons — with wide tracking. This is the one place the AI default (uppercase + `tracking-widest` eyebrow) is actually correct, which is exactly why it appears everywhere; the failure is extending it beyond micro-labels.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Sentence-case headings with size and weight doing the emphasis; the small-caps OpenType feature (`font-variant-caps: all-small-caps`) for a more refined label voice; if a paragraph needs emphasis, use borders, background tints, or a bold lead-in — never caps.
- **Supporting sources:** Butterick, "All caps" — https://practicaltypography.com/all-caps.html, accessed 2026; Butterick, "Letterspacing" — https://practicaltypography.com/letterspacing.html, accessed 2026.
- **Confidence:** High for the mechanism (contour homogenization is well explained and uncontroversial); Medium on the specific "10–20% slower" figure — legacy research, widely re-cited, not recently replicated.

### Poor Measure (Body Text Spanning Full Container Width)

- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **Visible symptoms:** Hero subheads, feature descriptions, and "about" paragraphs stretching edge-to-edge across a 1280px container — 120+ characters per line — usually centered (see the next pattern). AI layouts place text in the same full-width grid cells as cards and images, with no max-width guard.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** This is the single best-evidenced pattern in this section; multiple independent traditions agree within a narrow band of roughly 45–80 characters per line. Emil Ruder (via Baymard) puts the optimal body measure at 50–60 characters, with up to 75 acceptable. Bringhurst, via *The Elements of Typographic Style Applied to the Web*: "Anything from 45 to 75 characters is widely regarded as a satisfactory length of line... The 66-character line is widely regarded as ideal." WCAG SC 1.4.8 (Visual Presentation, AAA) requires that lines not exceed 80 characters (40 for CJK), cited by Baymard as accessibility-backed. Baymard's large-scale e-commerce testing found users perceive long lines as intimidating and overwhelming, skip reading, or abandon the page; their recommendation is `max-width: ~70ch / 34em`. Experimental support: Dyson & Haselgrove (2001) found 55 characters per line produced better comprehension than 100 cpl (reviewed in Dyson 2004). The mechanism is understood at both ends: at very long measures the eye struggles to find the start of the next line and loses its place; at very short ones, return-sweep frequency breaks reading rhythm.
- **Detection:** Paragraph or `text-lg` elements with no `max-w-*` class inside full-width flex/grid children; a computed measure above 80 characters per line at desktop (element width divided by average character width in devtools). The absence of Tailwind `max-w-prose` (65ch) or `max-w-xl`/`2xl` on text blocks is the Tailwind signature; `max-w-7xl` (80rem) section wrappers with direct paragraph children are an automatic fail.
- **Valid exceptions:** Marketing pages where paragraphs are genuinely one or two short lines; tables and spec sheets; intentionally wide pull-quotes at large sizes (large type tolerates a wider measure).
- **Stronger alternatives:** Cap text blocks at 60–75ch (`max-w-prose` is built into Tailwind but rarely emitted); in multi-column marketing layouts keep each text column between 45 and 75ch; if a block looks lost, enlarge the font size rather than widening the measure.
- **Supporting sources:** "Readability: The Optimal Line Length" — https://baymard.com/blog/line-length-readability, continuously updated, accessed 2026; "Choose a comfortable measure," The Elements of Typographic Style Applied to the Web — https://webtypography.net/2.1.2, accessed 2026; Dyson (2004), "How physical text layout affects reading from screen" — https://images3.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20060729105544/psychology/images/e/eb/Dyson,_M_C_(2004).pdf, 2004; WCAG Understanding SC 1.4.12 (see also SC 1.4.8's 80-character limit as cited by Baymard) — https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/text-spacing.html, accessed 2026.
- **Confidence:** High — Swiss typography (Ruder), Bringhurst, WCAG, Baymard's large-scale testing, and experimental comprehension research converge on the same narrow band (45–80 cpl).

### Center-Aligned Body Copy

- **Classification:** Avoid by default (Hard failure for paragraphs and long blocks; Contextual risk for short hero titles)
- **Visible symptoms:** Entire sections wrapped in `text-center`: centered H2, centered three-sentence paragraph, centered feature cards with centered multi-line descriptions — the hallmark AI landing-page layout.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** For any text longer than about two lines, centering removes the consistent left edge the eye returns to after each line; every line starts at a different x-position, increasing re-entry cost per line. Butterick is blunt: "Whole text blocks should not be centered. Centering makes text blocks difficult to read because both edges of the text block are uneven." The web-usability literature agrees (Lynch & Horton 2001; Nielsen 2000; Ivory 2001, as reviewed in a White Rose usability thesis): centered and right alignment "should not be used for blocks of text," with centered headings acceptable only for a few lines. Nielsen Norman Group's F-pattern scanning research describes the adjacent mechanism — users anchor on the left edge. It also reads as generic because it is the zero-decision layout: centering everything avoids making any compositional choice (Butterick: "the typographic equivalent of vanilla ice cream"). **Honesty caveat:** no modern NN/g page quantifying center-versus-left reading speed could be verified; older NN/g citations circulate secondhand, one previously referenced NN/g justified-text article now returns 404 and is excluded, and specific percentages found on content-farm blogs ("38% faster") are unattributed fabrications — do not cite them.
- **Detection:** Tailwind `text-center` (or `items-center` + `text-center`) on section wrappers containing `<p>` elements; CSS `text-align: center` inherited by paragraphs; any centered text block longer than two rendered lines.
- **Valid exceptions:** Hero title plus a one-to-two-line subhead, CTAs, pricing-card headers, invitations and quotes, empty states — short, self-contained phrases. Even then, descriptions inside feature cards are usually better left-aligned once they exceed two lines.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Left-align (RTL: right-align) all running text; constrain centered layouts to the hero; if a centered section is desired visually, center the *container* (max-width plus auto margins) while keeping the text left-aligned inside it.
- **Supporting sources:** Butterick, "Centered text" — https://practicaltypography.com/centered-text.html, accessed 2026; "A Multilingual Automated Web Usability Evaluation Agent" (literature review citing Lynch & Horton 2001, Nielsen 2000, Ivory 2001) — https://etheses.whiterose.ac.uk/id/eprint/3565/1/414690.pdf, accessed 2026; WVU course guidance referencing NN/g F-pattern research (Pernice, 2017) — https://community.wvu.edu/~lsmong/GRAD670-module-pages/05overcomine-f-pattern.html, accessed 2026.
- **Confidence:** High for long text (typography authorities, the usability literature, and decades of style guides agree); the caveat above stands for any quantified speed claim.

### Missing Fallback Stacks and Font-Loading Strategy

- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **Visible symptoms:** Invisible text for up to roughly three seconds on slow connections (FOIT) — or indefinitely on Safari; text that visibly reflows the whole page when the webfont arrives (cumulative layout shift); mojibake or tofu when the single specified family fails; CJK or extended-Latin text falling back to a jarring system font mid-sentence.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** web.dev's font best-practices guide (Katie Hempenius) documents the mechanics: Chromium and Firefox block text rendering for up to three seconds when a webfont has not loaded, Safari blocks indefinitely, and swapping causes layout shift whenever webfont and fallback metrics differ. AI output frequently declares `font-family: 'Inter';` (a single family with no generic fallback) or loads Google Fonts via `@import` without `display=swap`, with no `font-display`, no metric-matched fallback, and no `size-adjust`. Geist adds a coverage trap: it supports Latin, Latin-Extended, and Cyrillic only (no CJK, Arabic, or Devanagari as of 2026), so internationalized pages silently fall back per glyph. The mitigations are well known: full fallback stacks ending in a generic family, `font-display: swap` (or `optional` for body), WOFF2-only delivery, subsetting, and metric overrides (`size-adjust`, `ascent-override`) to eliminate CLS.
- **Detection:** `font-family` declarations with a single family and no `sans-serif`/`serif` terminator; `@font-face` blocks without `font-display`; Google Fonts CSS `@import` URLs lacking `display=swap`; Lighthouse's "Avoid invisible text during webfont load" (FOIT) audit and CLS attributed to font swap. Functional test: block the font domain in devtools — text should still render readably.
- **Valid exceptions:** `next/font` (Google or local) self-hosts, subsets, preloads, and auto-generates metric-matched fallback CSS with `size-adjust`, so Next.js projects using it correctly are largely exempt — which is precisely why detection must read the code path, not just the rendered page. Offline-installed apps with bundled fonts are also exempt.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Always terminate stacks: `font-family: 'Brand Sans', system-ui, -apple-system, 'Segoe UI', Roboto, sans-serif`. Use `font-display: swap` plus a `size-adjust`-matched fallback (tooling: fontaine, `next/font`). Use system font stacks for body text when the brand allows (zero latency, zero CLS). Ship WOFF2 with subsetting; preconnect if third-party hosting is unavoidable.
- **Supporting sources:** "Font best practices," Katie Hempenius, web.dev — https://web.dev/articles/font-best-practices, accessed 2026; "Geist Font Review" (script coverage limits) — https://fontcompressor.com/blog/geist-font-guide, accessed 2026; "How to use Vercel's Geist Font in Next.js" — https://peerlist.io/blog/engineering/how-to-use-vercel-geist-font-in-nextjs, 2024-10-17.
- **Confidence:** High — browser blocking behavior and Core Web Vitals impact are platform-documented facts.

## 4. Surface and Material Failures

This is the largest cluster of visual-identity failures, and the one where AI-generated sites are most recognizable at a glance: gradient-washed heroes, blurred aurora orbs, glass cards, glow shadows, hairline-bordered dark surfaces, and the spacing boilerplate that holds them together. It is also the cluster with the widest evidence spread — several patterns here are objectively broken in their most common implementation (reproducible scroll jank traced to specific GitHub issues, computed WCAG contrast failures, invisible gradient text), while others are purely generic signals whose only crime is being the statistical median of the training data. Each pattern below states which kind of failure it is; the classifications deliberately distinguish "measurably broken" from "measurably overused."

**Cluster 1 — Backgrounds, glows, and material effects.** These eight patterns concern what sits behind and around the content. They compound: the canonical AI hero stacks a purple gradient under aurora blobs under a grid pattern under a glass card, each layer individually borderline and jointly a rendering and readability problem.

### Gradient hero backgrounds and mesh gradients (the "AI purple" wash)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default; escalates to **Hard failure** when text-on-gradient contrast fails WCAG 1.4.3 at the worst stop.
- **Visible symptoms:** A full-viewport hero washed in a purple-to-blue, indigo-to-purple, or purple-to-pink linear gradient, or a multi-stop "mesh" of stacked radial gradients; a white centered headline on top, often with a gradient CTA button beneath it.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** This is the single most recognized AI-generated aesthetic — Hallmark (Together AI's anti-slop skill) lists "the purple-gradient hero" as its first critical tell — and its documented causal provenance (the Tailwind `indigo-500` demo default around 2020, amplified through the training corpus) is treated in §2's default-palette pattern block. The functional escalation is objective: gradient backgrounds have uneven luminance, and WCAG 1.4.3's 4.5:1 (normal text) / 3:1 (large text) requirement must hold against the *worst* point of the gradient under the text, not the average. Mid-tone purple and blue stops frequently sit in the 2–3:1 range against white; computed for this report's companion detection research, white on Tailwind `indigo-500` (#6366f1) is 4.47:1 — failing AA for normal text by 0.03 — and white on `blue-500` (#3b82f6) is 3.68:1. Compositionally, a saturated full-bleed gradient also competes with the headline and flattens the hierarchy of every section below it.
- **Detection:** In CSS, `linear-gradient(` on hero or full-viewport containers with stops in the `#6366f1` / `#8b5cf6` / `#a855f7` / `#ec4899` / `#3b82f6` / `#06b6d4` family, or the classic `#667eea → #764ba2`. In Tailwind, `bg-gradient-to-br from-indigo-500 via-purple-500 to-pink-500` and siblings; the regex `bg-gradient-to-[trlb]{1,2}\s+from-(indigo|purple|violet|blue|pink)-[4-6]00` catches most variants. Mesh: three or more stacked `radial-gradient(at X% Y%, …)` layers on one element. For the hard-failure case, sample the lightest stop under the headline and run the WCAG 1.4.3 formula.
- **Valid exceptions:** A brief that explicitly calls for a bold brand-gradient identity — the brand's *own* gradient, tokenized, with contrast-checked text (Stripe spent years building equity in a blurple-adjacent palette with high-craft custom execution). Creative and brand showcase sites more broadly. Note also the honest counterpoint: mesh gradients cycled back as a 2026 trend, and CSS-only mesh gradients are legitimately cheap to render (under 1KB of CSS, better LCP than image heroes) — the problem is the unchosen default purple, not gradients as a technique.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A solid tinted-neutral surface with one anchor accent hue; a brand-owned duotone; a subtle two-stop gradient confined to a band or card rather than the viewport; photography or illustration carrying the hero.
- **Supporting sources:** Hallmark anti-patterns.md — https://github.com/Nutlope/hallmark/blob/main/skills/hallmark/references/anti-patterns.md (retrieved 2026); 925Studios, "AI Slop Fonts and Gradients: The Tells That Give Away AI Design" — https://www.925studios.co/blog/ai-slop-design-tells (2026-07-16); Instant Gradient, "Making Gradient Backgrounds Accessible (Without Killing the Vibe)" — https://instantgradient.com/blog/accessible_gradient_guide (2026-04-13); Acquia, "Does text over images or background have minimum contrast?" — https://docs.acquia.com/web-governance/does-text-over-images-or-background-have-minimum-contrast (2026-02-20); Pravin Kumar, "Why Mesh Gradient Hero Sections Are Back on Webflow Sites in 2026" — https://www.pravinkumar.co/blog/mesh-gradients-hero-sections-webflow-2026-design-trend (2026-06-05); Lee Robinson, "How Stripe Designs Beautiful Websites" — https://archive.leerob.io/blog/how-stripe-designs-beautiful-websites (2017-12-02).
- **Confidence:** High — multiple independent sources converge on the generic signal, the Tailwind-default-to-training-median mechanism is documented, and the contrast math is objective and reproducible.

### Aurora blobs and radial glow orbs (runtime `filter: blur()` behind heroes)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default; **Hard failure** in the canonical implementation — large elements with runtime `filter: blur()` — due to rendering performance.
- **Visible symptoms:** Two to five absolutely positioned blurred circles or ellipses in purple, pink, cyan, or orange floating behind hero content (the "aurora" look), often slowly drifting or pulsing, sometimes with `mix-blend-mode`.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** As a generic signal, Hallmark names "aurora-blob background" a critical tell — flowing purple-to-pink-to-cyan blobs that audiences "pattern-match in milliseconds: AI template" — and its "floating-orb decoration" entry adds that ambient blurred circles "have no semantic role… It implies 'I needed something here, so I added something here.'" The performance failure is objective and reproducible. thirdweb's dashboard suffered "very poor" rendering performance and dropped frames on an M1 Pro MacBook caused exactly by `filter: blur` aurora elements; removing the blur fixed it, and the recommended remedy was a pre-blurred static image (GitHub thirdweb-dev/dashboard issue #703). The same convolution-cost family produced two more public reproductions: shadcn/ui issue #327 traced modal and sheet jank on resource-constrained machines to `backdrop-blur-sm` ("the performance issue was not Javascript… it was a CSS rendering/painting problem"), and Foundry VTT issue #10400 measured GPU and frame-rate hits from a mere `blur(5px)` backdrop and replaced it with a plain `rgba(0,0,0,70%)` fill. Blur cost scales with radius times area times repaint frequency; in one measured case GPU usage roughly doubled with blur effects enabled. Compositionally, the orbs also create uncontrolled gradient edges behind text — the same worst-point contrast problem as the gradient hero above.
- **Detection:** In markup, empty decorative `<div>`s as first children of hero sections — the signature is `absolute -top-40 -left-40 h-96 w-96 rounded-full bg-purple-500/30 blur-3xl`. In CSS, `filter: blur(` at 60px or more on elements 300px or larger; Tailwind `blur-3xl` (64px); keyframes named `float`, `drift`, `pulse-slow`, or `aurora`. At runtime, Chrome DevTools Performance shows long paint and composite blocks while scrolling past the hero.
- **Valid exceptions:** A brand brief that explicitly asks for an atmospheric or aurora identity (an "ambient" wellness product, for instance), implemented as a pre-rendered image or video rather than runtime CSS blur — cosmos.network is cited as the working example. Also acceptable: decorative-only placement with no text overlapping blob edges.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A solid surface; a subtle two-stop gradient plus SVG `<feTurbulence>` grain under 0.1 opacity (Hallmark's recommended replacement recipe); a pre-blurred static background image; a typographic anchor instead of simulated depth.
- **Supporting sources:** GitHub thirdweb-dev/dashboard issue #703, "Poor Rendering Performance caused by CSS blur filter" — https://github.com/thirdweb-dev/dashboard/issues/703 (2022-12-03); GitHub shadcn-ui/ui issue #327, "CSS Backdrop filter causing performance issues" — https://github.com/shadcn-ui/ui/issues/327 (2023-05-10); GitHub foundryvtt/foundryvtt issue #10400 — https://github.com/foundryvtt/foundryvtt/issues/10400 (2024-02-08); Hallmark anti-patterns.md — https://github.com/Nutlope/hallmark/blob/main/skills/hallmark/references/anti-patterns.md (retrieved 2026); F22 Labs, "How CSS Properties Affect Website Performance" — https://www.f22labs.com/blogs/how-css-properties-affect-website-performance/ (2025-01-30).
- **Confidence:** High — the generic-signal claim is broad community consensus, and the performance claim rests on a concrete public reproduction plus two corroborating issues from the same cost family.

### Gradient text on headings (`background-clip: text`)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default; **Hard failure** when shipped without a solid fallback color (invisible text) or when the lightest gradient stop fails contrast.
- **Visible symptoms:** A hero headline filled with a purple-to-pink or blue-to-cyan gradient instead of a solid ink color; a shimmer or animated variant sweeps color across the words.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Hallmark's assessment: "The gradient headline… Signals 'AI generated' faster than almost anything else." Beyond the tell, there are two distinct objective failure modes. First, contrast is unfixed: the text's effective color varies per glyph and per stop, so a gradient passing 4.5:1 at its midpoint can fail at its ends — audit guidance says to test the fallback color and the worst stop, never the midpoint. Second, the invisibility risk: the recipe requires `color: transparent` (or `-webkit-text-fill-color: transparent`), so wherever `background-clip: text` is unsupported, disabled, or overridden — old browsers, forced-colors and high-contrast modes, background-image blocking — the text renders as *nothing*, a failure documented as happening "rather spectacularly… You cannot even read the text." Modern guidance mandates a solid `color` fallback plus an `@supports` guard. There is also a branding cost: gradient fills reduce perceived type weight and make the most important words on the page the least consistent.
- **Detection:** The Tailwind signature cluster `bg-clip-text text-transparent bg-gradient-to-r from-…` is trivially greppable (regex: `bg-clip-text\s+text-transparent`). In CSS, `background-clip: text` combined with `color: transparent` or `-webkit-text-fill-color: transparent`. Then check for an `@supports` guard and a non-transparent fallback `color`; their absence marks the hard-failure risk.
- **Valid exceptions:** Short display words in a brand or creative showcase where the gradient is the brand's own and the darkest stop passes 4.5:1; SVG `<text>` with gradient fill for brand-critical headlines (works without `background-clip`). Always with a solid fallback, an `@supports` guard, and a reduced-motion pause for animated variants.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Solid ink with weight, italic, or a display face for energy (Hallmark's fix); one accent-colored word; an underline or highlight mark.
- **Supporting sources:** Hallmark anti-patterns.md — https://github.com/Nutlope/hallmark/blob/main/skills/hallmark/references/anti-patterns.md (retrieved 2026); Nimbupani (Divya Manian), "Using background clip for text with CSS fallback" — https://nimbupani.com/using-background-clip-for-text-with-css-fallback.html; TestMu AI (LambdaTest), "CSS background-clip Text: Browser Support, Use Cases, Fixes" — https://www.testmuai.com/learning-hub/background-clip-text-browser-support/ (2026-05-04); Instant Gradient — https://instantgradient.com/blog/accessible_gradient_guide (2026-04-13); Animation Patterns, "CSS Gradient Text Sweep with background-clip" — https://animationpatterns.art/animations/gradient-text-sweep/.
- **Confidence:** High for the fallback and contrast mechanics (objective CSS behavior); medium-high for "fastest AI tell" (practitioner consensus).

### Glassmorphism: `backdrop-blur` cards over busy backgrounds

- **Classification:** Contextual risk; **Hard failure** when text-on-glass contrast falls below 4.5:1 (common) or when applied to full-height or sticky elements that jank scrolling on low-end GPUs.
- **Visible symptoms:** Frosted-glass cards and navbars — a semi-transparent white or black fill, background blur, and a 1px light border — stacked over a gradient, aurora blobs, or photography, with content behind the glass showing through as smears of color.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** This is the best-evidenced pattern in the section. Nielsen Norman Group's glassmorphism guidance identifies text readability as "one of the most significant issues," because translucent components sit over *multiple* background colors, so contrast passes in some regions and fails in others. An OsloMet master's thesis (2025, task-based, three prototypes, n=18) found the highest-transparency glass prototype produced longer task times and more readability and distraction complaints, with a majority of participants preferring the no-glass version — while a *moderate* glass version maintained acceptable usability. Context makes the stakes concrete: low-contrast text is the single most common accessibility failure on the web, present on 83.9% of the top one million home pages (WebAIM Million, February 2026 data), and uncontrolled translucency manufactures exactly that condition. On performance, `backdrop-filter` forces the browser to re-capture and re-blur everything behind the element on every scroll or animation frame; the shadcn/ui and Foundry VTT issues cited above are both backdrop-blur cases, and practitioner guidance puts mobile tolerance at roughly three to five simultaneous backdrop blurs before scroll jank. As a generic signal, Hallmark lists "glassmorphism without purpose" as a major tell — "usually layered over a gradient that you also shouldn't have."
- **Detection:** The Tailwind cluster `backdrop-blur-md|lg` + `bg-white/5|10|20` or `bg-black/30` + `border-white/10|20`; in CSS, `backdrop-filter: blur(` or `-webkit-backdrop-filter`. Count occurrences — more than five glass surfaces on one page is a flag. For contrast, sample text against the *actual composited* backdrop (DevTools accessibility pane) across the busiest region behind the glass, not against the tint color alone, and remember the backdrop changes as content scrolls — a measurement that passes at rest can fail one screenful later. For performance, record scroll in DevTools with the glass element visible versus hidden.
- **Valid exceptions:** Genuine overlay contexts — a floating nav over scrolling content, modals, contextual menus — where glass communicates real depth; this is native-platform-sanctioned usage (Microsoft Fluent "acrylic," macOS materials, visionOS overlays). NN/g's best-practice conditions: more blur over intricate backgrounds, controlled and simple backgrounds where possible, user-adjustable transparency (Apple's Reduce Transparency), and WCAG-compliant contrast always. A semi-opaque tint layer behind text keeps glass readable.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Solid or near-solid surfaces with 1px hairline borders; elevation by surface lightness on dark themes; an `rgba()` tint without blur where the blur adds no meaning (the Foundry fix); glass restricted to one small fixed region (a nav pill) at 8–16px blur, with a `prefers-reduced-transparency` fallback.
- **Supporting sources:** Nielsen Norman Group, "Glassmorphism: Definition and Best Practices" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/glassmorphism/ (2024-06-07); OsloMet master's thesis on glassmorphism accessibility — https://oda.oslomet.no/oda-xmlui/bitstream/handle/11250/3206174/no.oslomet:inspera:348177680:130368616.pdf (2025); GitHub shadcn-ui/ui issue #327 — https://github.com/shadcn-ui/ui/issues/327 (2023-05-10); GitHub foundryvtt/foundryvtt issue #10400 — https://github.com/foundryvtt/foundryvtt/issues/10400 (2024-02-08); Axess Lab, "Glassmorphism Meets Accessibility: Can Frosted Glass Be Inclusive?" — https://axesslab.com/glassmorphism-meets-accessibility-can-frosted-glass-be-inclusive/ (2025-06-11); WebAIM Million — https://webaim.org/projects/million/ (February 2026 data); OpenReplay, "Creating Blurred Backgrounds Using CSS Backdrop-Filter" — https://blog.openreplay.com/creating-blurred-backgrounds-css-backdrop-filter/ (2025-11-08); Mavik Labs, "CSS Backdrop-Filter Performance in 2026" — https://www.maviklabs.com/blog/performance-with-backdrop-filter-2026/ (2026-01-18); UX Pilot, "12 Glassmorphism UI Features, Best Practices, and Examples" — https://uxpilot.ai/blogs/glassmorphism-ui (2025-11-25).
- **Confidence:** High — a UX-research organization, an empirical thesis, census data, and reproducible performance issues all converge; moderate, structural use is defensible, everywhere-default use is not.

### Colored box-shadow glows on cards and buttons ("neon glow on dark")

- **Classification:** Avoid by default; Hard failure only in the animated variant (see animated gradient borders below) or when a glow replaces visible focus states.
- **Visible symptoms:** Cards and buttons on dark backgrounds ringed by soft colored halos (`shadow-[0_0_40px_rgba(139,92,246,0.5)]`); the primary CTA "glowing" purple, blue, or cyan, intensifying on hover.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** uxcraft's anti-slop blacklist is blunt: "Neon glow effects on dark backgrounds — AI 'cyberpunk' default. Use brand accent sparingly, without glow," and SmoothUI names "glassmorphism with a neon glow" part of the spot-in-a-second slop cluster. The compositional argument is principled: a shadow is a luminance cue, and on dark surfaces elevation is conventionally communicated by *lighter* surfaces, not halos — Hallmark's "shadow-glow on dark" entry prescribes lightness for elevation and keeping any shadow "tight and dark." A colored halo also visually merges adjacent elements and muddies hierarchy. Performance adds a bounded cost: large-blur-radius and stacked shadows are among the most expensive CSS properties, and the cost multiplies when shadows animate on hover or scroll, landing hardest on low-end devices.
- **Detection:** In CSS, `box-shadow:` with a blur radius of 24px or more and a chromatic color (non-gray, non-black rgba) at zero or small offsets — `0 0 40px rgba(139,92,246,.4)`. In Tailwind, `shadow-lg shadow-purple-500/50`, arbitrary `shadow-[0_0_*px_rgba(...)]`, `drop-shadow-[0_0_...]`, or a custom `glow` utility.
- **Valid exceptions:** A brief that asks for a neon, cyberpunk, or gaming aesthetic; a single subtle glow reserved for the one primary CTA as an attention device; status indication (a "recording" red pulse) with reduced-motion respect.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Elevation via surface lightness steps on dark UIs; a hairline border plus a tight 2–8px dark shadow; the accent color reserved for text and icons, not halos.
- **Supporting sources:** Hallmark anti-patterns.md ("Shadow-glow on dark") — https://github.com/Nutlope/hallmark/blob/main/skills/hallmark/references/anti-patterns.md (retrieved 2026); uxcraft output-quality-gates.md, "Anti-AI-Slop Blacklist" — https://app.unpkg.com/uxcraft@1.5.4/files/references/output-quality-gates.md; SmoothUI, "AI Design Slop: Why AI-Generated UI Looks Generic — and the Fix" — https://smoothui.dev/blog/ai-design-slop (2026-06-24); F22 Labs, "How CSS Properties Affect Website Performance" — https://www.f22labs.com/blogs/how-css-properties-affect-website-performance/ (2025-01-30).
- **Confidence:** High for the "AI default" consensus; medium for harm beyond genericness — the elevation-semantics argument is principled but convention-based, not measured.

### Grid, dot, and line background patterns as decoration

- **Classification:** Contextual risk — low actual harm; this is an oversaturation and genericness issue, not a functional one.
- **Visible symptoms:** Faint engineering-grid lines or a dot matrix behind hero and CTA sections (the "graph paper" look), sometimes masked with a radial fade, often combined with orbs and glass cards in the same hero.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** The pattern spread from a handful of 2023 tutorials — ibelick's grid-and-dot CSS post is the canonical recipe, also packaged into background galleries like bg.ibelick.com — into countless Tailwind templates, and generator tools like MagicPattern now offer 50-plus one-click patterns aimed at "landing pages, SaaS dashboards." Its presence signals "template," especially stacked with the other effects in this cluster. The honest assessment of harm: mostly zero. Misuse can produce moiré or vibration on some displays at 1px scales, slight paint cost when the pattern layer is large and animated, and local contrast erosion if lines are too dark — but no research-grade harm was found. The issue is sameness.
- **Detection:** Exact signatures from the canonical recipe. Grid: `background-image: linear-gradient(to right, #80808012 1px, transparent 1px), linear-gradient(to bottom, #80808012 1px, transparent 1px); background-size: 24px 24px;`. Dot: `background-image: radial-gradient(#e5e7eb 1px, transparent 1px); background-size: 16px 16px;`. Tailwind arbitrary values: `bg-[linear-gradient(to_right,#80808012_1px,transparent_1px),…]`, `bg-[size:24px_24px]`, plus a radial mask `[mask-image:radial-gradient(ellipse_at_center,…)]`.
- **Valid exceptions:** A brief with an engineering, blueprint, plotter, or graph theme where the grid carries meaning; brands with a genuine graph-paper identity; any use where the pattern is quiet (under roughly 8% line opacity) and the hero is not also orb-plus-glass-plus-gradient.
- **Stronger alternatives:** No texture at all — whitespace is free; texture derived from the product domain (a fintech grid drawn from its chart language, cartography contours for a mapping tool); a single ruled line as a compositional device.
- **Supporting sources:** ibelick, "Crafting grid and dot backgrounds with CSS and Tailwind CSS" — https://ibelick.com/blog/create-grid-and-dot-backgrounds-with-css-tailwind-css (2023-07-14); MagicPattern CSS Background Patterns generator — https://www.magicpattern.design/tools/css-backgrounds.
- **Confidence:** Medium — oversaturation is well-evidenced; failure claims are weak, and no usability research against subtle static patterns exists.

### Animated gradient borders, rotating conic frames, and animated gradient backgrounds

- **Classification:** Avoid by default; **Hard failure** when the motion cannot be paused or removed (WCAG 2.2.2, vestibular risk) — a common case, since AI output rarely wires `prefers-reduced-motion`.
- **Visible symptoms:** Cards and buttons with a rainbow beam chasing around the border; hero backgrounds whose gradient slowly slides or hue-rotates; shimmer sweeps on text or buttons.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** On accessibility, looping decorative motion must respect `prefers-reduced-motion` and WCAG 2.2.2 (pause, stop, hide for auto-moving content), and gradient-text animation guidance explicitly requires pausing at a readable frame under reduced motion; continuous motion near text is also a documented reading distraction. On performance, animating `background-position`, gradient stops, or `border-color` forces a full repaint of the affected region every frame — only `transform` and `opacity` changes skip layout and paint. Practitioner guidance is specific: "conic-gradient with animated stop positions is paint-expensive — rotate the entire conic element via transform instead"; "don't animate box-shadow on a loop — paint + blur recalculation every frame. Catastrophic on low-end devices"; "backdrop-filter on a 100vh sticky element kills scroll FPS." The `@property --angle` registration trick used for smooth angle animation still repaints per frame. As a generic signal, conic-border glow cards were 2023–2024 tutorial fodder and now read as a component-library demo rather than a design decision.
- **Detection:** In CSS, `@property --angle` with `conic-gradient(from var(--angle), …)`; keyframes animating `background-position` on an oversized `linear-gradient(…); background-size: 200%+`; `border-image` with gradients; a rotating `::before` pseudo-element under a card with an `inset: -2px` inner cover. The accessibility check is structural: no `@media (prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)` block near the keyframes means hard failure.
- **Valid exceptions:** Skeleton-loading shimmer (functional, conventional, time-bounded); a brief asking for kinetic or maximalist brand expression (gaming, music, events) with a reduced-motion fallback; one deliberate attention pulse on a first-run CTA.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A static 1px gradient border (same look, zero motion, zero paint loop); an instant single-color shift on hover (Hallmark's fix: "Cut. Or pick one colour shift, instant"); transform- and opacity-only motion.
- **Supporting sources:** Codefronts animation-performance guidance — https://codefronts.com/motion/css-spinner-designs/ (2026-06-12); Hallmark anti-patterns.md — https://github.com/Nutlope/hallmark/blob/main/skills/hallmark/references/anti-patterns.md (retrieved 2026); TestMu AI (LambdaTest) — https://www.testmuai.com/learning-hub/background-clip-text-browser-support/ (2026-05-04); JSBits, "Animation performance: transform, opacity, will-change" — https://jsbits.com/css/animation-performance-transform-opacity-gpu (2026-05-28).
- **Confidence:** High — paint-cost and WCAG mechanics are objective; the "generic" label is consensus, honestly flagged as such.

### Noise and grain overlays (SVG `feTurbulence`, film grain)

- **Classification:** Contextual risk — the one pattern in this cluster that anti-slop sources actively *recommend* in small doses; it becomes a problem only when heavy or unbriefed.
- **Visible symptoms:** A full-page film-grain layer — usually an inline SVG turbulence data-URI or a repeating PNG — at low opacity over everything; sometimes animated as "flickering" grain.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** The failure modes are bounded and practitioner-grade: above roughly 0.1 opacity, grain visibly degrades text crispness and cheapens photography; animated grain is a constant full-viewport repaint and a distraction issue; large noise PNGs add network weight. No formal readability study on grain overlays was found — the evidence is thin, hence the classification. Conversely, Hallmark prescribes "subtle two-stop CSS gradient + SVG `<feTurbulence>` grain at < 0.1 opacity" as the *fix* for aurora blobs, and anti-slop agent guides recommend SVG noise overlays as the alternative to blob backgrounds. Static CSS/SVG noise is nearly free to render.
- **Detection:** `background-image: url("data:image/svg+xml,...feTurbulence...")`; class names like `noise` or `grain`; a fixed full-viewport overlay div with `pointer-events: none` and `opacity` between 0.03 and 0.1; `<animate>` on the turbulence seed for the animated variant.
- **Valid exceptions:** Editorial and print-inspired briefs, retro or analog brands, risograph aesthetics; anywhere it replaces heavier "depth" decoration. Keep opacity at or under 0.1, static, `pointer-events: none`, and `aria-hidden`.
- **Stronger alternatives (when it is the problem):** Drop it — flat clean surfaces; or move grain into imagery only.
- **Supporting sources:** Hallmark anti-patterns.md (prescribed fix recipe) — https://github.com/Nutlope/hallmark/blob/main/skills/hallmark/references/anti-patterns.md (retrieved 2026); aitmpl, "UI UX Designer" agent guidance — https://www.aitmpl.com/component/agent/ui-ux-designer; MagicPattern — https://www.magicpattern.design/tools/css-backgrounds.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the recommendation direction is consistent across anti-slop sources, but the quantitative thresholds are practitioner lore, not research.

**Cluster 2 — Radius, borders, shadows, and elevation systems.** These eight patterns concern how individual surfaces are shaped and separated. The systemic theme: AI generators apply one surface recipe everywhere instead of maintaining a token system, so shape, border, and shadow stop carrying meaning. The evidence base here is design-system doctrine (Material, Atlassian, Polaris, AgDS, Graphite, Carbon), anti-slop rule sets, and one automated corpus analysis — with a normative WCAG anchor where borders become control boundaries.

### Radius monoculture: `rounded-2xl`/`rounded-3xl` on everything, no radius system

- **Classification:** Avoid by default — a generic signal that harms hierarchy and brand distinctness; not a functional break.
- **Visible symptoms:** Buttons, inputs, cards, modals, images, and chips all sharing the same 16–24px radius; large-radius cards nested inside same-radius cards so inner and outer corner geometry clashes; pill (`rounded-full`) buttons beside 24px-radius cards with no shape logic.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Radius is one of the few continuous shape parameters a brand owns; setting one large default on every component type throws shape away as a differentiator. Mature systems deliberately restrain radius — no-slop-ui's reference standard caps cards at 8–12px and buttons at 6–10px with "no pills." There is no compositional reason a 40px-tall input and a 400px-tall card should share a radius; uniform large radius reads as an unexamined default rather than a decision — the "no decision" root cause of slop that Vibe Code Kit names when it calls out "the untouched `rounded-2xl shadow-lg p-6` shadcn default card" as a tell. Functionally, oversized radius on small interactive elements shrinks the perceived rectilinear hit-area boundary and can visually compete with genuinely pill-shaped elements (tags, toggles) whose shape is semantic.
- **Detection:** Tailwind signatures: `rounded-2xl` (16px), `rounded-3xl` (24px), or `rounded-4xl` (32px) applied to three or more different component types; `rounded-full` on primary buttons. In the codebase: no `--radius-*` token definitions, radius values hardcoded per component, the identical radius class repeated across button, input, card, and modal. The reviewer test is the squint test — if every element on the page has the same corner curvature, there is no radius system.
- **Valid exceptions:** Friendly consumer brands (kids, consumer fintech) where large radius is the briefed identity applied via a token scale; genuinely pill-shaped affordances — search fields, tags, avatar containers.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A radius token scale mapped to roles, such as Graphite's 2/4/8/16px small-to-x-large tokens; Polaris goes further and lints against hardcoded radius units entirely. A typical sober-product mapping: inputs and buttons 6–8px, cards 8–12px, modals 12–16px, pills only where shape is semantic.
- **Supporting sources:** no-slop-ui SKILL README and banned-patterns.md — https://github.com/LeoStehlik/no-slop-ui (2026-03); Vibe Code Kit, "AI Slop Design" — https://vibecodekit.dev/ai-slop-design (2026-06-22); Graphite Design System border-radius tokens — https://graphitedesignsystem.com/tokens/border-radius/; Shopify Polaris stylelint border rules — https://polaris-react.shopify.com/tools/stylelint-polaris/rules/border-declaration-property-unit-disallowed-list; Tailwind CSS border-radius docs — https://tailwindcss.com/docs/border-radius.
- **Confidence:** High — independent anti-slop rule sets and token-based design systems converge on restrained, role-mapped radius; the harm claim is compositional and branding consensus, not measured usability loss.

### The dark surface stack: `bg-white/5` + `border-white/10` on every card

- **Classification:** Avoid by default; escalates to **Hard failure** when the faint alpha border is the *only* visual boundary of an input or control (WCAG 1.4.11).
- **Visible symptoms:** A near-black page where every card is a translucent white wash (`bg-white/5`) ringed by a hairline white-alpha border (`border-white/10`, sometimes `border-white/[0.06]`–`[0.15]`); dividers also `bg-white/10`; the border doing all separation work because surfaces barely differ. The glassmorphism variant (`backdrop-blur` + `bg-white/10`) serves as the default dark surface.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** This is the single most reproduced dark-mode card recipe in v0/shadcn-style output — Vibe Code Kit calls the flat 1px gray border on every card "the #1 slop tell," and Adrian Krebs's Playwright analysis of 1,590 Show HN landing pages lists colored borders on cards and glassmorphism among its crowdsourced AI CSS patterns, with 22% of pages triggering four or more patterns. The accessibility escalation is normative: WCAG SC 1.4.11 requires 3:1 contrast for visual information required to identify user interface components, and a 10% white border computes to roughly 1.2:1 on black (1.32:1 on slate-900, computed with the WCAG luminance formula) — and WCAG explicitly notes that thin low-contrast lines render even fainter after anti-aliasing. If that border is the only way to perceive an input's bounds, the control fails. Compositionally, border-on-everything produces what Vibe Code Kit calls "Cardocalypse — every block boxed"; Atlassian's elevation guidance warns that raised treatments "can create visual noise, so don't use to group content when a border or white space would suffice" — the slop pattern uses *both* border and tint everywhere, always.
- **Detection:** Grep for `border-white/`, `bg-white/5`, `border-white\/\[0\.`, `rgba(255,255,255,0.1)`, and `backdrop-blur` on card classes (regex: `border-(white|black)\/(5|10|15|20)`); the computed style of any card on a dark page shows `border-color: rgba(255, 255, 255, 0.1)`. Then check whether input boundaries depend solely on that border — if so, measure contrast; under 3:1 is a WCAG failure.
- **Valid exceptions:** True glassmorphism briefs (a frosted overlay over rich imagery, control-center-style UI); hairline alpha borders as a *secondary* cue on top of already-distinct surfaces; divider lines at low alpha where whitespace alone is insufficient, raised to 3:1 or better if the line is the only boundary indicator.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Vibe Code Kit's separation order — whitespace first, then a 3–5% background-lightness shift, then soft elevation, then border only if all three fail, "never a flat gray line." Dark-mode surface hierarchy via tonal steps instead of borders (next pattern). If borders are used on inputs, raise the border alpha or switch to a solid gray-scale step until it reaches at least 3:1 against the adjacent background (slate-600 on slate-900 reaches roughly 3.2:1).
- **Supporting sources:** Vibe Code Kit, "AI Slop Design" — https://vibecodekit.dev/ai-slop-design (2026-06-22); Adrian Krebs, "An attempt to detect AI design patterns in Show HN pages" — https://adriankrebs.ch/blog/design-slop/ (2026-04-20); W3C, Understanding WCAG SC 1.4.11 Non-text Contrast — https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/non-text-contrast.html; Atlassian Design System, Elevation — https://atlassian.design/foundations/elevation.
- **Confidence:** High as a generic-signal and corpus-verified tell; the hard-failure escalation rests on a normative WCAG criterion and applies conditionally — only when the border is the sole boundary of a control.

### Meaningless shadows on dark UIs: `shadow-lg`/`shadow-2xl` on near-black surfaces

- **Classification:** Avoid by default — functionally meaningless decoration that signals unexamined light-mode habits; not a hard usability break by itself, but frequently co-occurs with the previous pattern's accessibility failures.
- **Visible symptoms:** A dark page where cards carry large dark box shadows (`shadow-xl`, `shadow-2xl`, `shadow-black/50`) that are invisible or read as muddy halos; modals and cards sharing the same shadow; "elevation" attempted only via shadow, never via surface lightness.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** A shadow is a luminance cue, and on a #0A0A0A–#121212 background there is almost no luminance headroom below the surface, so the depth cue does not register: "shadows become nearly invisible against dark backgrounds, because there is insufficient contrast between a dark shadow and a dark surface" (Tijo); "Leave shadows for the light mode!" (Supercharge Design). Design-system doctrine replaces shadow with tonal elevation on dark themes: Material's dark-theme model steps the base surface #121212 upward in lightness per elevation level (elevation 1 ≈ #1E1E1E, elevation 3 ≈ #272727), and Atlassian implements the same principle — "Shadows can be harder to see in dark mode, so dark mode elevations also rely on different surface colors… the higher the elevation, the lighter the surface looks." Honcho's glossary names elevation "the foundation that breaks first in dark mode." The mismatch is also a detection signature of automated generation: slop-detect scores "perma dark mode" and glow shadows among its rules.
- **Detection:** Page background luminance at or below roughly #1E1E1E combined with a dark-rgba `box-shadow` on cards (Tailwind `shadow-2xl` is `0 25px 50px -12px rgb(0 0 0 / 0.25)`) inside dark variants; the absence of any lighter "raised" surface token in the dark palette.
- **Valid exceptions:** Subtle shadows *in addition to* lighter raised surfaces (Atlassian keeps shadow paired with raised and overlay surfaces in dark mode "for added depth"); OLED-black media apps where elevation is conveyed by scrims and overlays.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Named surface tokens with tonal steps — `surface-base` → `surface-raised` → `surface-overlay`, each plus 3–6% lightness — with borders or scrims for separation; shadow reserved for true overlays (modals, dropdowns) at reduced opacity.
- **Supporting sources:** Tijo Creative, "Dark Mode UI Design Best Practices & Pitfalls" — https://tijocreative.com/articles/dark-mode-ui-design-best-practices-pitfalls-real-examples (2026-05-12); Supercharge Design, "6 Mistakes to Avoid in Dark UI Design" — https://supercharge.design/articles/6-mistakes-to-avoid-in-dark-ui-design (2026-06-11); ColorArchive, "Dark mode is not just inverted light mode" — https://colorarchive.me/notes/december-2026-dark-mode-design-decisions/ (2026-12-24); Honcho, "Elevation in a Design System" — https://honcho.agency/design-systems/glossary/elevation (2026-07-09); Atlassian Design System, Elevation — https://atlassian.design/foundations/elevation; ravidsrk/slop-detect — https://github.com/ravidsrk/slop-detect.
- **Confidence:** High — directly specified by Material dark-theme doctrine and Atlassian guidance, and the luminance-headroom mechanism is objective. (Material's own spec pages are JS-gated and were cited via secondary sources in the research notes.)

### Heavy and colored shadows on light UIs

- **Classification:** Avoid by default.
- **Visible symptoms:** Light pages where every card floats in `shadow-2xl`, 50px-blur darkness; purple, blue, or pink `box-shadow` glows under buttons and cards; hover states that only intensify an already-huge shadow.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** no-slop-ui hard-bans "dramatic box shadows (24px+ blur)" and "coloured glows on cards or buttons," prescribing at most `box-shadow: 0 2px 8px rgba(0,0,0,0.08)` for cards. Mature systems cap elevation sharply: the Australian Government AgDS defines only three shadow tokens, topping out at `0 16px 32px rgba(0,0,0,0.20)` for dropdowns and modals, with `0 2px 4px rgba(0,0,0,0.15)` as the card resting state. Large colored glows are an explicit AI pattern in Krebs's crowdsourced list and a scored rule ("big colored box-shadow glows (purple/blue/pink)") in slop-detect. The compositional harm: when every card is heavily elevated, elevation stops differentiating anything, the page acquires a restless over-lit quality, and shadow ink competes with content for attention.
- **Detection:** `box-shadow` blur radius above roughly 24px on resting-state cards; shadow colors with hue (Tailwind `shadow-(purple|blue|pink|cyan)-*`); every sibling card sharing the maximum shadow level.
- **Valid exceptions:** One focal element deliberately lifted in a marketing hero; hover transitions that *raise* elevation from a flat resting state — shadow communicating interactivity is a legitimate use ("use sparingly… indicating interaction," Basis).
- **Stronger alternatives:** Two to four shadow tokens maximum, mapped to roles (card resting at roughly 0 1–2px / 4–8px blur at 0.10 alpha or less; overlay and modal the largest); neutral black shadows, never brand-hue glows; border or tonal separation for resting cards.
- **Supporting sources:** no-slop-ui banned-patterns.md — https://github.com/LeoStehlik/no-slop-ui (2026-03); AgDS Elevation tokens — https://design-system.agriculture.gov.au/foundations/tokens/elevation; Adrian Krebs — https://adriankrebs.ch/blog/design-slop/ (2026-04-20); ravidsrk/slop-detect — https://github.com/ravidsrk/slop-detect; Basis Design System, Shadows — https://design.basis.com/foundations/shadows.
- **Confidence:** High — convergent across anti-slop rule sets, token-based systems, and the Show HN corpus analysis.

### No elevation model: flat versus raised carries no semantic meaning

- **Classification:** Avoid by default (systemic coherence failure); **Hard failure** only in specific compositions — for example, a modal or dropdown that does not visually out-elevate the card grid beneath it can fail to read as an overlay, and scroll-cropped content with no border or overflow cue hides that more content exists.
- **Visible symptoms:** Cards, popovers, modals, and sticky headers all sharing the same shadow-plus-border treatment; a dialog identical in "lift" to the cards behind the scrim; sidebars, wells, and content areas sitting at one surface level with hairline borders everywhere as the only structure.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Elevation is a z-axis vocabulary. Atlassian defines exactly four levels — sunken, default, raised, overlay — with role rules ("Raised… reserved for cards that can be moved"; "Overlay… reserved for UI that sits over another UI"), and a cross-system survey of design systems finds elevation levels "typically capping around 5 or 6," each mapped to a consistent visual treatment. Material-style elevation maps dp levels to roles, and a flat card carrying the same heavy shadow as a dialog is an explicit anti-pattern; real Material elevation is built from three stacked shadow layers (umbra, penumbra, ambient at 0.20/0.14/0.12 opacities), not one soft blur. When elevation carries no role meaning, users lose the depth cues that indicate draggability, transience (overlay versus content), and scroll state.
- **Detection:** Count distinct box-shadow and surface recipes against distinct surface *roles* — slop sites show one recipe for N roles; modal and card share a shadow class; no elevation token naming (`elevation.raised`, `shadow.overlay` absent); nested surfaces at identical lightness in dark mode.
- **Valid exceptions:** Intentionally flat systems — Carbon differentiates surfaces by contextual layer color tokens (`ui-01`/`ui-02`/`field-01`/`field-02`), not shadows; brutalist and editorial briefs where consistent flatness is the identity. Flatness itself is a system when deliberate.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Three to five named elevation levels with role mapping and paired surface-plus-shadow tokens (Atlassian's rule: "always pair matching surface and shadow tokens; don't mix"); overflow borders or shadows to signal scrollable regions; dark-mode tonal steps for the same roles.
- **Supporting sources:** Atlassian Design System, Elevation — https://atlassian.design/foundations/elevation; DesignSystems.surf, "Depth with Purpose: Elevation Design Patterns" — https://designsystems.surf/articles/depth-with-purpose-how-elevation-adds-realism-and-hierarchy (2026-07-15); oh-my-design-cli reference (Material elevation construction) — https://app.unpkg.com/oh-my-design-cli@1.8.6/files/skills/omd-feel/reference.md; Carbon Design System discussion #7701 — https://github.com/carbon-design-system/carbon/discussions/7701.
- **Confidence:** High for the system-level claim (design-system doctrine is unambiguous); the hard-failure escalation is situational and consensus-based, not research-measured.

### Cue stacking: border + shadow + gradient fill on one surface

- **Classification:** Avoid by default (redundant affordance, visual noise); Contextual risk for genuine hero or focal elements.
- **Visible symptoms:** A card that is simultaneously gradient-filled, bordered, and shadowed — sometimes additionally glass-blurred — three or four separation mechanisms firing where one would do; frequently combined with an inner `ring-1 ring-white/10` and an outer glow.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Each cue independently signals "this is a distinct surface"; stacking them signals indecision — the generator applied every cue from the training distribution rather than choosing a hierarchy. Vibe Code Kit makes the principle explicit: separate "in this order and **stop as soon as it reads**: whitespace → bg shift → elevation → border." Krebs's corpus treats gradient backgrounds plus glassmorphism cards as compounding patterns in heavy-slop sites. Gradient fills behind text also degrade contrast consistency across the surface — WCAG 1.4.11 testing guidance requires evaluating the *least*-contrasting area of a gradient.
- **Detection:** A single element whose computed style has `border` + `box-shadow` + `background-image: linear-gradient` (often plus `backdrop-filter`); the Tailwind triple `border … shadow-… bg-gradient-to-…` in one class list.
- **Valid exceptions:** One focal surface per view (a hero card, a key CTA panel) where maximal differentiation is the intent; sanctioned pairs exist — Atlassian pairs a raised surface with a raised shadow. The failure is undifferentiated triple- and quad-stacking on every surface.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Pick one primary separation mechanism per surface role; never combine gradient fill with border and shadow on the same box; spend the gradient budget on a single focal element.
- **Supporting sources:** Vibe Code Kit — https://vibecodekit.dev/ai-slop-design (2026-06-22); Adrian Krebs — https://adriankrebs.ch/blog/design-slop/ (2026-04-20); W3C, Understanding WCAG SC 1.4.11 — https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/non-text-contrast.html; Atlassian Design System, Elevation — https://atlassian.design/foundations/elevation.
- **Confidence:** Medium-high — strong doctrinal and community support; "redundancy harms comprehension" is consensus design reasoning, not directly measured.

### Colored accent border strips on cards (`border-l-4`, colored top edge)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default — the strongest single AI tell per community evidence; Contextual risk when the strip encodes real state.
- **Visible symptoms:** A 3–4px colored strip along the left or top edge of otherwise plain cards, in brand purple or blue, repeated on feature cards, alerts, and stat cards alike.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Krebs opens his analysis with a designer's observation that colored left borders are "almost as reliable a sign of AI-generated design as em-dashes for text," and his automated run treats "colored borders on cards, on the top or left edge" as a scored pattern. Vibe Code Kit's rule: "Save the colored left strip for true semantic state, never decoration." The honest labeling matters here: the harm is credibility and genericness, not usability — this pattern is merely oversaturated unless it masquerades as status it does not encode.
- **Detection:** `border-l-4` or `border-t-4` (or `border-left: 4px solid <brand hue>`) on generic cards; strip color unrelated to any status semantics; present on more than half of cards.
- **Valid exceptions:** Status and severity encoding (error, warning, success strips); category color-coding with a real legend; callout and admonition components in documentation.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A small colored icon or badge with a semantic label; a borderless card with a tonal shift; a colored chip instead of an edge strip when categorization is needed.
- **Supporting sources:** Adrian Krebs — https://adriankrebs.ch/blog/design-slop/ (2026-04-20); Chyshkala, "67% of Show HN Submissions Now Carry AI Design Fingerprints" — https://chyshkala.com/blog/67-of-show-hn-submissions-now-carry-ai-design-fingerprints (2026-04-23); Vibe Code Kit — https://vibecodekit.dev/ai-slop-design (2026-06-22).
- **Confidence:** High as an oversaturation and AI-tell claim (corpus-verified plus designer consensus); the harm is genericness, not usability.

### Inconsistent surface treatment across sibling components

- **Classification:** Avoid by default (coherence failure).
- **Visible symptoms:** Cards with mixed radius values (8px next to 24px); some cards bordered, some shadowed, some both; inputs with different radius and border treatment than buttons in the same form; one-off `rounded-[13px]` or `shadow-[0_7px_30px_rgba(0,0,0,0.12)]` arbitrary values scattered through the codebase.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Design tokens exist precisely to prevent this. Polaris lints against hardcoded radius units — "Use Polaris shape tokens instead of custom styles so that border is consistent across the Admin" — and elevation levels should be "a token… never an arbitrary blur radius and offset" (Honcho). Arbitrary one-off values are the fingerprint of per-prompt generation with no persistent design decisions — Vibe Code Kit's diagnosis: "The root cause of slop is no decision… lock its tokens."
- **Detection:** Extract all `border-radius` and `box-shadow` computed values page-wide; more than two or three distinct values with no token mapping, or Tailwind arbitrary-value syntax (`rounded-[…]`, `shadow-[…]`) in markup, indicates no system.
- **Valid exceptions:** Documented density tiers (compact data tables versus marketing sections) governed by named variants.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Radius, elevation, and border token scales consumed by every component; a `DESIGN.md` or theme file as the single source of truth.
- **Supporting sources:** Shopify Polaris stylelint border rules — https://polaris-react.shopify.com/tools/stylelint-polaris/rules/border-declaration-property-unit-disallowed-list; Honcho, "Elevation in a Design System" — https://honcho.agency/design-systems/glossary/elevation (2026-07-09); Vibe Code Kit — https://vibecodekit.dev/ai-slop-design (2026-06-22).
- **Confidence:** High — consistency is the minimal agreed purpose of design systems, and the detection method is objective.

**Cluster 3 — Spacing and grouping failures that manifest as surface problems.** These four patterns come from hierarchy and spacing research, but reviewers encounter them as surface defects: sections that read as identical train cars, density regimes that clash between marketing and product, concentric frames of nested cards, and whitespace that carries no grouping information. (Adjacent hierarchy failures — flat equal-weight sections, the default three-column icon grid, competing CTAs — are covered in their own section and referenced here only where they compound.)

### Monotonous vertical rhythm: every section padded identically (`py-24 sm:py-32` on repeat)

- **Classification:** Contextual risk — the honest reading of the evidence is that uniform spacing is not a usability failure; it is a provenance tell and a forfeited compositional tool, becoming a real problem only when combined with flat hierarchy (uniform padding + uniform type + uniform grids = total monotony).
- **Visible symptoms:** Six to ten consecutive `<section>`s, each `py-24 sm:py-32` (or `py-20`), each with the same `mx-auto max-w-7xl px-6 lg:px-8` container, alternating only their background shade (`bg-white` / `bg-gray-50`). Scrolling feels like passing identical train cars.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** The provenance is exact: this class string is Tailwind UI marketing-section boilerplate — `<div class="mx-auto max-w-2xl px-4 py-16 sm:px-6 sm:py-24 lg:max-w-7xl lg:px-8">` appears verbatim in Tailwind UI components and thousands of tutorials — and LLMs reproduce the modal example of their training corpus. The compositional argument is craft consensus rather than lab evidence: rhythm in page design works like rhythm in typography — repetition creates predictability, and variation within the system creates emphasis and pacing (the vertical-rhythm and 8pt-grid literature). A page with zero cadence variation forfeits pacing as a signal, the spacing equivalent of monotone speech. No study shows users are measurably harmed by uniform section padding, and this section says so plainly.
- **Detection:** Grep for `py-24|py-20|py-32` recurring on every section; identical container class strings repeated; sections differing only by background utility. A computed-style audit flags identical section padding-top and padding-bottom values across more than three consecutive sections.
- **Valid exceptions:** Systematic marketing pages where uniform bands are a deliberate minimalist system; long documentation; any design where variation would fight the brand.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Keep the spacing *scale* but vary the *values* by section role (tight for supportive content, generous around the key conversion moment); use spacing asymmetry to group related sections and separate acts of the page; occasionally break the grid with a full-bleed moment.
- **Supporting sources:** monet.design, Next.js landing-page guide (the `py-24 sm:py-32` / `max-w-7xl` signature in the wild) — https://monet.design/blog/posts/nextjs-landing-page-guide-with-monet (2025-12-11); Bryll, "8-Point Grid: Vertical Rhythm" — https://medium.com/built-to-adapt/8-point-grid-vertical-rhythm-90d05ad95032 (2017); spec.fm/prototypr, "The 8pt Grid" — https://blog.prototypr.io/the-8pt-grid-consistent-spacing-in-ui-design-with-sketch-577e4f0fd520; Adrian Krebs — https://adriankrebs.ch/blog/design-slop/ (2026-04-20).
- **Confidence:** Medium-high that this is a reliable AI/template tell; low-medium for any claimed user harm — this is an originality and craft critique, not a usability defect.

### Density incoherence: cramped dashboards bolted onto airy marketing pages (or vice versa)

- **Classification:** Contextual risk; escalates to **Hard failure** at the extremes — interactive targets below 24×24 CSS px fail WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8, and data tools so padded that key content scrolls away fail their users' tasks.
- **Visible symptoms:** The marketing site breathes (`py-24`, 72px type), then logging in drops into a screen where twenty KPI cards in `text-xs` fight for the viewport with `p-2` padding and 8px gaps — or the inverse: an analytics product whose working screens are padded like a landing page, showing four numbers per screenful. The two surfaces look designed by different companies.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Density is not an aesthetic dial; it is information-per-moment matched to the user's task and expertise (Matt Ström's UI-density essay distinguishes visual density from information density; Bloomberg Terminal is legitimately dense because its expert users need high data-ink ratios). AI generators apply the same component defaults everywhere: landing-page spacing leaks into work surfaces, or card-grid compression leaks into reading surfaces. The hard floor is normative: WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8 Target Size (Minimum) requires 24×24 CSS px at Level AA, with comfortable targets at 44×44 (SC 2.5.5 AAA, Apple HIG) or 48×48dp (Material); dense tables with tiny icon buttons are the usual violators. Practitioner consensus (community-grade, not settled science) also recommends chunking related metrics and limiting simultaneous KPIs, commonly citing Miller's 7±2.
- **Detection:** A cross-surface audit comparing the spacing and type scales between `/` (marketing) and `/app` (product) — divergent scales with no token relationship. Cramped-surface heuristics: computed padding or gap under 8px on repeated cards; interactive elements with a bounding box under 24×24px; `text-[10px]` or `text-xs` used for primary data. Over-air heuristics in tools: first meaningful data row below the fold at 1080p; tables wrapped in `py-24` sections.
- **Valid exceptions:** A deliberate two-register brand (editorial marketing plus professional tool) is fine *if* the product surface's density is intentional, internally consistent, and accessible. Bloomberg-style density is correct for expert terminals.
- **Stronger alternatives:** One spacing scale (shared tokens) with different *roles* per surface; progressive disclosure (summary KPIs to drill-down) instead of either wall-of-cards or one-stat-per-screen; target-size minimums enforced in the component library so density never goes sub-accessible.
- **Supporting sources:** Matt Ström, "UI Density" — https://mattstromawn.com/writing/ui-density/ (2024-05-20); W3C, WCAG 2.2 SC 2.5.8 Target Size (Minimum) — https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/#target-size-minimum; touch-target guidance summary (Apple HIG 44pt / Material 48dp) — https://www.zachswebdesigns.com/touch-target-size-guidelines-minimum-pixel-sizes-for-mobile-ui-elements/; dashboard chunking practitioner consensus — https://moldstud.com/articles/p-five-common-mistakes-bi-developers-make-with-looker-and-how-to-avoid-them.
- **Confidence:** Medium — the density-matching principle is well-argued craft theory and the target-size floor is a hard standard; the specific "AI products get this wrong" claim is observational consensus.

### Card-in-card-in-card nesting

- **Classification:** Avoid by default — a visual-noise failure with a clean gestalt explanation; rarely a hard break.
- **Visible symptoms:** A bordered, shadowed page panel containing a bordered card, which contains smaller bordered stat tiles, which contain pill badges — three or four nested rounded-rectangle boundaries, each with its own padding, so content shrinks into concentric frames; double borders 1–2px apart; "picture frame inside a picture frame."
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** The gestalt law of common region (Palmer & Rock) says elements inside a shared boundary are perceived as one group — so every border is a grouping *statement*, and nested boundaries make nested claims, each additional frame consuming pixels and attention to restate what proximity already said for free. The result is figure-ground noise: boundaries competing with the content they contain. Detector and critique consensus is uniform: Impeccable's AI-slop list names "nested cards" a top tell and prescribes "flatten the hierarchy: use spacing, typography, and dividers instead of nesting"; slop-detect rule 25 detects a card-like element inside a card-like ancestor deterministically; Material Design guidance has long discouraged nesting cards within cards. The cost is concrete: three 16–24px paddings consume 100+px of content width, and depth ambiguity worsens when shadows stack.
- **Detection:** DOM heuristic — any element matching the card signature (border-radius plus border or box-shadow plus non-transparent background) with an ancestor matching the same signature (slop-detect's exact approach). Tailwind grep: `rounded-xl border` inside a parent that also has `rounded-xl border` or `shadow`. Visual: count concentric boundaries around the deepest content; two or more nested frames is a flag.
- **Valid exceptions:** Genuine containment semantics — a modal over a card surface; a code block or media frame inside a card; drag-and-drop kanban (card inside column) where nesting *is* the data model; one level of nesting as a deliberate highlight (a featured stat inside a panel). Two levels deep is where noise begins.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Flatten: one surface per conceptual group, then use spacing, dividers (`divide-y`, `hr`), and typographic contrast for internal structure; background tint *or* border *or* shadow — one grouping cue, not three; let proximity, the cheapest gestalt cue, do the work.
- **Supporting sources:** Impeccable slop detector and README — https://impeccable.style/slop and https://github.com/pbakaus/impeccable; ravidsrk/slop-detect (rule 25) — https://github.com/ravidsrk/slop-detect; Laws of UX, Law of Common Region — https://lawsofux.com/law-of-common-region/; UX StackExchange, "How to avoid the use of nested cards" — https://ux.stackexchange.com/questions/142595/how-to-avoid-the-use-of-nested-cards (2022).
- **Confidence:** High — an empirically confirmed AI tell with uniform anti-nesting guidance across detector projects, design systems, and gestalt theory; the harm is noise and cramping, not breakage.

### Unintentional whitespace: padding everywhere, intention nowhere

- **Classification:** Contextual risk — the meta-pattern behind the previous three; harmless alone, corrosive in aggregate.
- **Visible symptoms:** Whitespace that comes from component defaults rather than decisions: identical card padding (`p-6`) regardless of content, gaps between unrelated elements equal to gaps between related ones, section padding identical down the page, and "empty" areas that are just the grid's leftovers. Related elements drift apart (a title far from its card) while unrelated elements sit adjacently.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Proximity is the signal — gestalt proximity means spacing *relationships* are how users parse what belongs together, pre-attentively. When internal spacing approximately equals external spacing (violating the classic "internal ≤ external" spacing rule of thumb), grouping information is destroyed even though the page looks "clean." Whitespace is also functional rather than decorative: Lin (2004), "Reading online text: A comparison of four whitespace layouts," found that margin whitespace conditions affected reading speed and comprehension — the study behind the widely cited "whitespace increases comprehension ~20%" claim, which should be cited with care since the 20% figure travels via secondary sources; the underlying finding, that whitespace manipulations measurably affect reading performance, is solid. The AI failure mode is that models emit the modal spacing token rather than a spacing *decision* — hence uniform `gap-8` between everything. The reviewer sees a page that is neither cramped nor airy but unauthored.
- **Detection:** Compare intra-group versus inter-group gaps in computed styles and flag when they are equal; list all distinct padding values on cards and sections (one value everywhere means default-driven); check whether section padding correlates with section importance.
- **Valid exceptions:** Strictly systematized minimal designs where a single spacing value is the concept.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Spacing scales with intent (4/8/16/24/48/96 assigned by relationship, not habit); the "space belongs to the relationship" rule — tighten inside groups, loosen between them; deliberate whitespace asymmetry to build focal points.
- **Supporting sources:** Lin, D.-Y., "Reading online text: A comparison of four whitespace layouts" — https://www.academia.edu/127468257/Reading_online_text_A_comparison_of_four_whitespace_layouts (2004); Babich, N., "The Power of Whitespace," UX Planet — https://uxplanet.org/the-power-of-whitespace-a1a95e45f82b (2017-06-30); Cieden, spacing best practices (internal ≤ external rule) — https://cieden.com/book/sub-atomic/spacing/spacing-best-practices; Laws of UX, Law of Common Region — https://lawsofux.com/law-of-common-region/; Matt Ström, "UI Density" — https://mattstromawn.com/writing/ui-density/ (2024-05-20).
- **Confidence:** Medium-high — grounded in gestalt theory and reading research, but "AI output specifically has unintentional spacing" is practitioner observation, not a measured study.

## 5. Imagery, Illustration, and Icon Failures

Imagery is where AI-generated sites most visibly betray their origins: emoji standing in for an icon system, a shared default illustration shipped in its factory color, and hero renders whose only subject is "technology." Some of these patterns are harmless stylistically but objectively broken functionally — screen readers announce emoji by their literal Unicode names, icon-only buttons announce as bare "button," and decorative images without alt semantics violate WCAG at Level A. Others are differentiation failures: they make a site interchangeable with the statistical median of every other AI-built site. The ten patterns below are classified accordingly — Hard failure where usability, accessibility, or honesty breaks; Avoid by default where the harm is to brand identity or signals an unexamined default; Contextual risk where the brief can legitimately support the pattern.

### Emoji-as-Icon in Feature Lists and UI Copy

- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **Visible symptoms:** Feature cards and lists where every item is prefixed with an emoji (U+1F680 ROCKET, U+26A1 HIGH VOLTAGE, U+2705 CHECK MARK BUTTON, U+1F512 LOCK, U+1F4A1 LIGHT BULB); emoji in section headings; "checkmark" lists built from U+2705 characters instead of CSS list styling; emoji-only status indicators. Extremely common in AI-generated landing pages and README files.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** When an emoji carries meaning — as a bullet marker, feature icon, or status indicator — screen readers do not announce the intended meaning; they announce the emoji's official Unicode name. Pope Tech's testing gives concrete failures: U+1F64F FOLDED HANDS announced as "hands pressed together" (intended: thank you), U+1F6A9 TRIANGULAR FLAG announced as "triangular flag on post" (intended: warning), U+1F44A RAISED FIST announced as "closed fist." A flag-as-warning therefore reads as a random fact rather than a warning — a deterministic, not stylistic, failure. Repetition compounds it: screen readers announce repeated emoji one by one ("cake cake cake cake cake"), and users abandon the content (Veronica Lewis, 2025; Aaardvark Accessibility, 2026). Peer-reviewed work corroborates the mechanism: Tigwell, Gorman & Menzies, "Emoji Accessibility for Visually Impaired People" (CHI 2020), documents misunderstanding and access problems for visually impaired users. Long emoji strings also increase processing load for people with cognitive disabilities (Pope Tech).
- **Detection:** In a design review, look for emoji used as list markers, heading decoration, or status indicators rather than as tonal decoration inside complete sentences. In code, grep the built HTML (not just source — LLM generators bake emoji into JSX and templates) for emoji Unicode ranges `[\u{1F300}-\u{1FAFF}\u{2600}-\u{27BF}]` (regex with the `u` flag) inside `<li>`, `<h1>`–`<h6>`, `<button>`, or table cells; flag any emoji appearing without `aria-hidden="true"` and without `role="img" aria-label="..."`; flag emoji list markers with no `list-style` CSS behind them.
- **Valid exceptions:** Informal or social copy where the emoji is purely tonal and the sentence reads correctly without it; brand voices that deliberately use emoji in marketing copy with accessible wrapping.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Real SVG icons with visible text labels (see "Icon-Only Controls Without Labels" below). If emoji are kept, wrap them: `<span aria-hidden="true">[emoji]</span> Fast`, and where the emoji alone carries meaning, add `role="img" aria-label="..."` or a screen-reader-only span — patterns documented by Pope Tech and by Scott Vinkle, "SVG and Emoji Icon Accessibility" (Aug 26, 2019).
- **Supporting sources:** Pope Tech, "Making emojis and icons screen reader accessible," April 2026 — https://blog.pope.tech/2026/04/01/making-emojis-and-icons-screen-reader-accessible/; Veronica Lewis, "Emoji Accessibility And Low Vision," Jan 6, 2025 — https://veroniiiica.com/emoji-and-low-vision/; Aaardvark Accessibility, "The Good, The Bad, And The Ugly: Emojis and Accessibility," 2026 — https://aaardvarkaccessibility.com/the-good-the-bad-and-the-ugly-emojis-and-accessibility/; Tigwell, Gorman & Menzies, "Emoji Accessibility for Visually Impaired People," CHI 2020, cited via CUNY Accessibility Toolkit, "Emoji Accessibility" — https://guides.cuny.edu/accessibility/memeEmoji; Scott Vinkle, "SVG and Emoji Icon Accessibility," Aug 26, 2019 — https://scottvinkle.com/blogs/work/svg-and-emoji-icon-accessibility
- **Confidence:** High — multiple independent accessibility sources plus a CHI 2020 paper converge, and the mechanism (literal Unicode-name announcement) is deterministic, not opinion.

### Icon-Only Controls Without Visible Labels

- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **Visible symptoms:** Navbars and toolbars composed of bare glyph buttons; feature cards where a lone outline icon is expected to explain the feature; icon buttons whose meaning is revealed only via a hover tooltip.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** NN/g's usability research shows "universal" icons are rare — home, print, and the search magnifier are the exceptions — because most icons' meanings vary across interfaces (heart versus star for favorites, bookmarks, ratings; hamburger used for lists). In one NN/g study, not a single participant clicked a clock icon used non-standardly for "history": obscure icon, wasted feature. NN/g's guideline is that a text label must accompany icons and be visible at all times — hover-only labels fail on touch — citing Tognazzini's dictum "a word is worth a thousand pictures." NN/g's icon-testing framework adds four quality criteria (findability, recognition, information scent, attractiveness) with out-of-context recognition tests and in-context time-to-locate tests for validating any genuinely space-constrained exception. On the accessibility axis, an icon-only `<button>` with no accessible name is announced as just "button" — an invisible button (Pope Tech).
- **Detection:** In a design review, count controls whose meaning disappears if the tooltip never fires. In code, flag any `<button>` or `<a>` whose only child is an `<svg>` with no `aria-label`, `title`, or visually hidden text; nav items labeled only by tooltip (`title=` as the sole name); Lucide or Feather SVGs (see "Icon-Library Monoculture" below) inside buttons without names. Automated: the axe-core `button-name` rule catches the worst cases.
- **Valid exceptions:** Genuinely standardized glyphs in context — magnifier inside a search field, X-to-close, play/pause in a media player — which still require an accessible name; dense professional toolbars validated by user testing per NN/g's own testing guidance.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Icon plus visible text label (NN/g's default recommendation). For space-constrained UI, icon plus `aria-label` plus a tooltip as a supplement — never as the only carrier of meaning.
- **Supporting sources:** Nielsen Norman Group, "Icon Usability" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/icon-usability/ (byline/date not displayed in fetched copy); Nielsen Norman Group, "Icon Testing" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/icon-testing/ (byline/date not displayed in fetched copy); Pope Tech, "Making emojis and icons screen reader accessible," April 2026 — https://blog.pope.tech/2026/04/01/making-emojis-and-icons-screen-reader-accessible/
- **Confidence:** High — direct NN/g usability-study evidence plus WCAG-adjacent accessible-name requirements.

### Missing or Wrong Alt Semantics for Decorative Imagery

- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **Visible symptoms:** Hero and section images with no `alt` attribute at all; junk alt values like `alt="image"`, `alt="hero"`, or `alt="Screenshot 2026-..."`; verbose machine-generated alt text ("A stunning 3D render of abstract purple shapes floating...") on images that carry no information; decorative inline SVGs without `aria-hidden`.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** This is a normative standard violation, not a taste judgment — WCAG 2.x SC 1.1.1 (Non-text Content) is Level A. The W3C WAI Images Tutorial is explicit that purely decorative images must use a null alt (`alt=""`) so assistive technology skips them; `role="presentation"` is a less-supported alternative. If the `alt` attribute is absent rather than empty, screen readers may read the file name aloud — a "major distraction" (UAB ALT Text Guidelines). AI-generated sites introduce a characteristic third failure: alt text that is technically present but functionally useless, such as `alt="A beautiful modern illustration of..."` on a purely decorative asset — the generator has described the image instead of deciding its role. Unlabeled decorative SVGs and emoji inherit the failures of the two patterns above.
- **Detection:** In code, flag `<img>` without `alt`; junk alt values matching `^(image|img|photo|hero|banner|untitled)` or a filename pattern `\.(png|jpe?g|webp|avif)`; `<svg>` elements without `aria-hidden="true"`, `role="img"`, or a `<title>`; heroes implemented as CSS backgrounds (no alt is possible — the image must be truly decorative). In a design review, ask of every image: does removing it lose information? If not, its alt should be empty, not descriptive.
- **Valid exceptions:** Informative images — product shots, charts, team photos — need real, concise alt text; an image whose content repeats adjacent text should get `alt=""` per the W3C tutorial's Example 3.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Decide decorative versus informative per image using the W3C alt decision tree; `alt=""` for decoration, task-focused alt for information; `aria-hidden="true"` on decorative SVGs and emoji.
- **Supporting sources:** W3C WAI, "Images Tutorial — Decorative Images" — https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/images/decorative/ (undated tutorial); UAB Disability Support, "ALT Text Guidelines" (PDF) — https://www.uab.edu/students/disability/images/Documents/ALT_Text_Guidelines.pdf (undated); Pope Tech, "Making emojis and icons screen reader accessible," April 2026 — https://blog.pope.tech/2026/04/01/making-emojis-and-icons-screen-reader-accessible/
- **Confidence:** High — normative standard (WCAG Level A) plus explicit W3C tutorial guidance.

### AI "Tech Aesthetic" Hero Imagery: Glossy 3D Blobs and Purple-Blue Renders

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** Abstract glossy or glassy 3D shapes — blobs, toruses, chrome ribbons — floating on gradient backgrounds; purple-to-blue "fintech glow"; isometric scenes of tiny people interacting with giant UI cards; "dashboard floating in space" renders. Lighting is uniformly soft, materials uniformly wet-looking, composition centered and empty of information.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Mass producibility is the defining property of slop: the academic framing (Kommers et al., ACM AI Letters, Jan 2026) names "superficial competence, asymmetric effort and mass producibility" as slop's prototypical properties, and philosopher Jonathan Gilmore describes the material as having an "incredibly banal, realistic style" — when hero art is infinitely cheap, identical hero art is what every site gets. Beyond sameness, the trope miscommunicates: Better Images of AI, a non-profit created specifically to combat clichéd tech imagery, calls out "humanoid robots, glowing brains, outstretched robot hands, blue backgrounds, and the Terminator" as tropes that are "not just overworked, they can be surprisingly unhelpful," arguing that abstract sci-fi imagery "hinders the understanding of the technology," "set[s] unrealistic expectations," and can "mask the accountability" of the humans behind products. The imagery is also functionally inert: NN/g eyetracking shows users skip "big feel-good images that are purely decorative" and attend to information-carrying ones. And the color language itself is a convergence marker — practitioner observers note blue has become "the default... overused to the point of meaninglessness in SaaS" (metabrand.digital, 2026). There is a secondary performance cost when these renders become the LCP element (see "Oversized Decorative Hero Imagery" below), and a misrepresentation risk when an abstract render purports to explain the product.
- **Detection:** In the asset itself: no photographic EXIF/metadata; unnaturally uniform specular highlights; physically impossible geometry or lighting; nonsensical background text or UI glyphs inside "dashboard" renders; smooth plastic skin and objects. In code: hero `<img>` with generic filename in a decorative role; alt text describing abstraction ("3D render of abstract shapes"); the same image family recycled across hero, features, and CTA sections; filenames containing `midjourney`, `dalle`, `sdxl`, `flux`, or `generated`.
- **Valid exceptions:** A brand system deliberately built on a distinctive 3D language with real art direction — consistent materials, brand palette, repeated motifs — for example a company whose product is 3D; abstract art where the brief is explicitly atmospheric. Even then, keep such imagery out of information-bearing slots.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Product screenshots or UI photographs (information-carrying imagery is exactly what NN/g found users attend to); commissioned illustration under a documented style system; diagrams that explain the mechanism. If AI imagery is used, art-direct it into a consistent, brand-colored, non-default style and disclose where appropriate.
- **Supporting sources:** Wikipedia, "AI slop" (aggregating Kommers et al., ACM AI Letters, Jan 2026; Simon Willison, May 8, 2024; Guardian and NYT coverage) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AI_slop; Better Images of AI — https://betterimagesofai.org/; Nielsen Norman Group, "Photos as Web Content" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/photos-as-web-content/ (~2010); metabrand.digital, "B2B SaaS Branding," Jan 4, 2026 — https://www.metabrand.digital/guides/startup-branding-guide/b2b-saas-branding
- **Confidence:** Medium-high — "ignored when decorative" is research-backed eyetracking; "feels generic / slop signal" is broad, documented community consensus (up to Word-of-the-Year-level discourse), not lab research.

### Uncanny AI-Generated People: Fake Customers, Fake Teams, Fake Testimonials

- **Classification:** Hard failure when AI people stand in for real humans the site claims exist (team pages, testimonials, "our customers") — that is deception; otherwise Avoid by default given documented backlash and uncanny-valley response
- **Visible symptoms:** Over-smooth, symmetric faces; dead-eyed smiles; artifact zones — hands, ears, teeth, glasses frames merging into skin; six fingers; strange wires and straps; background figures with incomplete faces; a "diverse team grid" where every face shares identical lighting and skin texture.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Uncanny-valley backlash is documented with named brands and dates. McDonald's Japan's AI fries ad (Aug 2024) had viewers fixating on a woman appearing to have six fingers; the ad was called "creepy" and "disturbing" (cascaded.ai). NetEase Cloud Music's AI ad showing a woman's neck rotated 180 degrees drew mass complaints and a customer-service apology (aibase, Apr 2026). ID@Xbox's Dec 2023 AI image promoting indie games showed "uncanny telltales" — incomplete faces, odd wires — and the backlash was hostile enough that the post was deleted, with observers noting that using AI instead of paying artists was "antithetical" to the message (Jisc National Centre for AI). Coca-Cola's 2024 AI holiday ads were "widely dismissed as soulless," and H&M, Skechers, Guess, and Toys "R" Us all faced backlash for AI avatars or AI brand film in place of human models (neuron.expert; corroborated by Wikipedia "AI slop" citing NYT and IGN). The deception precedent is Willy's Chocolate Experience (Glasgow, Feb 2024): AI-generated promotional imagery sold tickets to a real event that did not resemble it — now a canonical case of AI imagery as consumer deception (Wikipedia "AI slop," citing The Register and Vanity Fair). The usability layer reinforces this: NN/g eyetracking shows users scrutinize photos of real people and ignore generic or model people — authenticity of people photos is precisely the dimension users evaluate.
- **Detection:** Inspect at 100% zoom for artifacts — hands, ears, jewelry, background text. Check for identical lighting and depth-of-field across supposedly different people; reverse-image searches that return nothing while the face looks model-perfect; no photographer credit anywhere on a "team" page. AI-detector tools are a weak signal only — unreliable, never treat as proof.
- **Valid exceptions:** Obviously stylized illustrated or 3D characters that do not claim to be photographs; disclosed synthetic influencers as an explicit creative concept; placeholder imagery clearly marked as such in mockups.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Real photos of real people — NN/g found this is the imagery users actually study; consistent illustrated characters, which are brand-owned and cannot be uncanny; or no people imagery at all.
- **Supporting sources:** cascaded.ai, "7 Rules to Avoid AI Ad Backlash," May 19, 2026 — https://cascaded.ai/en/blog/gen-ai-ad; aibase, "NetEase Cloud Music AI advertisement caused a backlash due to the uncanny valley," Apr 14, 2026 — https://news.aibase.com/news/27096; Jisc National Centre for AI, "Considering image generation and education today," Mar 11, 2024 — https://nationalcentreforai.jiscinvolve.org/wp/2024/03/11/considering-image-generation-and-education-today/; neuron.expert, "Brands Embrace Authenticity Amid AI Backlash in Advertising" — https://neuron.expert/news/big-brands-are-cashing-in-on-your-ai-ick/14932/en/; Wikipedia, "AI slop" (citing The Register, Feb 28, 2024; Vanity Fair, Feb 29, 2024; NYT, Nov 20, 2024; IGN, Nov 15, 2025) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AI_slop; Nielsen Norman Group, "Photos as Web Content" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/photos-as-web-content/ (~2010)
- **Confidence:** High — the backlash cases are dated, named, and multiply reported, and deceptive fake team or testimonial imagery is an honesty failure independent of aesthetics.

### Generic Stock Photography: Handshakes, Headsets, and Laptops Full of Green Code

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** The anonymous handshake over a glossy desk; the over-cheerful headset operator; models laughing at a laptop; a laptop screen full of green code as the visual for "software"; puzzle pieces and lightbulbs for "solutions" and "ideas."
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** Users measurably ignore it. NN/g eyetracking: "Some types of pictures are completely ignored... typically big feel-good images that are purely decorative," while photos of products and real people are scrutinized — "on the web, jazzed-up = ignored." The clichés themselves are named specimens in practitioner literature: "the overly-cheerful person with a headset, the anonymous handshake over a glossy table, or the group of models laughing at a laptop," with the warning that visitors "have likely seen the exact same photos... on dozens of other websites. It undermines your credibility" (SeniorMarketSales, 2025); "The woman smiling at a laptop? The handshake across a desk? The 'team huddle in a modern office'? These are visual cliches. They add nothing" (kapa99, 2026); lightbulb and businesswoman imagery as "easy, and lazy" visual language (Cova Communications, 2023). The failure mechanism is therefore twofold: the image consumes layout and bandwidth while being skipped, and its recognizability as stock actively signals that no one invested in the brand.
- **Detection:** In code, look for hotlinked or re-hosted stock: `images.unsplash.com`, `images.pexels.com`, `media.istockphoto.com`, `shutterstock`, or `freepik` in `src` attributes or CSS; filename patterns like `pexels-`, `unsplash-`, `stock-`. In a design review, run a reverse-image search (Google Lens, TinEye) — hundreds of prior uses confirms the signal — and be suspicious of model-perfect "employees" on an About page with no names.
- **Valid exceptions:** Genuinely contextual stock used sparingly on content sites or blogs under budget constraints; stock as raw material substantially re-composited into a brand system.
- **Stronger alternatives:** One real photo shoot of the actual team and product — NN/g notes "a great photographer can add a fortune to your website's business value"; product screenshots; commissioned illustration; or simply no image where the image adds nothing.
- **Supporting sources:** Nielsen Norman Group, "Photos as Web Content" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/photos-as-web-content/ (~2010); SeniorMarketSales, "Insurance Agent Website SEO: 10 Mistakes Costing You Traffic and Leads," Jun 18, 2025 — https://www.seniormarketsales.com/blog/10-mistakes-on-your-website; kapa99, "Online Course Design: Make Your Content Look Premium," Mar 5, 2026 — https://kapa99.com/blog/coach-course-design/; Cova Communications, "Visual cliches to avoid in a presentation," Apr 11, 2023 — https://covacommunications.com/blog/visual-cliches-to-avoid-in-a-presentation
- **Confidence:** High — the "ignored" claim is eyetracking research; the "cliché undermines credibility" claim is consistent practitioner consensus.

### Icon-Library Monoculture: Lucide/Feather Outlines for Everything, No Brand Illustration Style

- **Classification:** Contextual risk
- **Visible symptoms:** Every icon on the site drawn in the same 2px-stroke 24×24 outline style: rocket for "fast," shield-check for "secure," zap for "powerful," bar-chart for "analytics." Feature sections become interchangeable across thousands of sites. Often mixed carelessly with emoji (see "Emoji-as-Icon" above) or with a second icon family at a different stroke weight.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** The failure is default-ness and the total absence of a brand-owned visual layer — not usability; Lucide is a well-made, internally consistent library, and this pattern alone breaks nothing functionally. The monoculture is documented: shadcn/ui pairs with Lucide as its default icon system (shadcnstudio, 2026), and the Tailwind-plus-shadcn ecosystem drives visible convergence — "when thousands of developers are using similar combinations of tools, patterns, and components, convergence becomes inevitable... The same card layouts, the same navigation patterns, the same hero sections" (Ross O'Neill, 2025). A controlled test building the same SaaS app in v0, Bolt, Lovable, and a fourth AI builder produced structurally similar dashboards, all Tailwind/shadcn-family output (DesignRevision, Dec 2025) — same prompt, same output across builders. NN/g's branding rationale supplies the principle: icons "convey brand personality through color and style" only after they communicate meaning, and a library everyone shares conveys no one's personality. Anti-slop design guidance now explicitly targets this look: "Do not default to generic developer-style icon packs or bland Lucide-like icon vibes" (explainx.ai skill listing, 2026 — note that this source belongs to the same taste-skill ecosystem as this report's host project and is cited as evidence the concern is codified, not as independent validation).
- **Detection:** In code: `lucide-react`, `lucide`, or `react-icons` (`fi` = Feather) in `package.json`; markup like `class="lucide lucide-rocket"`; the SVG signature `viewBox="0 0 24 24" fill="none" stroke="currentColor" stroke-width="2" stroke-linecap="round" stroke-linejoin="round"` (Lucide/Feather defaults); vanilla `data-lucide="rocket"` attributes or `feather.replace()` calls; zero custom SVG assets in `/public` or `/assets`. In a design review: the same icon names and motifs as competitor sites.
- **Valid exceptions:** Application UI, admin panels, and dev tools, where predictability aids use — Ross O'Neill's counterpoint is that standard components ship with accessibility "baked in" and familiar patterns reduce cognitive load; early-stage MVPs where speed legitimately beats differentiation.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Keep the library for UI chrome but commission or design a small custom set — or a distinctive treatment such as a custom stroke, duotone, or brand-colored fills — for marketing and feature surfaces; one library only, one weight, one size scale; document an icon grid in the design system.
- **Supporting sources:** shadcnstudio, "Designing Websites with Shadcn UI and Lucide Icons," Apr 2026 — https://shadcnstudio.com/blog/shadcn-ui-and-lucide-icons/; Ross O'Neill, "Do Modern Frameworks Make All Websites Look the Same?", Aug 4, 2025 — https://ross-oneill.com/blog/do-modern-websites-look-identical; DesignRevision, "Lovable vs Bolt vs v0: We Tested All 4 AI Builders," Dec 6, 2025 — https://designrevision.com/blog/forge-vs-bolt-vs-lovable-vs-v0-comparison; Nielsen Norman Group, "Icon Usability" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/icon-usability/; explainx.ai, "imagegen-frontend-mobile" skill listing, Jun 2026 — https://explainx.ai/skills/Leonxlnx/taste-skill/imagegen-frontend-mobile (same ecosystem as host project; codification, not independent evidence)
- **Confidence:** Medium — convergence is well-documented discourse with comparative tests, but no formal research quantifies the brand harm; the accessibility and usability of the library itself is not in question.

### Default Stock Illustration Shipped Unmodified: unDraw's #6C63FF, Storyset, Humaaans

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** Flat vector people in the same contorted poses seen on thousands of indie sites; the unmistakable unDraw blue-purple accent; "empty state" and "features" illustrations that match the site's palette nowhere else.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** This is the single most machine-detectable generic signal in this section, because the default is literally a constant: unDraw's illustrations ship with default primary color `#6C63FF`, confirmed across the ecosystem — the react-undraw README documents `primaryColor` defaulting to `6c63ff` (npm, 2021), the undraw-mcp server documents `UNDRAW_DEFAULT_COLOR` defaulting to `6c63ff`, and community tooling exists specifically to recolor it ("to change the default color of the images replace 6c63ff with whatever color you want" — GitHub gists by WesleyBatista, 2021, and rydmike, 2021; the iamB0ody/grit repo documents recoloring all unDraw SVGs away from "unDraw's default accent color (typically 6c63ff)"). Shipping `#6C63FF` therefore signals one thing precisely: the customization step was skipped. The broader flat-figure style has a name and a documented backlash — "Corporate Memphis" / "Alegria," from Facebook's 2017 illustration system by Buck — criticized as "so bland and inoffensive that it's soulless... the animation equivalent of royalty-free ukulele music" (shots magazine, 2022), and, in Claire L. Evans's words (she curated the original Are.na collection), a style that "makes big tech companies look friendly, approachable, and concerned with human-level interaction and community — which is largely the opposite of what they really are" (quoted via Webflow, 2022; the full Webflow page and the Wired UK piece on Corporate Memphis could not be fetched in full at research time and are cited from listings and secondary quoting).
- **Detection:** In code: hex `#6C63FF` (case-insensitive) inside inline or linked SVG `fill` or `stop-color` attributes; filenames matching `undraw_*.svg`; `<svg>` blocks with unDraw's characteristic multi-hundred-node paths plus that fill; Storyset or Freepik attribution comments or URLs. In a design review: an illustration style that matches neither the brand palette nor any other asset on the site.
- **Valid exceptions:** Hackathons, throwaway prototypes, internal tools; deliberate ironic use (rare).
- **Stronger alternatives:** If using stock illustration, recolor to the brand palette at minimum — the libraries explicitly support this, which is what the recolor tooling exists for. Better: commission a small custom illustration set with documented style rules. Better still: replace decorative illustration with information-carrying diagrams or screenshots.
- **Supporting sources:** react-undraw README, npm, Feb 17, 2021 — https://npmjs.com/package/react-undraw; undraw-mcp configuration docs — https://lobehub.com/zh/mcp/techcow2-undraw-mcp-server; GitHub gists by WesleyBatista (2021) — https://gist.github.com/WesleyBatista/c61e4309856c7277c30b7a724acb60e6 and rydmike (Mar 27, 2021) — https://gist.github.com/rydmike/b9b7353b12765094796e07586e24a48b; iamB0ody/grit repo — https://github.com/iamB0ody/grit; shots magazine, "Corporate Memphis; the design style that quietly took over the internet," May 2, 2022 — https://magazine.shots.net/news/view/corporate-memphis-the-design-style-that-quietly-took-over-the-internet; Webflow, "Blue people and long limbs: How one illustration style took over the corporate world," Mar 14, 2022 — https://webflow.com/blog/corporate-memphis (quoted via search listing; full fetch failed); Wired UK, "Why does every advert look the same? Blame Corporate Memphis" (title verified via two independent secondary sources; direct URL not retrieved)
- **Confidence:** High for the detection signature and the "skipped customization" signal; Medium for the harm magnitude — the harm is genericness, not usability.

### Illustration and Imagery Style Inconsistency Within One Site

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** The hero is a glossy 3D render; the features section uses flat outline icons; the about section uses stock photos; the CTA uses an unDraw flat illustration; the blog uses AI photorealism. No shared palette, stroke, texture, perspective, or subject logic across any two of them.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** This is a brand-coherence failure, and it is endemic to AI-generated sites because each section is generated independently with no shared imagery spec. The trust mechanism is standard design-practice doctrine: "A cohesive branding and design approach signals professionalism... Inconsistent design suggests a lack of attention to detail or, worse, a hastily assembled site that might not be legitimate" (Invoke Media, 2026). The positive existence proof that imagery must be a system: Facebook commissioned Buck in 2017 to build Alegria, "a new style guide, illustration and animation system for the entire Facebook ecosystem," precisely because uncoordinated illustration had become a problem at scale (BUCK case study as cited by shots magazine, 2022, and miscellaneousdetailstm.wordpress.com, 2022). NN/g's icon guidance generalizes the principle: consistency of iconography and style across a product family is one of icons' legitimate benefits, and mixing systems forfeits it.
- **Detection:** In a design audit, screenshot every page and section; inventory imagery by type (3D render, flat vector, photo, icon set, emoji) and by palette; two or more unrelated illustration families on one site constitutes the failure. In code: mixed icon libraries in `package.json` (for example both `lucide-react` and `@fortawesome`); SVGs with unrelated `stroke-width` values (2 versus 1.5 versus filled); unDraw `#6C63FF` coexisting with a non-purple brand palette.
- **Valid exceptions:** Editorial sites where illustration is commissioned per-article under art direction — the variety is the system; deliberate collage or mixed-media brand identities.
- **Stronger alternatives:** One illustration system with written rules — grid, stroke, palette, perspective, subject matter — applied everywhere; style tiles settled before page generation; for AI pipelines, a single imagery spec injected into every section prompt.
- **Supporting sources:** Invoke Media, "Trust Signals Design for UK Businesses," Apr 24, 2026 — https://invokemedia.co.uk/trust-signals-design-elements-that-make-visitors-trust-your-uk-business; shots magazine, "Corporate Memphis; the design style that quietly took over the internet," May 2, 2022 — https://magazine.shots.net/news/view/corporate-memphis-the-design-style-that-quietly-took-over-the-internet; miscellaneousdetailstm.wordpress.com, Feb 2022 — https://miscellaneousdetailstm.wordpress.com/tag/illustration/; Nielsen Norman Group, "Icon Usability" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/icon-usability/
- **Confidence:** Medium — strong professional consensus and documented design-system practice, but no controlled research isolating style-inconsistency harm; honest tier: practitioner consensus.

### Oversized Decorative Hero Imagery with No Informational Role

- **Classification:** Contextual risk that escalates to Hard failure on performance grounds (when the decorative image is the LCP element and pushes real content down)
- **Visible symptoms:** Full-viewport hero images or videos behind a headline; the actual value proposition and CTA pushed below the fold; multi-megabyte hero assets; hero carousels of abstract images.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:** On the user side, NN/g found that "users dislike huge photos that get in the way of their tasks," and decorative "jazzed-up" images are ignored. On the performance side, the mechanics are precisely specified in web.dev's Core Web Vitals documentation: the LCP target is 2.5 seconds or less at the 75th percentile; "the LCP resource of a page (if it has one) will either be an image or a web font"; applying `loading="lazy"` to the hero image delays LCP and is called out as an anti-pattern; heroes referenced only from CSS backgrounds are undiscoverable by the preload scanner; the fix is early discovery plus `fetchpriority="high"`. Composing the two sources yields the failure: a decorative image that is also the largest above-the-fold element imposes the page's single biggest render cost while delivering zero information — the worst cost-benefit trade in the layout.
- **Detection:** In metrics: Lighthouse or PageSpeed showing the LCP element as a hero `<img>` or CSS `background-image`; a hero asset over roughly 200 KB in the network waterfall. In code: `<img loading="lazy">` above the fold; missing `fetchpriority="high"` on the LCP image; CSS-background heroes with text overlay — a double failure, combining late discovery with the absent alt semantics of "Missing or Wrong Alt Semantics" above.
- **Valid exceptions:** User-requested enlargements — NN/g notes big photos are good when requested; photography, portfolio, and venue sites where the imagery is the content; brand films on campaign microsites where the brief is explicitly atmospheric.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A smaller, information-carrying hero — product shot or real screenshot — served in AVIF or WebP with `srcset`, early discovery, and `fetchpriority="high"`; move decoration to lightweight CSS or SVG; never let decoration be the LCP element.
- **Supporting sources:** Nielsen Norman Group, "Photos as Web Content" — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/photos-as-web-content/ (~2010); web.dev, "Optimize Largest Contentful Paint," published Apr 30, 2020, updated Mar 31, 2025 — https://web.dev/articles/optimize-lcp
- **Confidence:** High — NN/g research plus web.dev/Core Web Vitals documentation; both are primary sources.

## 6. Brand Consistency Failures

This section covers failures of brand identity and brand consistency: the ways AI-generated sites arrive visually interchangeable with one another, and the ways a single site's own identity drifts apart across pages, components, and code. Two boundaries apply to everything below. First, the critique targets the identity layer — color voice, type voice, shape and motion language — not shared interaction conventions, which measurably improve learnability (Jakob's Law) and are defended in the closing note of this section. Second, the evidence asymmetry is stated up front: token drift and cross-page inconsistency are objectively testable hard failures, while the sameness patterns rest on marketing science, measured convergence data, and practitioner consensus — no controlled study links visual sameness to conversion loss, and none is claimed here.

### Logo-Swap Interchangeability (Template Convergence)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** A dark-or-white hero with a radial glow or gradient, a centered headline in a neutral sans, two buttons (one filled, one outlined), three feature cards with icons, a logo cloud, a bento grid, a pricing table. Replace the wordmark with a competitor's name and nothing breaks; the page could belong to any product in any category. The saschb2b anatomy essay states the fingerprint: "The dark hero with the radial glow. The centered headline in Inter. The two buttons, one filled, one outlined. The three feature cards with the little icons. The indigo. Always the indigo."
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:**
  - *Compositional mechanism:* unconstrained generation samples the statistical center of its training distribution, so the output is the median Tailwind-era landing page. Anthropic's frontend-aesthetics guidance concedes the mechanism in its own words (quoted verbatim by prg.sh): "You tend to converge toward generic, 'on distribution' outputs. In frontend design, this creates what users call the 'AI slop' aesthetic."
  - *Branding mechanism:* recognition is built by unique, consistently repeated assets (Romaniuk's distinctive-asset framework, Ehrenberg-Bass marketing science). A design identical to competitors' carries no uniqueness signal regardless of polish — a branding-function failure, not an aesthetic preference.
  - *Measured pre-AI baseline:* Goree, Doosti, Crandall & Su (CHI 2021) analyzed 227,000+ screenshots of roughly 10,000 sites spanning 2003–2019 (2M+ pairwise comparisons): sites grew *less* homogeneous from 2003–2007, then dramatically more so; average layout difference between websites declined over 40% between 2010 and 2019, shared front-end library usage strongly predicted layout similarity, and color-scheme standardization also increased. The authors warn that homogenization may "limit the perceived repertoire of possible and legitimate designs," while themselves noting it "is not necessarily a cause for alarm." AI is an accelerant of a measured trend, not its origin. Boris Müller's 2018 essay adds the design-theory frame: templates are content-agnostic, and content-agnostic form severs the form–content connection design is supposed to maintain.
  - *Feedback loop:* two independent 2025–2026 write-ups (prg.sh; Alan West on dev.to) document the self-reinforcing loop — Tailwind UI's `bg-indigo-500` demo default saturated tutorials and repos (Tailwind creator Adam Wathan's Aug 2025 X post acknowledging this reportedly passed 1M views; the post itself was not retrieved first-hand and should be treated as well-attested community lore), LLMs learned "modern web = indigo/purple," and AI-generated purple sites entered the next training corpus.
- **Detection:**
  - The swap test: replace the wordmark with a competitor's name; if nothing breaks visually, the page has no brand voice. (Reviewer procedure, no tooling.)
  - Code fingerprints: `bg-indigo-500` / `from-indigo-500 to-purple-600`; `font-family: Inter` as the sole family; the section sequence hero → centered H1 → dual CTA → 3-column icon-card grid. The composite layout fingerprint is documented at scale in Adrian Krebs's Playwright study of 1,590 Show HN landing pages (Apr 2026); the related hierarchy, spacing, and grouping failures are covered in §1's "Visual hierarchy and spacing" subsection and §4's spacing-and-grouping cluster.
  - Compare against category competitors' sites: identical structure and palette means no differentiation.
- **Valid exceptions:** Internal tools, throwaway MVPs, and pages whose only job is a familiar conversion flow, where external consistency (Jakob's Law) is the priority. NN/g's "Consistency and Standards" explicitly notes that conventions increase learnability; homogenization of *interaction patterns* is not the same failure as homogenization of *visual identity*.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Brief the generator with (a) a named aesthetic direction, (b) explicit avoid-lists, and (c) one distinctive-asset decision made up front — signature color, type pairing, shape motif — enforced through tokens. These mirror the three strategies in Anthropic's cookbook: guide specific design dimensions, reference inspirations, and call out defaults to avoid.

### Default Font Stack as Brand Voice (Inter-Everything; Logo-Less Wordmark)

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** The entire site is set in Inter (or Roboto/system-ui) at weights 400–600; the wordmark is just the company name typed in the same default UI font, possibly with a gradient applied; there is no typographic hierarchy beyond size and no typeface-pairing intent. The wordmark is indistinguishable from body UI text on every other AI-generated site.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:**
  - *Branding:* type is the highest-coverage brand surface on a web page, and a typeface chosen by default is a brand asset with zero uniqueness — the exact property distinctive-asset theory says drives recognition (Romaniuk 2018; pattern above). Inter-as-brand is statistically invisible; the quantified ubiquity evidence behind that judgment (the 414-billion-request Google Fonts figure, the Web Almanac font-coverage data) is documented in §3's default-font-monoculture block — see also that pattern, which carries the full shared evidence base for the monoculture itself.
  - *Compositional:* no pairing contrast (display vs. text), no weight-range expression, no tracking decisions — the flat "one family, three weights" treatment removes the cheapest dimension of differentiation. AYDesign (Jul 2026) notes the "universal Inter-only SaaS look" is now read as the *absence* of a premium brand signal. The sharpest form is the wordmark itself: a company name typed in the default UI font at body weight reads as interface chrome, not identity.
  - *Honest counter-evidence:* Inter is functionally excellent for dense UI — tabular numerals, disambiguation sets, small-size legibility; it was designed to disappear inside interfaces. The failure is using it as *identity*, not as *UI text*. The middle path — staying in Inter while activating a stylistic set so the wordmark reads as customized rather than defaulted (the Raycast precedent) — and Anthropic's avoid-Inter guidance for marketing-style outputs are both detailed in §3's font-monoculture block.
- **Detection:**
  - CSS: `font-family: 'Inter', sans-serif` or `system-ui` as the *only* family; a Google Fonts link for Inter/Roboto/Poppins; `Inter({ subsets: ['latin'] })` via next/font/google.
  - Wordmark rendered as plain text in the same stack and weight as nav items; absence of `font-feature-settings`, tracking, or pairing rules; wordmark weight equal to body weight.
- **Valid exceptions:** Developer tools, data-dense dashboards, and docs sites where "type should disappear" is the brief (designmd.app: Inter is right "when you need a type system that disappears"); pre-brand-investment products (fullstop360's "System A is an acceptable trade-off" — practitioner opinion, not research).
- **Stronger alternatives:** A distinctive display face paired with a neutral workhorse (fullstop360 "System B": e.g., Amplitude's Gellix/IBM Plex split); stylistic-set activation if staying in Inter (the Raycast precedent); a wordmark that is drawn or at minimum customized — tracking, weight, ligatures — rather than typed.

### Token Drift: Hardcoded Values Alongside Tokens (Three "Primary" Blues)

- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **Visible symptoms:** A `tokens.css` or Tailwind config exists, but components also contain raw hex — `#3b82f6`, `#2563eb`, and `#1d4ed8` all used as "the brand blue" in different files; invented off-palette colors appear in charts and one-off sections; radii, paddings, and font sizes repeat as magic numbers; dark mode or a rebrand requires find-and-replace archaeology. Visually: the same element is a slightly different blue on different pages.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:**
  - *Function/maintainability:* drift converts any global change (rebrand, dark mode, contrast fix) into an error-prone manual sweep; hardcoded values ignore the variable cascade and break runtime theming. This is objective breakage of the codebase's change-management function, not taste.
  - *Branding:* near-duplicate hues (`#0055FF` vs `#0044EE`) are perceptible inconsistency in the most important brand asset — the signature color. Distinctiveness depends on *consistent repetition*; drift is programmatic non-repetition.
  - *AI-specific amplifier (documented, not speculative):* Design Systems Collective / The Maker's Lab (Apr 2026) tested Claude Design against a real token file. The model used `#F79009` directly even though that exact value existed as `--warning-light`, and it *invented* off-system greens (`#075A46`, `#A6DCC7`) for a chart — roughly 20 detached hex occurrences and 15 off-scale values in a single generation. The diagnosed root cause: the model reads token *syntax* but not the *semantic constraints* governing when a token is correct. This is the mechanism by which AI generation manufactures drift even when a token system exists.
  - *Scale (vendor statistic, treat with caution):* Replay.build (Feb 2026) claims 74% of React components across 5,000+ enterprise repos contain three or more hardcoded color/spacing values bypassing the design system. This is a vendor blog selling a drift tool — direction plausible, number unverified.
- **Detection:**
  - `grep -rE "#[0-9a-fA-F]{3,8}" src/ | wc -l` versus the count of `var(--` usages — the raw-hex-to-variable ratio.
  - Cluster unique colors and collapse near-duplicates (RGB distance / ΔE) — the classic "47 shades of gray" audit.
  - stylelint `color-no-hex` or token-audit scripts in CI; hvpandya.com (Mar 2026) publishes a full LLM-readable audit prompt: scan every CSS file, list every hardcoded hex/px/radius/duration, count totals, fail CI on errors.
  - In AI-generated code specifically: verify that generated components reference the project's token layer rather than re-declaring values.
- **Valid exceptions:** Essentially none for colors and brand values. Data-visualization categorical palettes are a defensible documented exception when no semantic token fits — but they should live in one declared constant, not inline per component.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A three-layer token architecture (primitives → semantic aliases → components referencing only aliases); a CI token audit that exits non-zero on violations; AI instruction files (CLAUDE.md, .cursorrules) mandating token-only styling — the remediation pipeline in hvpandya.com — and clearly.design's approach of logging drift for human reconciliation rather than auto-normalizing.
- **Supporting sources:**
  - *Tested Claude Design, It failed to read Tokens. Here's Why!* — Design Systems Collective (The Maker's Lab) — https://www.designsystemscollective.com/tested-claude-design-it-failed-to-use-tokens-heres-why-3ec611b59ce5 — Apr 24, 2026
  - Pandya, H. — *Expose your design system to LLMs* — https://hvpandya.com/llm-design-systems — Mar 1, 2026
  - *The Essential Guide to Managing Design Token Drift in 2026* — https://www.replay.build/blog/the-essential-guide-to-managing-design-token-drift-in-2026 — Feb 25, 2026 (vendor source; statistics unverified)
  - *Turning a one-project pattern into a reusable Claude Code skill* — https://clearly.design/articles/ai-ready-ds-7-claude-code-skill — Jun 5, 2026
- **Confidence:** High — directly observable in code, objectively breaks change-management and theming, and the AI-specific failure mode is documented with reproduction detail.

### Stated Brand Values vs. Visual Execution Mismatch

- **Classification:** Avoid by default (when a brief exists); note that the *absence* of a brief is the common root cause
- **Visible symptoms:** The copy says "playful," "approachable," "human" — the render is a cold indigo-on-white corporate template; an enterprise security product ships neon gradients and cartoon blobs; a "bold, editorial" brand book is paired with default Inter at three weights. The verbal brand layer and the visual brand layer describe two different companies.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:**
  - *Branding:* brand value is created by *consistent association* between a cue set and the brand (distinctive-asset theory). Contradictory signals across the verbal and visual layers split the memory structure the brand is trying to build — each exposure reinforces conflicting cues. This is an inference from marketing-science first principles, not a measured UX outcome.
  - *Process:* AI generation has no access to unstated intent. Without an explicit brief, the model fills every unspecified dimension with the training-data median (the interchangeability mechanism above), which is statistically guaranteed to mismatch any non-median brand position. The lobehub frontend-design skill codifies this as the "Missing Context" anti-pattern: "Minimalist design for maximalist brand. Playful design for enterprise security."
- **Detection:** Extract the brand brief's adjectives (brief, about page, voice guidelines); audit the shipped palette, type, shape, and motion against each adjective; run the swap test plus a "does this render contradict any stated value?" checklist. In code: the absence of any project-level design-intent file — no DESIGN.md, no tokens derived from brand values — is a leading indicator.
- **Valid exceptions:** None when a brief exists. Where no brief exists, mismatch is undefined — the correct critique is "no brand direction was set," not "mismatch."
- **Stronger alternatives:** Write the aesthetic direction *before* generation. Principle-based direction — mood, font category, color family, motion philosophy, explicit avoid-list — beats both total openness and pixel-level over-specification, per thomas-wiegold.com's practitioner testing of Anthropic's frontend-design guidance; then audit the output against the brief's adjectives as a review gate.
- **Supporting sources:**
  - *How to Use the Claude Code Frontend-Design Plugin to Stop Shipping AI Slop* — https://thomas-wiegold.com/blog/claude-code-frontend-design-plugin/ — Apr 27, 2026
  - *frontend-design-enhanced skill documentation* — https://lobehub.com/zh/skills/teguharia172-github-ai-trend-tracker-frontend-design-enhanced — Mar 11, 2026
  - Romaniuk, J. — *Building Distinctive Brand Assets* — Oxford UP, 2018 (as above)
  - *Building distinctive brand assets — framework for small businesses* — https://www.nineblaess.de/blog/distinctive-brand-assets/ — Jun 22, 2026
- **Confidence:** Medium — strong logical grounding in branding science and practitioner consensus; no controlled UX research quantifying the harm was found.

### Unmodified Library Default Theme Shipped as Brand ("Stock shadcn/Tailwind Skin")

- **Classification:** Avoid by default
- **Visible symptoms:** The site ships the exact shadcn/ui default theme — neutral/zinc grays, the stock radius token, the default chart palette — or the default Tailwind palette with `bg-indigo-500` accents; components are visually identical to the library documentation; every radius, shadow, and border matches the docs screenshots. Reviewers recognize the library before they recognize the product.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:**
  - *Branding:* defaults are shared by every non-customized user of the library, so they carry zero uniqueness — the same mechanism as the two patterns above. Mario Ottmann (Dec 2025) lists "unmodified shadcn/ui components straight from documentation" as a top tell that gets a product "immediately categorized as low-effort, regardless of its functional quality" — a practitioner claim, but its recognizability premise is verifiable because the default theme values are published and stable.
  - *Community-scale corroboration:* the largest quantitative study of slop discourse (vibecoded-design-tells, 2026 — 3.2M Reddit posts scanned across 47 subreddits; 46,971 on-topic; 3,033 comments from 125 canonical threads) ranked "default shadcn/Tailwind look" the #1 feature-level tell and the "AI purple" gradient #2. The authors' own caveat applies: the corpus proxies vocal online opinion, so trust the ordering, not the exact percentages.
  - *Detectability is itself proof of non-uniqueness:* the default shadcn theme block is a byte-stable artifact (`--primary: oklch(0.205 0 0)`, `--ring: oklch(0.708 0 0)`, per the official theming docs). An entire ecosystem of theme customizers exists *because* shipping defaults is a known anti-signal (themux: "Use this to make your shadcn components stand out").
  - *Necessary counter-evidence:* the failure is identity, not function. Computed shadcn defaults pass WCAG AA — muted-foreground light (~#64748b) on white = 4.76:1; dark muted (~#94a3b8) on #09090b = 7.76:1; white on primary (~#0f172a) = 17.85:1. "Shadcn defaults are inaccessible" is false and must not be flagged as such.
- **Detection:** Diff the site's `:root` variable block against the official default theme CSS (ui.shadcn.com/docs/theming) — a hash-compare works because the block is byte-stable; two or fewer changed tokens means unmodified. Grep for `oklch(0.205 0 0)`, `bg-indigo-500`, and the stock radius token (`--radius: 0.5rem` or `0.625rem`, depending on the published theme version); check `components.json` for a stock `baseColor`; look for unchanged `components/ui/*.tsx` cva variants and the `tailwindcss-animate` plugin; compare border/shadow/radius values to docs screenshots.
- **Valid exceptions:** Internal tools, prototypes, and developer-facing products where the shadcn aesthetic is itself the expected vernacular; using shadcn as a *base* with custom tokens is its intended use. Note that even Raycast and Vercel customize heavily within that vernacular — custom feature flags, zero-shadow hairline systems.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Customize the three highest-leverage tokens at minimum (primary hue, radius scale, ring/focus color); run a theme customizer pass (themux, shadcn/create — the library's own 2025–26 preset direction exists to break the monoculture); derive tokens from brand values rather than accepting library defaults.

  - shadcn/ui — *Theming* (official docs, default theme CSS block) — https://ui.shadcn.com/docs/theming
  - *themux — shadcn theme customizer* — https://github.com/syntax-syndicate/shadcn-theme-mixin — May 2025

### Cross-Page Component & Label Inconsistency (Per-Page Re-generation Drift)

- **Classification:** Hard failure
- **Visible symptoms:** The same component (nav, footer, button, card) renders with different paddings, radii, hover states, or labels on different pages — because each page was generated or edited in a separate AI session; identical functions are labeled differently across pages ("Search" on one page, "Find" on another); icons gain or lose meaning between routes; primary/secondary button order flips.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:**
  - *Accessibility (objective, normative):* WCAG 2.2 Success Criterion 3.2.4 *Consistent Identification* (Level AA): "Components that have the same functionality within a set of web pages are identified consistently." W3C failure F31 maps exactly onto per-page label drift, and the Understanding document's Example 8 covers visually identical buttons announced inconsistently to assistive technology because they carry different accessible names. Inconsistent identification raises cognitive load for screen-reader users and people with cognitive limitations — a documented user-harm mechanism, not taste.
  - *Usability:* NN/g heuristic #4 (Consistency and Standards): internal inconsistency forces users to re-learn patterns page by page, degrading learnability and confidence; NN/g's documented Sutter Health example shows button-position flips causing users to accidentally abort a multi-page flow.
  - *Process root cause:* AI codegen is session-scoped; without a shared token and component layer, each generation re-solves styling locally — the per-page version of the token-drift mechanism above.
- **Detection:** Render every route and diff the computed styles of shared components; grep for duplicate component definitions with divergent class strings (same component name, different Tailwind classes per page); audit accessible-name and visible-label consistency for repeated functions (axe or manual audit against SC 3.2.4); count one-off page-level overrides of shared components.
- **Valid exceptions:** WCAG itself allows *different* labels when components serve *different* functions (a printer icon that prints receipts vs. invoices); context-specific variants are fine when deliberate, documented, and consistently applied.
- **Stronger alternatives:** A single component library consumed by all pages (no per-page re-implementation); a shared token layer (the token-drift fix above); per-component spec files — states, tokens used, anatomy — that the AI must read before generating UI (the hvpandya pattern); cross-route visual regression testing.
- **Supporting sources:**
  - W3C — *Understanding WCAG 2.2 SC 3.2.4: Consistent Identification* — https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/consistent-identification.html
  - Nielsen Norman Group — *Consistency and Standards* (heuristic #4; internal/external consistency; Sutter Health button-flip example) — https://www.nngroup.com/articles/consistency-and-standards/
  - Pandya, H. — *Expose your design system to LLMs* — https://hvpandya.com/llm-design-systems — Mar 1, 2026
  - *Turning a one-project pattern into a reusable Claude Code skill* — https://clearly.design/articles/ai-ready-ds-7-claude-code-skill — Jun 5, 2026
- **Confidence:** High — anchored in a normative accessibility standard plus NN/g usability guidance; the AI-session root cause is documented practitioner observation.

### Missing Brand Primitives Beyond Color and Type (No Shape Language, No Motion Language)

- **Classification:** Contextual risk (harm scales with how much the brief demands distinctiveness)
- **Visible symptoms:** Every corner is the same 8px radius; every shadow is the same low-opacity blur; motion is either absent or generic (fade-up-on-scroll, 0.3s-ease-everything); icons are stock Lucide/Heroicons with no customization; the brand has a color and a font and nothing else. No element would survive as a recognizable fragment if shown out of context.
- **Why it feels generic or fails functionally:**
  - *Branding:* distinctive-asset theory is asset-type-agnostic — colors, shapes, type styles, taglines, sonic cues, and *motions* are all candidate assets (Romaniuk 2018). A brand that invests only in color and type leaves most recognition channels unclaimed, and an unclaimed channel belongs to no one, competitors included.
  - *Compositional:* uniform radius, shadow, and motion remove hierarchy and texture; the saschb2b anatomy essay and Anthropic's cookbook both identify timid, evenly distributed decisions across every dimension — not just color — as the statistical fingerprint of generated design.
- **Detection:** A token audit beyond color: count distinct `border-radius`, `box-shadow`, and `transition`/`animation` values; check for motion-design tokens or an orchestrated load choreography; check icon usage for customization (stroke width, corner style, custom glyphs); the fragment test — screenshot a 200×200 crop and test whether it is attributable to the brand.
- **Valid exceptions:** Quiet, domain-appropriate interfaces — internal dashboards, accessibility-critical flows, and products whose brand position is explicitly "invisible utility." The Raycast counter-example calibrates this pattern: its restraint (zero shadows, hairline borders, a single CTA color) is itself a *deliberate* shape language — restraint chosen and enforced, not defaulted.
- **Stronger alternatives:** Define at least one owned primitive per channel — a radius/elevation signature (Raycast's no-shadow hairline system), a motion signature (one orchestrated page-load choreography instead of scattered micro-interactions, per Anthropic's cookbook guidance), a customized icon treatment — and document them as tokens so they survive regeneration. A single owned shape motif can carry a brand: Vercel's blueprint grid became a recognizable signature worth copying (setproduct.com).

  - *Distinctive Brand Assets framework summary (Ehrenberg-Bass / Romaniuk)* — https://umbrex.com/resources/frameworks/marketing-frameworks/distinctive-brand-assets-framework-ehrenberg-bass/ — Feb 9, 2026

  - *Vercel aesthetic: a complete guide to Blueprint Grid design* — https://www.setproduct.com/blog/complete-guide-to-blueprint-grid-design — Apr 21, 2026
- **Confidence:** Medium — well-grounded in branding science and consistent practitioner critique, but harm is contextual and no usability or accessibility standard is implicated.

---

**The boundary this section does not cross.** Shared interaction conventions — navigation placement, checkout flows, form patterns — improve learnability: Jakob's Law holds that users spend most of their time on *other* websites and therefore expect yours to work like the sites they already know (Nielsen, NN/g, 2000; summary via uxtigers.com, 2023), and even the CHI 2021 homogenization paper frames the goal as design diversity *without* giving up the advantages that standardization provides users and creators. Plainness is likewise not a failure: GOV.UK won the Design Museum's Design of the Year award in 2013 with a content-only design built on explicit principles ("Accessible design is good design… If we have to sacrifice elegance — so be it"), and Craigslist has thrived essentially unchanged since ~2000 because its plainness signals no-nonsense utility — the differentiator between *designed* restraint and slop is intentionality, not ornament count. Finally, the honesty constraint stated at the top bears repeating: no retrieved source provides controlled evidence that visual sameness, by itself, costs conversions or trust; that claim circulates widely as community consensus and is labeled as such wherever it appears above.
